SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION
======================================================================

1.1 SCOPE AND PURPOSE
This document provides comprehensive specifications for building materials
used in Bangladesh construction industry, including:
- Material properties and grades
- Quality requirements and testing
- Conformance to national and international standards
- Storage and handling guidelines
- Application guidelines

1.2 STANDARDS FRAMEWORK
1.2.1 Bangladesh Standards (BDS)
Bangladesh Standards and Testing Institution (BSTI) develops and maintains
national standards for construction materials:
- BDS standards based on ISO, ASTM, BS, and IS standards
- Mandatory for government projects
- Increasingly adopted in private sector
- BSTI certification ensures quality

1.2.2 International Standards Referenced
- ISO (International Organization for Standardization)
- ASTM (American Society for Testing and Materials)
- BS (British Standards)
- IS (Indian Standards)
- ACI (American Concrete Institute)
- AASHTO (American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials)

1.3 QUALITY ASSURANCE
1.3.1 BSTI Certification
BSTI provides:
- Product certification
- Testing services
- Quality mark licensing
- Factory inspections
- Market surveillance

1.3.2 Testing Laboratories
Approved facilities:
- BSTI Central Laboratory, Dhaka
- BUET Materials Testing Laboratory
- PWD Materials Testing Laboratory
- RAJUK Testing Laboratory
- Private BSTI-accredited laboratories

SECTION 2: CEMENT
======================================================================

2.1 TYPES OF CEMENT
2.1.1 Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC)
Standard: BDS EN 197-1, Type I

Properties:
- Initial setting time: Minimum 30 minutes
- Final setting time: Maximum 10 hours
- Compressive strength (7 days): ≥ 33 MPa
- Compressive strength (28 days): ≥ 53 MPa
- Soundness (Le Chatelier): Maximum 10 mm
- Fineness (Blaine): 2800-3500 cm²/g

Grades in Bangladesh:
- 42.5 Grade: Most common (compressive strength 42.5 MPa at 28 days)
- 52.5 Grade: High early strength (for fast-track construction)
- 32.5 Grade: Lower grade (limited use)

Applications:
- General construction
- Concrete and mortar
- Plastering works
- Structural members

Major brands in Bangladesh:
- Holcim (Strength, Cemex brands)
- Heidelberg Cement (Ruby, Tiger, Scan brands)
- Shah Cement
- Crown Cement
- Fresh Cement
- Premier Cement
- King Cement (Plus 50+ brands)

2.1.2 Portland Pozzolana Cement (PPC)
Standard: BDS 846 or ASTM C595

Composition:
- Portland clinker: 65-80%
- Pozzolanic material (fly ash): 20-35%
- Gypsum: 3-5%

Advantages:
- Lower heat of hydration (mass concrete)
- Better workability
- Higher ultimate strength
- Better durability (chemical resistance)
- Lower permeability
- Economical (uses industrial waste)
- Environmental benefits (lower CO2)

Disadvantages:
- Slower early strength gain
- Requires longer curing
- Not suitable where early strength critical

Applications:
- Mass concrete (dams, foundations)
- Marine structures
- Sewage treatment plants
- Underground structures
- General construction

2.1.3 Portland Slag Cement (PSC)
Standard: ASTM C595 or BS 146

Composition:
- Portland clinker: 45-70%
- Ground granulated blast furnace slag: 30-55%
- Gypsum: 3-5%

Advantages:
- Excellent durability
- Very good chemical resistance
- Lower heat of hydration
- Better sulfate resistance
- Good for marine environments

Applications:
- Marine structures (coastal areas)
- Foundations in sulfate-bearing soils
- Hydraulic structures
- Sewage and industrial effluent structures
- Mass concrete

2.1.4 Sulfate Resisting Cement (SRC)
Standard: ASTM C150 Type V or BS 4027

Special composition:
- Low C3A content (< 5%)
- Resists sulfate attack
- Slightly slower strength gain

Applications:
- Foundations in sulfate-rich soils
- Coastal areas (seawater exposure)
- Sewage treatment plants
- Industrial areas with chemical exposure

Where needed in Bangladesh:
- Coastal districts (Cox's Bazar, Chittagong, Khulna)
- Some inland areas with sulfate soils
- Industrial zones

2.1.5 White Cement
Standard: Similar to OPC but with color specification

Characteristics:
- Pure white color (low iron content)
- Higher cost
- Similar strength to OPC
- Special burning and grinding

Applications:
- Decorative works
- Architectural finishes
- Tile adhesives and grouts
- Terrazzo flooring
- Precast architectural elements

2.1.6 Rapid Hardening Cement
High early strength:
- 3-day strength equivalent to 7-day OPC strength
- Finer grinding
- Higher cost

Applications:
- Emergency repairs
- Fast-track construction
- Precast concrete production
- Cold weather concreting

2.2 CEMENT TESTING
2.2.1 Standard Tests
Fineness Test:
- Blaine specific surface area
- Requirement: 2800-3500 cm²/g
- Finer cement = faster strength gain

Setting Time Test:
- Vicat apparatus
- Initial set: ≥ 30 minutes
- Final set: ≤ 10 hours
- Ensures workability time

Soundness Test:
- Le Chatelier method
- Expansion: ≤ 10 mm
- Checks volume stability

Compressive Strength Test:
- 50mm mortar cubes
- Standard sand (Islampur sand or equivalent)
- Water-cement ratio: 0.40
- Test at 3, 7, and 28 days
- Requirements vary by grade

Chemical Composition:
- Loss on ignition
- Insoluble residue
- Sulfuric anhydride (SO3)
- Magnesia (MgO)
- Chloride content

2.2.2 Frequency of Testing
For projects:
- Initial: Test certificate from manufacturer
- Routine: Every 50 tons or every lot
- Strength test: Minimum monthly
- Doubtful batches: Immediate testing

2.3 STORAGE AND HANDLING
2.3.1 Storage Requirements
- Dry, moisture-proof shed
- Raised platform (300mm above ground)
- Stack height: Maximum 10 bags (1.4m)
- Clear labeling with date
- First-in, first-out usage
- Covered from rain
- Sealed bags

2.3.2 Shelf Life
- Fresh cement: Use within 3 months
- After 3 months: Test before use
- After 6 months: Significant strength loss
- Lumpy cement: Reject (or test if needed)

2.3.3 Handling
- Avoid rough handling (bag damage)
- No walking on stacked bags
- Protected during transport
- Check bags for damage/moisture

SECTION 3: AGGREGATES
======================================================================

3.1 FINE AGGREGATE (SAND)
3.1.1 Sources in Bangladesh
Natural sand:
- River sand (Padma, Jamuna, Meghna, Brahmaputra)
- Pit sand (limited sources)
- Marine sand (not suitable for concrete due to salt)

Manufactured sand:
- Crushed stone sand
- Increasingly used due to river sand shortage
- Better quality control possible

3.1.2 Specifications
Standard: BDS ISO 13055, ASTM C33

Grading:
- Passes through 4.75mm sieve
- Retained on 0.075mm sieve
- Grading zones: I, II, III, IV (Zone II most common)
- Fineness modulus: 2.3-3.1

Quality requirements:
- Clean, free from silt, clay, organic matter
- Deleterious materials: < 3% by mass
- Clay lumps and friable particles: < 1%
- Organic impurities: Light color (colorimetric test)
- Soundness: < 10% loss (sodium sulfate test)
- Specific gravity: 2.60-2.75
- Water absorption: < 2%

Silt content test (field test):
- Fill jar 1/3 with sand
- Add water to 2/3, shake well
- Let settle 3 hours
- Silt layer on top: Should be < 8%
- If > 8%: Wash sand before use

3.1.3 Washing of Sand
When required:
- Silt content > 8%
- Organic matter present
- Clay content high

Methods:
- Water spraying during stockpiling
- Washing plant
- Settle, drain, and reuse water

3.2 COARSE AGGREGATE
3.2.1 Sources in Bangladesh
- Crushed stone from Sylhet region
- Imported stone chips from India (Meghalaya)
- River boulders (limited, mainly from northern rivers)
- Brick chips (traditionally used, but not preferred now)

3.2.2 Specifications
Standard: BDS ISO 13055, ASTM C33

Sizes:
- 40mm (1.5 inch): Mass concrete, heavy foundations
- 20mm (3/4 inch): Structural concrete (most common)
- 12.5mm (1/2 inch): Thin members, dense reinforcement
- 10mm: Specialty applications

Grading:
- Single-sized: Narrow range (e.g., 20mm)
- Graded: Mixture of sizes for better packing
- Continuous grading preferred

Quality requirements:
- Hard, strong, durable particles
- Angular to rounded (crushed angular preferred)
- Deleterious materials: < 2%
- Soft particles: < 5%
- Clay lumps: < 0.25%
- Water absorption: < 2%
- Specific gravity: 2.60-2.80
- Crushing value: < 30% (aggregate strength)
- Impact value: < 30% (resistance to impact)
- Abrasion value (Los Angeles): < 40%
- Flakiness index: < 35% (shape)
- Elongation index: < 35% (shape)

Alkali-aggregate reaction:
- Some aggregates reactive with cement alkalis
- Petrographic examination for critical work
- Use low-alkali cement if reactive aggregate

3.2.3 Brick Chips (Khoa)
Traditional practice in Bangladesh:
- Crushed burnt clay bricks
- Used extensively in past
- Lower strength than stone
- Higher water absorption

Current status:
- Being replaced by stone chips
- Not recommended for structural concrete
- May be used for low-grade concrete
- Economical for roads, lean concrete

Properties (typical):
- Crushing strength: 15-20 MPa (lower than stone)
- Water absorption: 10-15% (high)
- Specific gravity: 1.8-2.0 (light)
- Requires more cement for same strength

If used:
- Presoak before use (24 hours)
- Adjust mix design for absorption
- Not for strength concrete > M20
- Quality checks essential

3.3 LIGHTWEIGHT AGGREGATES
Types:
- Expanded clay
- Pumice (not available locally)
- Sintered fly ash
- Limited use in Bangladesh

Applications:
- Lightweight concrete
- Roof insulation layers
- Precast panels

3.4 AGGREGATE TESTING
3.4.1 Routine Tests
Sieve analysis (grading):
- Determine particle size distribution
- Verify conformance to specification
- Fineness modulus calculation
- Frequency: Every 200 tons or weekly

Specific gravity and absorption:
- Pycnometer method (fine aggregate)
- Wire basket method (coarse aggregate)
- Determines mix design parameters
- Frequency: Monthly or each source

Moisture content:
- Critical for batching
- Oven drying method
- Rapid methods available (calcium carbide)
- Frequency: Daily during construction

Silt content (fine aggregate):
- Sedimentation test
- Indicates cleanliness
- Frequency: Weekly

Deleterious materials:
- Visual and washing
- Weight percentage
- Frequency: Each batch/source

Organic impurities:
- Colorimetric test (for fine aggregate)
- Color comparison after NaOH solution
- Frequency: Each source

3.4.2 Strength and Durability Tests
(For approval of new sources or annual verification)

Crushing value (coarse aggregate):
- Measures aggregate strength
- Standard cylinder compression test
- < 30% for concrete, < 45% for road base

Impact value (coarse aggregate):
- Resistance to impact
- Dropping weight test
- < 30% for concrete

Abrasion value (coarse aggregate):
- Los Angeles abrasion test
- Rotational drum with steel balls
- < 40% for concrete, < 50% for base course

Soundness:
- Sodium or magnesium sulfate test
- Resistance to weathering
- < 10-12% loss

3.5 STORAGE AND HANDLING
Storage:
- Separate stockpiles for each size/type
- Hard, clean surface (concrete platform preferred)
- Drainage provided
- Prevent contamination (soil, debris)
- Separate by low partition walls

Handling:
- Avoid segregation
- Load from top, discharge from bottom
- Minimize handling
- Keep moisture constant (for batching)

SECTION 4: WATER
======================================================================

4.1 SPECIFICATIONS
Standard: BDS ISO 12439:2011, ASTM C1602

General principle:
Water fit for drinking is suitable for concrete.

4.2 QUALITY REQUIREMENTS
4.2.1 Physical Properties
- Clear, colorless
- Free from suspended solids
- Free from oil, grease, organic matter
- No objectionable odor

4.2.2 Chemical Limits
pH: 6.0-8.0
Total dissolved solids: < 2000 mg/L
Chlorides (as Cl): < 500 mg/L (1000 mg/L for non-reinforced concrete)
Sulfates (as SO4): < 400 mg/L
Alkalis (as Na2O equivalent): < 600 mg/L
Organic matter: < 200 mg/L
Sugar: Nil (delays setting)

4.2.3 Prohibited Waters
- Sea water: For reinforced concrete (high chlorides cause corrosion)
- Industrial effluents: Variable quality, chemical contamination
- Sewage: Organic matter, harmful chemicals
- Acidic water (pH < 6): Attacks concrete
- Water with sugar or acids

4.3 SOURCES IN BANGLADESH
Municipal supply:
- WASA (Dhaka, Chittagong) water
- Generally suitable
- Test if quality suspected

Tube well water:
- Usually suitable
- Test for high iron content (staining risk)
- Test for industrial contamination

River/pond water:
- Possible contamination
- Test before use
- Filtration may be needed

4.4 RECYCLED WASH WATER
From concrete plants:
- Can be reused with limits
- Solids content < 50,000 mg/L
- Test fresh concrete properties
- Chloride contribution considered

4.5 WATER TESTING
Comparison test (if doubtful):
- Make concrete cubes with test water
- Make concrete cubes with known good water
- Same mix, same curing
- Test strength at 7 and 28 days
- Acceptable if test water cubes ≥ 90% of control strength

Chemical analysis:
- By approved laboratory
- For large projects
- If source changes

SECTION 5: ADMIXTURES
======================================================================

5.1 TYPES OF ADMIXTURES
5.1.1 Water-Reducing Admixtures (Plasticizers)
Standard: ASTM C494 Type A

Function:
- Reduce water content (5-12%)
- Maintain workability
- Increase strength
- Improve durability

Mechanism:
- Disperse cement particles
- Release entrapped water

Dosage:
- 0.1-0.3% by weight of cement
- As per manufacturer recommendation

Applications:
- Normal concrete
- Improve workability
- Reduce water-cement ratio

Products in Bangladesh:
- Sika Plastiment
- BASF Pozzolith
- Fosroc Conplast
- Local brands

5.1.2 High-Range Water Reducers (Superplasticizers)
Standard: ASTM C494 Type F or G

Function:
- Reduce water content 12-30%
- Produce flowing concrete
- High-strength concrete
- Self-compacting concrete

Types:
- Sulfonated naphthalene formaldehyde (SNF)
- Sulfonated melamine formaldehyde (SMF)
- Polycarboxylate ether (PCE) - Latest generation

Dosage:
- 0.5-2.0% by weight of cement
- Higher for flowing concrete

Applications:
- High-strength concrete (M40+)
- High-rise buildings
- Dense reinforcement areas
- Pumped concrete
- Self-compacting concrete

Products:
- Sika ViscoCrete (PCE)
- BASF Glenium (PCE)
- Fosroc Conplast SP
- MYK Schomburg

Caution:
- High slump loss over time (especially SNF, SMF)
- Retempering not recommended
- Use fresh concrete quickly
- PCE type has better slump retention

5.1.3 Retarding Admixtures
Standard: ASTM C494 Type B or D

Function:
- Delay setting time
- Useful in hot weather
- Prevent cold joints in large pours
- Long transportation time

Mechanism:
- Delay cement hydration
- Maintain workability longer

Dosage:
- 0.2-0.5% by weight of cement

Applications:
- Hot weather concreting (Bangladesh summer)
- Large continuous pours
- Pumped concrete over long distances
- Ready-mixed concrete with long travel time

Products:
- Sika Retarder
- BASF Delvo
- Fosroc Conplast R

5.1.4 Accelerating Admixtures
Standard: ASTM C494 Type C or E

Function:
- Accelerate early strength gain
- Reduce setting time
- Enable early formwork removal

Types:
- Chloride-based (calcium chloride): Not for reinforced concrete
- Non-chloride (calcium formate, etc.): Preferred

Dosage:
- 1-2% by weight of cement (chloride type)
- As per manufacturer (non-chloride)

Applications:
- Cold weather (not common in Bangladesh)
- Emergency repairs
- Fast-track construction
- Precast concrete production

Caution:
- Chloride causes corrosion: Avoid in reinforced concrete
- May increase shrinkage
- May reduce long-term strength

5.1.5 Air-Entraining Admixtures
Standard: ASTM C260

Function:
- Introduce tiny air bubbles (4-7%)
- Improve freeze-thaw resistance
- Improve workability

Note for Bangladesh:
- Freeze-thaw not an issue (tropical climate)
- Rarely used
- May reduce strength slightly

5.1.6 Waterproofing Admixtures
Function:
- Reduce permeability
- Improve water resistance
- Crystalline or pore-blocking types

Types:
- Integral waterproofing compounds
- Hydrophobic agents

Dosage:
- As per manufacturer (typically 1-3% of cement)

Applications:
- Water tanks
- Basements
- Bathrooms
- Swimming pools
- Retaining walls

Products:
- Sika 1
- BASF Masterpel
- Fosroc Proofex

Effectiveness:
- Helps but not substitute for proper waterproofing membrane
- Best as supplementary measure
- Good curing essential

5.1.7 Fly Ash (Pozzolanic Admixture)
Standard: ASTM C618 Class F or Class C

Source:
- Thermal power plant waste
- Imported (limited local production in Bangladesh)

Function:
- Partial cement replacement (15-35%)
- Pozzolanic reaction (long-term strength)
- Reduce heat of hydration
- Improve workability
- Lower cost
- Environmental benefit

Applications:
- Mass concrete
- High-performance concrete
- General construction

Considerations:
- Slower early strength gain
- Longer curing required
- Quality varies by source
- Requires trial mixes

5.1.8 Silica Fume (Microsilica)
Standard: ASTM C1240

Properties:
- Very fine (100 times finer than cement)
- Highly pozzolanic
- Expensive (imported)

Function:
- Produce very high strength concrete (M60+)
- Extremely low permeability
- High durability

Dosage:
- 5-15% by weight of cement
- With superplasticizer (essential)

Applications:
- High-rise buildings (high-strength concrete)
- Marine structures (high durability)
- Bridge decks
- Repair mortars

Products:
- Elkem Microsilica
- BASF Rheomac SF
- Limited availability in Bangladesh

5.2 ADMIXTURE TESTING
Compatibility:
- Test with project cement
- Check slump retention
- Check setting time
- Check strength development

Quality control:
- Use reputable brands
- Store as per manufacturer instructions
- Check expiry date
- Uniform dispersion in concrete

Dosage control:
- Accurate measuring
- Usually by volume (liquid admixtures)
- Dispense into mixing water

SECTION 6: CONCRETE
======================================================================

6.1 READY-MIXED CONCRETE (RMC)
6.1.1 Advantages
- Consistent quality (plant-controlled batching)
- Faster construction
- Reduced site space requirement
- Less labor
- Better quality control
- Eliminates on-site batching errors

6.1.2 RMC Plants in Bangladesh
Major operators in Dhaka:
- Heidelberg Cement (Ruby RMC)
- Holcim (Cemex RMC)
- Shah RMC
- Akij Cement
- Several others

Availability:
- Dhaka: Multiple plants
- Chittagong: Limited
- Other cities: Developing

6.1.3 Ordering RMC
Specify:
- Grade (M20, M25, M30, etc.)
- Workability (slump)
- Maximum aggregate size
- Special requirements (exposure conditions, durability)
- Delivery schedule
- Quantity

Delivery:
- Transit mixers (6-10 m³ capacity)
- Mixing in transit
- Discharge within 90 minutes of mixing

Site requirements:
- Access for trucks (turning, clearance)
- Pumping arrangement or direct discharge
- Labor for placing
- Vibrators
- Curing arrangements

Testing:
- Slump test each load
- Compression test cubes (1 set per 50 m³ minimum)
- Visual inspection of concrete

6.2 SITE-MIXED CONCRETE
Still common in Bangladesh for small projects:

Batching:
- Weight batching preferred (accurate)
- Volume batching acceptable (mark gauge boxes)
- Consistent moisture in aggregates

Mixing:
- Machine mixing mandatory for quality
- Mixer types: Tilting drum, non-tilting drum
- Mixing time: Minimum 2 minutes after all materials in
- No hand mixing except very small quantities (poor quality)

Quality control:
- Daily checks of materials
- Calibration of batching equipment
- Slump tests
- Cube tests

6.3 PRECAST CONCRETE
6.3.1 Applications in Bangladesh
- Piles (precast, prestressed)
- Boundary wall panels
- Utility covers and chambers
- Pavers and kerbs
- Septic tank rings
- Lintel beams
- RCC poles

6.3.2 Advantages
- Factory-controlled quality
- Faster construction
- Better finish
- Economical for repetitive elements

6.3.3 Manufacturers
- Precast concrete piles: Several specialist firms
- RCC poles: DESA, PDB suppliers
- General precast: Local fabricators

6.4 SPECIAL CONCRETES
6.4.1 High-Strength Concrete (M40, M50, M60)
Requirements:
- Quality materials
- Low water-cement ratio (0.30-0.40)
- Superplasticizers
- Silica fume or fly ash
- Good curing
- Quality control

Applications:
- High-rise buildings (columns)
- Long-span structures
- Prestressed concrete

6.4.2 Self-Compacting Concrete (SCC)
Properties:
- Flows into formwork without vibration
- Passes through reinforcement
- No segregation

Requirements:
- High powder content
- Superplasticizer
- Viscosity modifier
- Careful mix design
- Trial mixes essential

Applications:
- Dense reinforcement
- Complex shapes
- Inaccessible areas

Status in Bangladesh:
- Limited use
- Requires expertise
- Availability of materials improving

6.4.3 Fiber-Reinforced Concrete
Fiber types:
- Steel fibers: Strength, crack control
- Polypropylene fibers: Plastic shrinkage control, fire resistance
- Glass fibers: Thin panels (GFRC)

Applications:
- Industrial floors
- Shotcrete
- Precast panels
- Repair works

Availability in Bangladesh:
- Imported fibers
- Growing use
- Specialist application

6.4.4 Lightweight Concrete
Using lightweight aggregates or cellular concrete:
- Reduced dead weight
- Better insulation
- Lower strength

Limited use in Bangladesh.

6.4.5 Waterproof Concrete
Achieved by:
- Low water-cement ratio
- Integral waterproofing admixtures
- Proper curing
- Dense concrete
- Crack control

Applications:
- Basements
- Water tanks
- Swimming pools

Note: External waterproofing membrane still recommended for critical areas.

SECTION 7: MASONRY MATERIALS
======================================================================

7.1 BRICKS
7.1.1 Types and Grades
Clay bricks are primary masonry unit in Bangladesh.

First Class Bricks:
Standard: BDS 208
- Well burnt, uniform color
- Hard, no cracks
- Sharp edges and corners
- Metallic ringing sound when struck
- Compressive strength: Minimum 10 MPa (1000 psi)
- Water absorption: 12-15% by weight
- Dimensions: 240 × 115 × 75 mm (±5mm tolerance)
- Weight: 2.5-3.0 kg

Second Class Bricks:
- Slightly irregular
- Minor cracks acceptable
- Slightly under-burnt
- Compressive strength: Minimum 7 MPa
- Use: Backing, concealed work

Third Class Bricks:
- Irregular shape
- Under-burnt or over-burnt
- Low strength: 3.5 MPa
- Use: Temporary works, soling (not for structural walls)

Special Bricks:
- Perforated bricks: Hollow cavities, lighter, better insulation (BDS 1788)
- Fire bricks: High-temperature resistance (kilns, chimneys)
- Acid-resistant bricks: Chemical industry
- Paving bricks: Hard, low absorption

7.1.2 Brick Manufacturing in Bangladesh
Production:
- Extensive brick industry (5,000+ kilns)
- Seasonal production (dry season)
- Traditional and modern kilns
- Quality varies significantly

Kiln types:
- Fixed Chimney Kiln (FCK): Traditional, inefficient
- Zigzag Kiln: Improved efficiency
- Hybrid Hoffman Kiln (HHK): Modern, efficient, continuous
- Tunnel Kiln: Highest quality, limited in Bangladesh

Raw material:
- Topsoil clay (environmental concern)
- Alternative: Dredged river silt, pond silt

Issues:
- Use of agricultural topsoil (soil degradation)
- Air pollution from brick kilns
- Variable quality
- Seasonal availability

Government initiatives:
- Promote efficient kilns
- Phase out traditional kilns
- Alternative materials research

7.1.3 Brick Testing
Compressive strength test:
- Standard: ASTM C67 or BDS 208
- Sample: 5 bricks minimum
- Preparation: Grind flat surfaces
- Load perpendicular to bed face
- Report average strength
- Frequency: Every 10,000 bricks or per lot

Water absorption test:
- Dry weight (after oven drying)
- Immerse in water 24 hours
- Wet weight
- Absorption % = (Wet - Dry)/Dry × 100
- Acceptable: 12-15%
- High absorption = porous, weaker

Efflorescence test:
- Brick placed in water dish
- Water evaporates, salts deposit
- Visual rating: Nil, slight, moderate, heavy, serious
- Serious efflorescence: Reject

Soundness test:
- Strike two bricks together
- Should produce clear ringing sound
- Dull thud indicates poor quality

Dimensional tolerance:
- Measure length, width, height
- Should be within ±5mm of standard
- Check squareness

7.1.4 Selection and Handling
Selection:
- Visual inspection: Color, shape, cracks
- Sound test: Ringing sound
- Check dimensions
- Sample testing for important work

Storage:
- Stack neatly (maximum 2m height)
- Protect from rain (may cause efflorescence)
- Dry storage for at least 2 weeks after burning

Handling:
- Avoid throwing (causes cracks/breakage)
- Transport carefully
- Separate grades

7.2 CONCRETE BLOCKS
7.2.1 Types
Hollow Concrete Blocks:
Standard: ASTM C90 or BDS equivalent
- Most common: 400 × 200 × 200 mm (nominal)
- Actual: 390 × 190 × 190 mm (allows 10mm mortar joint)
- Hollow cores: 50-60% of volume
- Compressive strength: 4-7 MPa (on gross area)
- Lightweight, good insulation

Solid Concrete Blocks:
- 100% solid
- Compressive strength: 7-10 MPa
- Use: Load-bearing applications

Aerated Concrete Blocks (AAC):
- Autoclave cured
- Very light (density 400-600 kg/m³)
- Good insulation
- Imported or limited local production
- Higher cost but growing use

7.2.2 Advantages over Bricks
- Faster construction (larger size)
- Lighter (reduces dead load)
- Better insulation (hollow cores)
- Uniform dimensions (machine-made)
- Reduced mortar consumption
- No firing (environmental benefit)

7.2.3 Production in Bangladesh
Status:
- Growing industry
- Manual and semi-automatic plants
- Mainly Dhaka, Chittagong areas

Manufacturers:
- Various local producers
- Quality improving
- Increasing market acceptance

7.2.4 Applications
- Partition walls (non-load bearing)
- External walls (with proper finishing)
- Load-bearing walls (solid blocks, engineered design)
- Boundary walls
- Industrial buildings

7.3 STONE
7.3.1 Types Available in Bangladesh
Hard stone:
- Sylhet stone (sandstone): Local, main source
- Indian granite: Imported (Meghalaya)
- Limited igneous rock sources in Chittagong Hill Tracts

Properties required:
- Compressive strength: > 100 MPa
- Specific gravity: > 2.6
- Water absorption: < 3%
- Tough, durable, non-porous

7.3.2 Forms
Crushed stone:
- 40mm, 20mm, 12.5mm sizes
- Concrete aggregate (most common use)

Dimension stone:
- Cut to size
- Architectural features, cladding
- Flooring (granite, marble)
- Monuments

Rubble:
- Irregular stones
- Masonry (retaining walls, foundations)

7.3.3 Testing
Crushing strength test:
- Sample cubes 40-50mm
- Compress to failure
- Should exceed 100 MPa for building stone

Water absorption:
- Oven dry weight and saturated weight
- Should be < 3%

Impact test:
- Resistance to sudden loads
- Impact value

Abrasion test:
- Resistance to wear
- For flooring stone

7.4 LIME
7.4.1 Types
Fat lime (calcium lime):
- From pure limestone
- High plasticity
- White color

Hydraulic lime:
- Contains clayey impurities
- Sets under water
- Limited availability in Bangladesh

7.4.2 Uses (Limited in Modern Construction)
Traditional uses:
- Lime mortar (old masonry)
- Lime wash (traditional finish)
- Soil stabilization

Modern use:
- Heritage building restoration
- Limited use in plaster (for workability)
- Mostly replaced by cement

7.5 MORTAR
7.5.1 Cement-Sand Mortar (Standard)
Proportions (by volume):
- 1:3 (Rich): Exposed work, high stress
- 1:4 (Standard): General construction
- 1:5: Internal partitions
- 1:6 (Lean): Backing, temporary work

Preparation:
- Dry mix cement and sand thoroughly
- Add water gradually
- Mix to uniform consistency
- Use within 30 minutes

Properties:
- Workability: Should spread easily
- Retention: Should not dry too fast
- Adhesion: Bond to masonry units
- Compressive strength: Varies with proportion

7.5.2 Lime-Cement-Sand Mortar
Rarely used now in Bangladesh.
Lime addition improves workability.

7.5.3 Ready-Mixed Mortar
Available in Bangladesh:
- Dry mix in bags
- Add water on site
- Consistent quality
- Expensive but convenient
- Useful for small works, repairs

Products:
- Tile adhesive mortars
- Repair mortars
- Specialized applications

SECTION 8: STEEL
======================================================================

8.1 REINFORCING STEEL
8.1.1 Grades and Properties
Grade Fe 415 (60 ksi):
Standard: ASTM A615, BDS ISO 6935-2
- Yield strength: 415 MPa minimum
- Ultimate tensile strength: 485 MPa minimum
- Elongation: 14.5% minimum (in 200mm gauge)
- Most common grade in Bangladesh

Grade Fe 500 (75 ksi):
- Yield strength: 500 MPa minimum
- Ultimate tensile strength: 545 MPa minimum
- Elongation: 12% minimum
- High-strength applications
- Increasing use

Grade Fe 550:
- Yield strength: 550 MPa minimum
- Less common
- Special applications

8.1.2 Bar Types
Deformed bars (HYSD - High Yield Strength Deformed):
- Ribbed surface for bond
- Required for reinforced concrete
- Cold twisted or hot rolled

TMT bars (Thermo-Mechanically Treated):
- Modern production method
- Better ductility
- Better weldability
- Corrosion resistance
- Earthquake resistant
- Most common now

Plain bars:
- Smooth surface
- Limited use (stirrups in some cases)
- Poor bond
- Mostly replaced by deformed

8.1.4 Major Manufacturers/Brands
Local production:
- BSRM (Bangladesh Steel Re-Rolling Mills)
- AKS (Abul Khair Steel)
- GPH Ispat
- Anwar Ispat
- Ratna Steel
- Ruby Steel (Heidelberg)
- Shah Steel
- Many others (100+ mills)

Quality issues:
- Wide variation in quality
- Underweight bars common problem
- Lower grade sold as higher grade
- Inadequate quality control

8.1.5 Testing
Tensile test:
Standard: ASTM A370, BDS ISO 6892-1
- Determine yield strength
- Determine ultimate tensile strength
- Measure elongation (ductility)
- Frequency: 1 test per 40 tons or per grade/size/lot

Bend test:
- Check ductility
- Bar bent 180° around mandrel
- Should not crack or break
- Mandrel diameter: 4× bar diameter (Fe 415)
- Frequency: With tensile test

Weight test (Field test):
- Weigh 5m length
- Check against standard weight
- Variation: ±3% acceptable per bar, ±1.5% for sample average
- Many bars underweight in Bangladesh market

Visual inspection:
- Check deformations (ribs)
- Surface condition (no rust, cracks)
- Straightness

8.1.6 Selection and Handling
Selection:
- Buy from reputable brands
- Check mill test certificate
- Visual inspection
- Random testing for large projects

Storage:
- Above ground (timber platforms)
- Protected from moisture (rust)
- Separate by size and grade
- Cover during rain

Handling:
- Avoid dropping (may damage)
- No welding of lifting hooks (weakens bars)
- Cut and bend as per schedule

8.1.7 Corrosion Protection
Corrosion issues in Bangladesh:
- High humidity (coastal areas)
- Salt-laden air (coastal)
- Chloride in water (some areas)
- Poor quality concrete (porous)

Protection measures:
- Adequate concrete cover (40-50mm exposure)
- Dense, low-permeability concrete
- Proper curing
- Waterproofing where needed
- Epoxy-coated bars (expensive, for marine structures)
- Galvanized bars (rarely used)
- Stainless steel bars (very expensive, special cases)

8.2 STRUCTURAL STEEL
8.2.1 Grades
Mild Steel (MS):
- Grade Fe 250: Yield 250 MPa
- Older structures
- Limited use now

High Tensile Steel:
- Grade Fe 410: Yield 410 MPa (most common)
- Grade Fe 540: Yield 540 MPa

Standard: ASTM A36 (Fe 250), ASTM A572 (Fe 410, Fe 540)

8.2.2 Sections
Available in Bangladesh:
- I-sections (ISMB, ISJB, ISLB, ISWB)
- Channel sections (ISMC, ISLC)
- Angle sections (ISA)
- Hollow sections (CHS, SHS, RHS)
- Plates and flats
- Rods and bars

Import or local:
- Large sections: Mostly imported
- Small/medium: Local rolling mills
- Hollow sections: Imported
- Plates: Mix of local and imported

8.2.3 Applications
- Industrial buildings (factories, warehouses)
- Roof trusses
- Mezzanine floors
- Staircases
- Doors and windows
- Transmission towers
- Bridges (major bridges)

Limited use in multi-storey buildings:
- Concrete structures more common in Bangladesh
- Steel more expensive
- Corrosion concerns
- Limited fabrication capacity
- But increasing for high-rise (steel-concrete composite)

8.2.4 Surface Protection
Essential in Bangladesh climate:

Shop primer:
- Applied at fabrication shop
- Red oxide or zinc-rich primer
- Protects during storage and transport

Field painting:
- Surface preparation (remove rust, mill scale)
- Primer: 2 coats minimum
- Intermediate and finish coats
- Total dry film thickness: 150-200 microns

Galvanizing:
- Hot-dip galvanizing
- Zinc coating: 85 microns minimum
- Best protection
- More expensive
- For long life and severe exposure

Maintenance:
- Regular inspection
- Touch up damaged paint
- Repainting as needed

8.3 METAL ROOFING AND CLADDING
8.3.1 Corrugated Iron (CI) Sheets
Galvanized Iron (GI):
- Most common roofing in Bangladesh
- Gauge: 24-28 (thicker = stronger)
- Width: 750-900mm
- Length: 1.8-3.6m standard
- Corrugated or trapezoidal profile
- Galvanized coating: 120-275 g/m²

Color-coated sheets:
- Pre-painted GI
- PPGI (Pre-Painted Galvanized Iron)
- Color on both sides
- UV-resistant paint
- Longer life, aesthetic
- Popular for quality construction

Aluminum sheets:
- Lighter than GI
- Better corrosion resistance
- More expensive
- Limited use

8.3.2 Purlin and Structure
Materials:
- Steel angles, channels, or RHS
- Timber (small buildings)
- Cold-formed steel sections

Spacing:
- 1.0-1.5m depending on sheet gauge and load
- Closer for thinner sheets

8.3.3 Fasteners
Types:
- J-bolts or hook bolts
- Self-drilling screws (modern)
- With rubber/neoprene washers

Installation:
- Fix on sheet crests (not valleys)
- Straight line alignment
- Proper tightening (not over-tight)

8.3.4 Manufacturers in Bangladesh
- AkijBlend (color-coated)
- BSRM Steels
- City Group
- Various importers

SECTION 9: WOOD (TIMBER)
======================================================================

9.1 SPECIES AVAILABLE IN BANGLADESH
9.1.1 Hardwoods (Traditional)
Local species (increasingly scarce):
- Garjan (Dipterocarpus): Strong, durable, termite-resistant
- Sal (Shorea robusta): Very strong, durable
- Teak: Excellent properties (rare, expensive)
- Mahogany: Good workability
- Sundari (Heritiera): From Sundarbans (protected)
- Champa, Mehgoni, others

Import sources:
- India: Sal, teak
- Myanmar: Teak
- Malaysia: Meranti, Keruing

Properties:
- High density (700-900 kg/m³)
- High strength
- Durable
- Expensive
- Limited availability

9.1.2 Softwoods
Imported (limited local):
- Pine (various species)
- Spruce
- Fir

Properties:
- Lower density (400-600 kg/m³)
- Lower strength
- Less durable (needs treatment)
- Economical
- Good workability

9.1.3 Engineered Wood Products
Plywood:
- Multi-layer wood veneers
- Various grades and thicknesses
- Water-resistant (BWR, BWP grades)
- Common use in Bangladesh

Particle board:
- Compressed wood particles
- Furniture, non-structural
- Lower cost
- Limited moisture resistance

MDF (Medium Density Fiberboard):
- Fine wood fibers compressed
- Smooth surface
- Furniture, decorative panels
- Not moisture resistant

Laminates:
- Decorative surfaces
- On plywood, MDF, or particle board
- Wide range of finishes

Local production:
- Some plywood mills in Bangladesh
- Particle board production limited
- MDF production growing
- Quality variable

Imported products:
- Malaysia, Indonesia, China, India
- Higher quality generally
- Various brands and grades

9.2 TIMBER GRADING AND SELECTION
9.2.1 Grading
Visual grading (common in Bangladesh):
- Grade I: Best quality, few defects
- Grade II: Medium quality
- Grade III: Lower quality

Defects to check:
- Knots (size, location)
- Splits and cracks
- Warp, twist, bow
- Decay or insect damage
- Grain orientation

9.2.2 Seasoning
Importance:
- Reduce moisture content
- Prevent warping, splitting
- Increase strength
- Better paint adhesion

Methods:
- Air seasoning: Stack with spacers, under cover
- Kiln seasoning: Faster, controlled (limited in Bangladesh)

Target moisture content:
- Interior use: 8-12%
- Exterior use: 12-15%
- Structural use: < 19%

Checking:
- Moisture meter
- Weight comparison

Problem in Bangladesh:
- Inadequate seasoning common
- Timber used too wet
- Causes shrinkage, warping after installation

9.2.3 Preservation Treatment
Necessity in Bangladesh:
- High humidity
- Termites widespread
- Fungal decay risk

Treatment methods:
- Brushing/spraying (surface only, limited effectiveness)
- Dipping (short-term immersion)
- Pressure treatment (best, but limited facilities)

Preservatives:
- Creosote: Traditional, very effective (but messy, odor)
- CCA (Copper-Chromium-Arsenic): Effective (environmental concerns)
- ACQ (Alkaline Copper Quaternary): Modern alternative
- Borax-boric acid: For interior use, non-toxic

Treatment status:
- Commercial treatment limited in Bangladesh
- Mostly DIY surface treatment
- Pre-treated timber available (limited, imported)

9.3 APPLICATIONS
9.3.1 Structural Uses
- Roof trusses (small buildings)
- Purlins and rafters
- Door and window frames
- Temporary structures (formwork, scaffolding)
- Floor joists (limited, mainly in old buildings)

Declining use:
- Concrete structures more common now
- Steel roof structures replacing wood
- But still used in low-cost, rural construction

9.3.2 Joinery and Finishing
- Doors (panel, flush)
- Windows (frame, shutter)
- Furniture (built-in and movable)
- Cabinets and paneling
- Flooring (parquet, planks) - limited in Bangladesh
- Moldings and trim

9.3.3 Temporary Works
- Formwork (concrete)
- Scaffolding (increasingly replaced by metal)
- Site huts

9.4 PROPERTIES AND DESIGN DATA
Strength varies by species:
- Modulus of elasticity: 8,000-15,000 MPa
- Bending stress: 8-20 MPa
- Compression parallel to grain: 10-30 MPa
- Shear parallel to grain: 1-3 MPa

Density:
- Hardwoods: 700-900 kg/m³
- Softwoods: 400-600 kg/m³

For design: Refer to BNBC 2020 Part 6, Chapter 11 (Timber Structures)

9.5 STORAGE AND HANDLING
Storage:
- Above ground (on timber bearers)
- Horizontal stacking
- Under cover (roof but ventilated)
- Spacers between layers (air circulation)
- Protect from rain, sun, soil contact
- Prevent termite access

Handling:
- Avoid dragging (damages surfaces)
- Careful lifting
- No nailing into faces (for storage)

11.1.2 Private Laboratories
Growing number:
- BSTI-accredited
- Concrete, steel, soil testing
- Quality control services
- Various locations

11.2 FREQUENCY OF TESTING
11.2.1 Recommended Testing Schedule
Cement:
- Mill test certificate (each batch)
- Verification testing: Every 50 tons or monthly
- Strength test: Monthly minimum

Aggregates:
- Initial approval testing (comprehensive)
- Routine testing: Weekly or every 200 tons
- Moisture content: Daily during batching

Concrete:
- Slump: Every load or every 20 m³
- Compressive strength: 1 set (3 cubes) per 50 m³ minimum
- Minimum 1 set per pour/per grade/per day

Reinforcing steel:
- Mill test certificate
- Verification: 1 test per 40 tons or per lot
- Weight check: Random, frequent

Masonry units (bricks/blocks):
- Initial: Comprehensive testing
- Routine: Every 10,000 units or per lot
- Visual inspection: Continuous

Soil:
- Site investigation: As per BNBC requirements
- Compaction: Every 500 m² or per layer

11.3 SAMPLE COLLECTION
Proper sampling critical:
- Random selection
- Representative of lot
- Proper identification
- Timely testing
- Follow standard procedures

11.4 TEST REPORTS
Should include:
- Material tested
- Sample source and identification
- Date of sampling and testing
- Test method/standard
- Results
- Acceptance criteria
- Conclusion (pass/fail)
- Laboratory accreditation
- Signature and seal

11.5 ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA
Compare results to:
- Code requirements (BNBC)
- Material standards (BDS, ASTM, etc.)
- Project specifications
- Manufacturer specifications

Action if failed:
- Retest if doubtful
- Reject material if confirmed
- Investigate cause
- Quality control review
- Corrective measures
