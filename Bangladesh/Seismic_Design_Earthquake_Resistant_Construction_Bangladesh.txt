SECTION 1: EARTHQUAKE HAZARD IN BANGLADESH
======================================================================

1.1 SEISMIC CONTEXT
1.1.1 Geological Setting
Bangladesh is located near one of the most seismically active regions in the
world - the convergence zone between the Indian Plate and the Eurasian Plate.

Key seismic features:
- Himalayan convergent boundary to the north
- Burmese Arc subduction zone to the east
- Dauki Fault system to the northeast
- Madhupur Tract (elevated Pleistocene terrace) in central Bangladesh
- Bengal Basin (deep sedimentary basin)
- Numerous active and potentially active faults

Historical context:
- Great Assam Earthquake 1897 (M8.1): Severe damage in Sylhet and Mymensingh
- Dhaka Earthquake 1885 (M7.0): Significant damage in Dhaka
- Srimangal Earthquake 1918 (M7.6): Major damage in northeastern Bangladesh
- Chittagong Earthquake 1762 and 1918: Coastal seismicity
- Recent earthquakes (M5-6): Frequent moderate earthquakes

1.1.2 Current Seismic Risk
Population vulnerability:
- High population density (over 1,200 per km²)
- Dhaka: World's most vulnerable megacity to earthquakes (Risk Index)
- Rapid urbanization and unplanned construction
- Many buildings do not comply with seismic codes
- Soft soil conditions amplify ground motion
- High water table affects foundation stability

Economic impact potential:
- Concentrated infrastructure in seismic zones
- Garment industry (major economic sector) vulnerable
- Essential facilities (hospitals, fire stations) at risk
- Historical and cultural heritage buildings vulnerable

1.2 SEISMIC ZONING OF BANGLADESH
1.2.1 Seismic Zone Map (BNBC 2020)
Bangladesh divided into four seismic zones based on peak ground acceleration:

ZONE 1 (HIGHEST SEISMIC HAZARD):
- Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA): 0.36g
- Seismic Zone Factor Z = 0.36
- Expected Modified Mercalli Intensity: VIII-IX
- Geographical Coverage:
  * Sylhet Division (Sylhet, Sunamganj)
  * Northern Mymensingh (Netrokona, Sherpur)
  * Parts of Kishoreganj
- Tectonic Features:
  * Proximity to Dauki Fault
  * Shillong Plateau influence
  * Historical major earthquake damage
  * Potentially liquefiable soils

ZONE 2 (HIGH SEISMIC HAZARD):
- Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA): 0.28g
- Seismic Zone Factor Z = 0.28
- Expected MMI: VII-VIII
- Geographical Coverage:
  * Chittagong Division (Chittagong, Cox's Bazar)
  * Chittagong Hill Tracts (Rangamati, Bandarban, Khagrachari)
  * Southeastern districts (Feni, Noakhali, Comilla, Brahmanbaria)
  * Mymensingh and Jamalpur
- Tectonic Features:
  * Burmese Arc subduction zone influence
  * Active fault systems
  * Folded belt geology
  * Site amplification potential

ZONE 3 (MODERATE SEISMIC HAZARD):
- Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA): 0.20g
- Seismic Zone Factor Z = 0.20
- Expected MMI: VI-VII
- Geographical Coverage:
  * Dhaka Division (Dhaka, Narayanganj, Gazipur, Munshiganj)
  * Central Bangladesh (Tangail, Manikganj)
  * Parts of Mymensingh Division
  * Habiganj, Maulvibazar
  * Chandpur, Lakshmipur
- Tectonic Features:
  * Madhupur Tract (may experience amplification)
  * Deep alluvial deposits in Dhaka
  * Potential for liquefaction
  * Distance from major faults (but not immune)

ZONE 4 (LOWER SEISMIC HAZARD):
- Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA): 0.12g
- Seismic Zone Factor Z = 0.12
- Expected MMI: V-VI
- Geographical Coverage:
  * Western Bangladesh (Rajshahi, Rangpur, Bogra, Pabna)
  * Northwestern districts (Dinajpur, Thakurgaon)
  * Southwestern Bangladesh (Khulna, Jessore, Kushtia, Satkhira)
  * Southern coastal districts (Barisal, Patuakhali, Pirojpur)
- Tectonic Features:
  * Greater distance from active plate boundaries
  * Stable alluvial plains
  * Lower but not negligible seismic risk

1.2.2 Urban Centers and Seismic Risk
Major cities by seismic zone:

Highest Risk (Zone 1):
- Sylhet (Zone 1, PGA 0.36g)
- Sunamganj (Zone 1, PGA 0.36g)

High Risk (Zone 2):
- Chittagong (Zone 2, PGA 0.28g)
- Cox's Bazar (Zone 2, PGA 0.28g)
- Comilla (Zone 2, PGA 0.28g)
- Mymensingh (Zone 2 transitioning to Zone 1)

Moderate Risk (Zone 3):
- Dhaka (Zone 3, PGA 0.20g) - Highest consequence due to population
- Narayanganj (Zone 3, PGA 0.20g)
- Gazipur (Zone 3, PGA 0.20g)

Lower Risk (Zone 4):
- Rajshahi (Zone 4, PGA 0.12g)
- Rangpur (Zone 4, PGA 0.12g)
- Khulna (Zone 4, PGA 0.12g)
- Barisal (Zone 4, PGA 0.12g)

Note: Even Zone 4 areas should implement seismic design provisions. Lower
risk does not mean no risk.

1.3 SITE-SPECIFIC SEISMIC CONSIDERATIONS
1.3.1 Soil Conditions and Site Amplification
Bangladesh soil characteristics significantly affect earthquake response:

Dhaka City:
- Deep alluvial deposits (300-400m of soft to medium soil)
- Holocene clay and silt layers (soft, high plasticity)
- Water table at 3-6m depth
- Site Class D or E (soft to very soft soil)
- Amplification factor: 1.5-2.5 times bedrock motion
- Increased earthquake duration and intensity
- High liquefaction potential in sandy layers

Chittagong:
- Variable geology (coastal alluvium, tertiary hills)
- Folded sedimentary rocks in hills
- Soft marine clay in coastal areas
- Site Class C to E (depending on location)
- Potential landslides in hill areas during earthquakes

Sylhet:
- Floodplain deposits
- Thick soft clay and silt
- Very high water table
- Site Class D to E
- Severe liquefaction risk

Other areas:
- Western Bangladesh: Relatively better soil (Site Class C-D)
- Coastal areas: Soft clay, liquefaction risk
- Madhupur Tract: Relatively firm Pleistocene clay (Site Class C)

1.3.2 Liquefaction Susceptibility
Liquefaction occurs when saturated loose sand loses strength during shaking.

High liquefaction risk areas:
- Riverbank areas throughout Bangladesh
- Recently reclaimed land (common in Dhaka)
- Filled low-lying areas
- Areas with shallow water table (<3m)
- Loose sandy deposits

Consequences of liquefaction:
- Loss of bearing capacity (foundation failure)
- Lateral spreading (ground displacement)
- Settlement and tilting of buildings
- Floating of buried structures (tanks, basements)
- Failure of retaining walls

Mitigation measures:
- Deep foundations through liquefiable layer
- Ground improvement (stone columns, densification)
- Structural design for lateral spreading effects
- Avoid construction on liquefiable soil if possible

SECTION 2: SEISMIC DESIGN PRINCIPLES
======================================================================

2.1 FUNDAMENTAL OBJECTIVES
2.1.1 Performance Objectives
Seismic design aims to achieve:

Life Safety (Primary Objective):
- Prevent collapse in major earthquake (Design Basis Earthquake)
- Protect occupants during and after shaking
- Maintain egress paths
- Allow time for evacuation

Property Protection (Secondary Objective):
- Minimize structural damage
- Limit non-structural damage
- Enable continued occupancy after moderate earthquakes
- Reduce economic losses

Functional Continuity (For Critical Facilities):
- Hospitals remain operational
- Emergency response facilities functional
- Critical infrastructure continues service
- Communications maintained

2.1.2 Design Earthquake Levels
Two earthquake levels considered:

Design Basis Earthquake (DBE):
- 10% probability of exceedance in 50 years (475-year return period)
- Basis for code design
- Structure should survive with repairable damage
- Life safety ensured

Maximum Considered Earthquake (MCE):
- 2% probability of exceedance in 50 years (2475-year return period)
- 1.5 times DBE intensity
- Structure should not collapse
- Severe damage acceptable but progressive collapse prevented

2.2 SEISMIC FORCE RESISTING SYSTEMS
2.2.1 Lateral Load Resisting Systems
Common systems in Bangladesh:

1. Moment-Resisting Frames (Most Common):
   - Beams and columns resist lateral loads through flexure
   - Joints transfer moments
   - Ductile behavior through plastic hinges
   - Response Modification Factor R:
     * Special moment frame: R = 8 (high ductility)
     * Intermediate moment frame: R = 5
     * Ordinary moment frame: R = 3 (avoid in high seismic zones)

2. Shear Wall Systems:
   - Reinforced concrete walls resist lateral loads
   - Walls act as vertical cantilevers
   - High stiffness reduces deformation
   - Response Modification Factor R:
     * Special reinforced shear walls: R = 6
     * Ordinary shear walls: R = 4

3. Dual Systems:
   - Combination of moment frames and shear walls
   - Frames provide redundancy and ductility
   - Walls provide stiffness
   - Response Modification Factor R = 7-8 (properly designed)

4. Braced Frames:
   - Diagonal members resist lateral loads
   - Limited use in Bangladesh
   - Steel construction
   - R = 6-8 (depending on brace type and ductility)

5. Bearing Wall Systems:
   - Masonry or concrete walls carry vertical and lateral loads
   - Limited ductility
   - Height limitations
   - R = 1.5-4 (depending on reinforcement)
   - Not recommended for Zone 1-2

2.2.2 Structural Configuration
Good seismic configuration principles:

Simplicity:
- Clear load paths
- Understandable behavior
- Easy to analyze and construct

Regularity:
- Symmetric plan
- Uniform distribution of mass and stiffness
- No abrupt changes in geometry
- Reduces torsional effects

Redundancy:
- Multiple load paths
- If one element fails, others carry load
- Minimum 3 bays in each direction preferred

Continuity:
- Well-connected structural elements
- Forces transfer smoothly
- No weak links in load path

2.2.3 Irregularities to Avoid
Plan irregularities:
- Excessive asymmetry (torsion)
- Re-entrant corners (stress concentration)
- Irregular shapes (L, T, U shapes worse than rectangular)
- Discontinuous diaphragms

Vertical irregularities:
- Soft storey (ground floor open for parking) - CRITICAL PROBLEM
- Weak storey (abrupt strength reduction)
- Mass irregularity (heavy floor on light structure)
- Geometric irregularity (setbacks, towers on podium)
- Discontinuous shear walls or columns

Consequences of irregularities:
- Concentration of damage
- Unpredictable behavior
- Premature failure
- Collapse risk

Solutions:
- Avoid irregularities in design stage
- If unavoidable, use detailed analysis
- Strengthen critical areas
- Provide enhanced detailing

SECTION 3: SEISMIC LOAD CALCULATION
======================================================================

3.1 EQUIVALENT STATIC METHOD
3.1.1 Applicability
Permitted for:
- Regular structures up to 15 storeys
- Regular structures with period T < 3.5 × Ts
- Structures in Seismic Design Category A, B, or C
- Most buildings in Bangladesh qualify

Not permitted for:
- Highly irregular structures
- Very tall/flexible structures
- Critical/essential facilities (use dynamic analysis)

3.1.2 Seismic Base Shear Formula
V = Cs × W

Where:
V = Design base shear (total horizontal force)
Cs = Seismic response coefficient
W = Effective seismic weight

Seismic Weight (W):
W = Total dead load + Applicable portion of live load

Live load factors:
- Residential: 25% of LL
- Commercial/Office: 25% of LL
- Storage: 25% of LL (or 100% if always loaded)
- Snow: 0% (not applicable in Bangladesh)
- Partition load: 100%

3.1.3 Seismic Response Coefficient (Cs)
Cs = (Z × I × Sa) / (R × g)

Components:

Z = Seismic Zone Factor
- Zone 1: Z = 0.36
- Zone 2: Z = 0.28
- Zone 3: Z = 0.20
- Zone 4: Z = 0.12

I = Importance Factor
- Importance Category I (Low): I = 1.0
  (Agricultural buildings, temporary structures)
- Importance Category II (Normal): I = 1.0
  (Residential, commercial, offices)
- Importance Category III (Essential): I = 1.5
  (Schools, hospitals, fire stations, emergency shelters)
- Importance Category IV (Critical): I = 1.75
  (Emergency operation centers, critical facilities)

Sa = Spectral Acceleration (depends on soil type and period)
- Obtained from design response spectrum
- Function of natural period T and soil site class

R = Response Modification Factor (ductility factor)
- Accounts for inelastic energy dissipation
- Higher R means higher ductility requirement
- Values given in Section 2.2.1

g = Acceleration due to gravity = 9.81 m/s²

Limits on Cs:
Cs(max) = 2.5 × I / R (for very short period structures)
Cs(min) = 0.01 (absolute minimum, always apply)

3.1.4 Natural Period Estimation
Approximate period T (in seconds):

For concrete moment frames:
T = 0.075 × h^(0.75)

For steel moment frames:
T = 0.085 × h^(0.75)

For other structures:
T = 0.05 × h^(0.75)

Where:
h = Height of building in meters from base to roof

Alternatively (more accurate):
T = Ct × h^x

Where:
Ct = Building type coefficient (0.016 for concrete moment frames)
x = 0.9
h = Height in meters

Example calculation:
For 10-storey concrete frame building, h = 30m:
T = 0.075 × 30^0.75 = 0.075 × 13.4 = 1.0 second

3.1.5 Design Response Spectrum
Spectral acceleration Sa varies with period:

Short period range (T < 0.4 seconds):
Sa increases linearly from 0.4 × Sa,max to Sa,max

Constant acceleration range (0.4s ≤ T ≤ Ts):
Sa = Sa,max = 2.5

Descending branch (T > Ts):
Sa = 2.5 × (Ts / T)

Where Ts = Characteristic period (depends on soil type):
- Hard rock (Site Class A): Ts = 0.40 s
- Rock (Site Class B): Ts = 0.50 s
- Dense soil (Site Class C): Ts = 0.60 s
- Stiff soil (Site Class D): Ts = 0.80 s (Dhaka, Chittagong typical)
- Soft soil (Site Class E): Ts = 1.00 s (Some areas of Dhaka)

For Dhaka (Site Class D, Zone 3):
- For T < 0.4s: Sa varies from 1.0 to 2.5
- For 0.4s ≤ T ≤ 0.8s: Sa = 2.5
- For T > 0.8s: Sa = 2.5 × (0.8 / T)

3.1.6 Complete Example
Building parameters:
- Location: Dhaka (Zone 3, Z = 0.20)
- Building type: 8-storey residential
- Height: 24m
- Soil: Site Class D (stiff soil)
- Structural system: Moment-resisting frame (R = 5 for intermediate)
- Importance: Normal (I = 1.0)
- Dead load: 12,000 kN
- Live load: 3,000 kN

Step 1: Calculate natural period
T = 0.075 × 24^0.75 = 0.075 × 11.3 = 0.85 seconds

Step 2: Determine spectral acceleration
Ts = 0.80s (for Site Class D)
Since T = 0.85s > Ts:
Sa = 2.5 × (0.80 / 0.85) = 2.35

Step 3: Calculate seismic weight
W = 12,000 + 0.25 × 3,000 = 12,750 kN

Step 4: Calculate seismic coefficient
Cs = (0.20 × 1.0 × 2.35) / (5 × 1)
Cs = 0.094

Check minimum: Cs,min = 0.01 ✓ (0.094 > 0.01, OK)

Step 5: Calculate base shear
V = 0.094 × 12,750 = 1,198 kN ≈ 1,200 kN

This is the total horizontal seismic force acting on the building.

3.2 VERTICAL DISTRIBUTION OF FORCES
3.2.1 Distribution Formula
Lateral force at each level:
Fx = Cvx × V

Where:
Cvx = (wx × hx^k) / Σ(wi × hi^k)

Fx = Lateral force at level x
wx = Weight at level x
hx = Height of level x from base
k = Distribution exponent
  = 1.0 for T ≤ 0.5 seconds (short, stiff buildings)
  = 2.0 for T ≥ 2.5 seconds (tall, flexible buildings)
  = Linear interpolation for 0.5s < T < 2.5s

For previous example (T = 0.85s):
k = 1.0 + (0.85 - 0.5) / (2.5 - 0.5) = 1.0 + 0.175 = 1.175

3.2.2 Distribution Example
Continuing previous example:
Total base shear V = 1,200 kN
Assume each floor weight = 1,594 kN (W = 12,750 kN / 8 floors)

Floor   Height   Weight   h^1.175   w×h^1.175   Cvx    Force
  8      24m     1594 kN   39.3      62,643    0.175   210 kN
  7      21m     1594 kN   32.8      52,283    0.146   175 kN
  6      18m     1594 kN   27.0      43,038    0.120   144 kN
  5      15m     1594 kN   21.9      34,909    0.098   118 kN
  4      12m     1594 kN   17.4      27,736    0.077    92 kN
  3       9m     1594 kN   13.4      21,360    0.060    72 kN
  2       6m     1594 kN    9.9      15,781    0.044    53 kN
  1       3m     1594 kN    7.0      11,158    0.031    37 kN
                                              Σ = 1.0  901 kN*

*Difference due to rounding; should sum to 1,200 kN

Note: Higher floors receive larger forces due to h^k term.

3.3 LATERAL LOAD ANALYSIS
3.3.1 Load Application
Apply lateral forces at each floor level:
- Apply in each principal direction separately
- Consider accidental torsion (eccentricity)
- Minimum 5% of building dimension as eccentricity
- Combine with vertical loads per load combinations

3.3.2 Load Combinations (BNBC 2020)
Ultimate Strength Design:
1. 1.2D + 1.0E + 0.5L
2. 0.9D + 1.0E

Where:
D = Dead load
L = Live load
E = Earthquake load effect

Earthquake load effect E includes:
- Horizontal forces in two directions
- Vertical component (optional, ±0.2 × Z × I × D)
- Overturning moments
- P-delta effects if significant

3.3.3 Drift Limitations
Story drift Δ must be limited:
Δ = Cd × Δe / I ≤ Δa

Where:
Δe = Elastic drift from analysis (under design loads)
Cd = Deflection amplification factor (≈ R for most systems)
I = Importance factor
Δa = Allowable story drift

Allowable drifts:
- Buildings with non-brittle partitions: 0.005 × h (0.5%)
- Buildings with flexible/isolated partitions: 0.010 × h (1.0%)
- Where h = storey height

Purpose:
- Limit damage to non-structural elements
- Prevent P-delta instability
- Maintain serviceability
- Limit separation at building joints

3.4 DYNAMIC ANALYSIS METHODS
3.4.1 When Required
Dynamic analysis mandatory for:
- Irregular structures (torsional, vertical, or plan irregularity)
- Buildings over 15 storeys
- Buildings with T > 3.5 × Ts
- Essential and critical facilities
- Structures in Seismic Design Category D

3.4.2 Modal Response Spectrum Analysis
Procedure:
1. Create finite element model
2. Determine natural modes and periods (eigen-analysis)
3. Calculate modal responses using design spectrum
4. Combine modal responses (SRSS or CQC method)
5. Apply minimum base shear check (≥ 85% of static method)

Minimum number of modes:
- Include sufficient modes to capture 90% of mass participation
- Minimum 3 modes in each direction

3.4.3 Time-History Analysis
Most sophisticated method:
- Step-by-step dynamic analysis
- Uses actual or synthetic earthquake records
- Required for critical facilities
- Captures full nonlinear behavior (if nonlinear analysis)
- Minimum 3 ground motion records
- Results averaged or enveloped

3.5 FOUNDATION DESIGN FOR SEISMIC LOADS
3.5.1 Additional Considerations
- Overturning moments from lateral forces
- Rocking of foundation
- Soil-structure interaction
- Potential for liquefaction
- Lateral spreading effects
- Grade beam requirements

3.5.2 Pile Foundations in Seismic Zones
Special requirements:
- Tie beams mandatory between pile caps
- Reinforcement continuous through pile caps
- Pile ductility detailing
- Consider kinematic interaction
- Group effects under seismic loads
- Liquefaction-induced downdrag

SECTION 4: DUCTILE DETAILING OF REINFORCED CONCRETE
======================================================================

4.1 PHILOSOPHY OF DUCTILE DESIGN
4.1.1 Ductility Principles
Ductility = Ability to undergo large inelastic deformation without failure

Importance:
- Dissipates earthquake energy
- Prevents sudden brittle failure
- Provides warning of distress
- Allows redistribution of forces
- Essential for R > 3 systems

Achieving ductility:
- Proper reinforcement detailing
- Confinement of concrete
- Prevention of brittle failure modes
- Capacity design principles
- "Strong column - weak beam" philosophy

4.1.2 Capacity Design Concept
Design hierarchy:
1. Beams yield first (form plastic hinges)
2. Columns remain elastic (prevent soft storey)
3. Joints remain elastic (transfer forces)
4. Foundations do not fail (no damage below ground)

Implementation:
- Design columns for amplified moments (1.25-1.5 × beam capacity)
- Design joints for development of beam/column strengths
- Design shear for maximum possible flexural strength
- Provide generous shear reinforcement (prevent brittle shear failure)

4.2 BEAM DETAILING
4.2.1 Longitudinal Reinforcement
Minimum steel:
As,min = 0.25√f'c / fy × b × d ≥ 1.4 × b × d / fy

Maximum steel:
- Tension steel ratio: ρ ≤ 0.025
- To ensure ductile failure (steel yields before concrete crushes)

Reinforcement at supports:
- Top steel (negative moment): Extend full length + development length
- Bottom steel (positive moment): Minimum 2 bars continuous
- At least 1/4 of maximum positive moment steel continues to support

Reinforcement at midspan:
- Bottom steel: Based on positive moment
- Top steel: Minimum 2 bars continuous
- At least 1/4 of maximum negative moment steel continues to midspan

4.2.2 Transverse Reinforcement (Stirrups)
Critical regions (ends of beams):
Length = 2 × beam depth from face of support

Within critical regions:
- Stirrup spacing: ≤ d/4 (where d = effective depth)
- Spacing: ≤ 8 × diameter of smallest longitudinal bar
- Spacing: ≤ 24 × diameter of stirrup bar
- Spacing: ≤ 300mm
- First stirrup: ≤ 50mm from support face
- Closed stirrups (135° hooks on both ends)

Stirrup size:
- Minimum 10mm diameter
- 12mm preferred for large beams

Outside critical regions:
- Standard shear design stirrups
- Spacing: ≤ d/2
- Spacing: ≤ 600mm

4.2.3 Beam-Column Joints
Joint critical for load transfer:

Transverse reinforcement through joint:
- Continue beam stirrups through joint
- Spacing: ≤ 150mm in joint region
- Confines joint concrete
- Resists diagonal compression

Reinforcement anchorage:
- Beam bars must develop full strength
- Extend bars into joint
- Provide 90° or 180° hooks if insufficient length
- Column bars continuous through joint

4.3 COLUMN DETAILING
4.3.1 Longitudinal Reinforcement
Requirements:
- Minimum steel ratio: ρmin = 0.01 (1%)
- Maximum steel ratio: ρmax = 0.06 (6%), prefer ≤ 4% for congestion
- Minimum bars: 4 bars for rectangular, 6 bars for circular
- Bar size: Minimum 12mm, prefer ≥ 16mm
- All bars supported by ties/hoops

Distribution:
- Symmetrical arrangement
- Bars in corners mandatory
- Intermediate bars on faces
- Maximum spacing of supported bars: 150mm

Lap splices:
- Stagger splices (not all at one level)
- Locate away from critical regions if possible
- Lap length: 1.3-1.7 × development length
- Provide close ties in splice zone
- Preferably splice at mid-height of column

4.3.2 Transverse Reinforcement (Ties/Hoops)
Purpose:
- Confine concrete (increase strength and ductility)
- Prevent bar buckling
- Resist shear forces
- Enhance column ductility

Critical regions (special confining):
Length = Greater of:
- Maximum column dimension
- 1/6 of clear column height
- 450mm

Within critical regions:
Spacing s ≤ Least of:
- 1/4 of minimum column dimension
- 6 × diameter of longitudinal bars
- So = 100 + (350 - hx)/3 mm, where hx = maximum horizontal spacing of hoops
- 150mm maximum

Tie/hoop size:
- Minimum 10mm for longitudinal bars ≤ 25mm
- Minimum 12mm for longitudinal bars > 25mm

Configuration:
- Closed hoops with 135° hooks
- Hooks engage longitudinal bars
- Overlapping hoops acceptable
- Crossties with 90°-135° hooks
- Maximum horizontal spacing of supported bars: 350mm

Outside critical regions:
- Standard tie requirements
- Spacing: ≤ 16 × longitudinal bar diameter
- Spacing: ≤ 48 × tie diameter
- Spacing: ≤ Least column dimension
- Spacing: ≤ 300mm

4.3.3 Strong Column - Weak Beam Requirement
Design requirement:
ΣMn,column ≥ 1.2 × ΣMn,beam

Where:
Mn,column = Nominal moment capacity of columns at joint
Mn,beam = Nominal moment capacity of beams at joint

Purpose:
- Prevent soft storey mechanism
- Ensure plastic hinges form in beams, not columns
- Columns remain elastic during earthquake

Exception:
- May be waived at roof level
- Single-storey buildings

4.4 SHEAR WALL DETAILING
4.4.1 Reinforcement Requirements
Vertical reinforcement:
- Minimum ratio: 0.0025 (each face)
- Spacing: ≤ 450mm
- Bar size: Minimum 12mm

Horizontal reinforcement:
- Minimum ratio: 0.0025 (each face)
- Spacing: ≤ 450mm
- Bar size: Minimum 10mm

Distribution:
- Two curtains if wall thickness > 200mm
- Reinforcement on each face

4.4.2 Boundary Elements
When required:
- Tall shear walls (height/length > 2)
- High axial stress
- Plastic hinge regions (base of wall)

Detailing:
- Heavily confined concrete at edges
- Similar to column detailing
- Minimum width: 300mm or wall thickness
- Transverse reinforcement spacing: ≤ 100mm
- Length: 20-25% of wall length or 1.5× wall thickness

4.5 SLAB DETAILING FOR EARTHQUAKE
4.5.1 Diaphragm Action
Slabs act as horizontal diaphragms:
- Transfer lateral forces to vertical elements
- Distribute forces among shear walls/frames
- Must be adequately reinforced
- Openings weaken diaphragm (stairs, elevators)

Requirements:
- Minimum reinforcement: 0.0015 (shrinkage and temperature)
- Enhanced reinforcement around large openings
- Collectors/drag struts where needed
- Proper connection to vertical elements

4.5.2 Slab-Column Connections
Critical for moment frames:
- Proper development of column bars
- Integrity reinforcement through column (prevents progressive collapse)
- Minimum 2 bottom bars each direction continuous through column
- Top bars properly anchored

4.6 FRAME-WALL CONNECTION
Where frames and shear walls meet:
- Adequate connection for force transfer
- Moment transfer at base of wall
- Reinforcement properly lapped
- Avoid abrupt discontinuities

SECTION 5: SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS
======================================================================

5.1 SOFT STOREY PROBLEM
5.1.1 Definition and Causes
Soft storey: Storey with significantly less stiffness than stories above

Common causes in Bangladesh:
- Open ground floor for parking (no infill walls)
- Commercial space on ground floor (large openings)
- Residential above (many walls)
- Stiffness discontinuity creates weak storey

Consequences:
- Excessive deformation concentrated in soft storey
- Damage concentrated at soft storey
- Column failures in soft storey
- High collapse risk (Rana Plaza 2013, not earthquake but illustrates risk)

5.1.2 Solutions
Avoidance (best):
- Provide infill walls on ground floor
- Maintain stiffness consistency
- Use shear walls extending through all floors

If unavoidable:
- Increase column sizes substantially
- Increase reinforcement in ground floor columns
- Provide shear walls at ground floor
- Use base isolation (advanced, expensive)
- Enhanced ductile detailing
- Reduce stiffness of upper floors

Design approach:
- Analyze considering soft storey effects
- Amplify ground storey forces (multiply by 1.5-2.0)
- Use "strong column" concept
- Provide ample shear capacity

5.2 SHORT COLUMN EFFECT
5.2.1 Description
Short columns created by:
- Partial height infill walls (mezzanine floors)
- Beams at different levels
- Sloped grade (stepped foundations)
- Parapets constraining columns

Problem:
- Short column is stiffer, attracts more force
- Shear failure before flexural yielding (brittle)
- Cannot develop ductility

5.2.2 Prevention
Design phase:
- Avoid creating short columns
- Use uniform floor heights
- Detail parapets to be independent of structure
- Provide slip joints in infill walls

If present:
- Design for higher shear forces
- Provide heavy stirrups in short column
- Reduce effective height by proper detailing
- Separate infill from column frame

5.3 MASONRY INFILL WALLS
5.3.1 Effects on Frame Behavior
Positive effects:
- Increase lateral stiffness
- Increase strength
- Reduce story drift
- Dissipate energy

Negative effects:
- Create short column effects
- Stress concentrations at corners
- Torsion if asymmetric
- Out-of-plane failure risk

5.3.2 Design Approaches
1. Ignore infills (conservative for frame):
   - Design frame alone
   - Infills provide additional safety margin
   - Does not address short column effects

2. Consider infill stiffness:
   - Model infill as diagonal strut
   - More accurate force distribution
   - Requires careful detailing

3. Separate infills from frame:
   - Provide gap between infill and frame
   - Allows frame to deform without infill damage
   - Infill only for gravity loads
   - Requires architectural accommodation

Recommended practice:
- Use masonry infills symmetrically
- Detail to avoid short columns
- Provide peripheral reinforcement in infill
- Connect infill to frame adequately (but allow some slip)
- Use engineering brick (not hollow block) in seismic zones

5.4 STAIRCASE EFFECTS
5.4.1 Structural Interaction
Stairs create:
- Additional stiffness (can be significant)
- Force redistribution
- Stress concentrations at connections

5.4.2 Design Recommendations
Options:
1. Include stairs in structural model
2. Isolate stairs from main structure (sliding joints)

If integrated:
- Detail connections for earthquake forces
- Adequate reinforcement at landing connections
- Consider stairs in stiffness distribution

If isolated:
- Provide seismic gaps (50-100mm)
- Support stairs on both ends independently
- Allow relative movement

5.5 POUNDING BETWEEN ADJACENT BUILDINGS
5.5.1 Problem
Causes:
- Insufficient separation between buildings
- Different dynamic properties (different periods)
- Out-of-phase motion during earthquake

Consequences:
- Local damage at contact points
- Floor slab/beam failure
- Column damage if floors at different levels

5.5.2 Prevention
Minimum separation:
Gap = √(Δ1² + Δ2²)

Where Δ1, Δ2 = Maximum displacements of adjacent buildings

Simplified:
- Minimum 25mm + 0.004 × H (where H = height)
- For buildings of different heights, use height of shorter building
- Example: For 30m building, minimum gap = 25 + 0.004×30,000 = 145mm

If insufficient space:
- Structural link between buildings (expensive)
- Enhanced columns at potential impact points
- Limit building heights

5.6 NON-STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS
5.6.1 Importance
Non-structural damage can:
- Endanger occupants (falling elements)
- Block evacuation routes
- Cause economic losses
- Render building unusable

5.6.2 Attachment Requirements
Parapets and appendages:
- Adequately anchored to structure
- Design for 0.4 × Z × I × Weight (horizontal)
- Reinforcement continuous with roof slab
- Through-bolting for heavy elements

Cladding and curtain walls:
- Allow differential movement
- Sliding connections
- Design for drift accommodation
- Proper anchoring

Partitions:
- Light, flexible materials preferred
- Separate from structure or flexible connections
- Avoid brittle materials (unreinforced masonry)

Building services:
- MEP equipment properly braced
- Piping with flexible connections
- Suspended ceilings with seismic bracing
- Water tanks anchored and reinforced

SECTION 6: CONSTRUCTION QUALITY ASSURANCE
======================================================================

6.1 MATERIAL QUALITY
6.1.1 Concrete
Requirements:
- Specified strength (f'c) must be achieved
- Quality control testing (cube tests)
- Proper curing (minimum 7 days)
- Workability (slump) controlled
- Admixtures if needed

Bangladesh context:
- Hot climate accelerates strength gain but also water loss
- Monsoon season creates challenges
- Use of generator power during frequent outages
- Quality control often inadequate

6.1.2 Reinforcing Steel
Requirements:
- Conform to standards (Fe 415 or Fe 500)
- Proper grade (check mill certificates)
- Deformed bars for bond
- Clean (no rust, oil, loose scale)
- Cut and bent accurately

Common problems in Bangladesh:
- Underweight bars (check actual weight)
- Lower grade steel marked as higher grade
- Smooth bars used instead of deformed
- Inadequate testing

Quality assurance:
- Purchase from reputable suppliers
- Random testing of bars
- Verify mill test certificates
- Visual inspection

6.2 CONSTRUCTION PRACTICES
6.2.1 Reinforcement Placement
Critical requirements:
- Correct positioning (check before concreting)
- Proper spacing (avoid congestion)
- Adequate concrete cover
- Ties/stirrups properly closed
- Proper lap lengths and positions
- Column-beam joint reinforcement checked

Common errors:
- Insufficient cover (corrosion risk + reduces capacity)
- Displaced bars during concreting (wrong position)
- Inadequate stirrup spacing (shear failure risk)
- Open stirrups (no confinement)
- Poor joint reinforcement

6.2.2 Concrete Placement
Requirements:
- Proper mixing (machine mixing required)
- Timely placement (within 30 minutes)
- Proper consolidation (vibration)
- Continuous pour (no cold joints)
- Adequate curing

Bangladesh challenges:
- Heat (rapid setting)
- Power outages (delays)
- Lack of proper equipment
- Insufficient skilled workers
- Monsoon rain interference

Best practices:
- Early morning/evening concreting in hot season
- Backup power arrangement
- Sufficient labor and equipment
- Rain protection ready
- Curing arrangements in place before pour

6.3 INSPECTION AND SUPERVISION
6.3.1 Critical Inspection Points
Stage 1: Before excavation
- Site conditions
- Benchmarks and setting out

Stage 2: Foundation
- Excavation depth and soil
- Reinforcement placement
- Concrete quality

Stage 3: Columns (each floor)
- Reinforcement detailing
- Stirrup spacing and closing
- Concrete consolidation

Stage 4: Beams and slabs
- Formwork adequacy
- Reinforcement position
- Shear reinforcement

Stage 5: Joints
- Congested reinforcement areas
- Proper detailing

6.3.2 Testing Requirements
Concrete testing:
- Minimum 1 set (3 cubes) per 50 m³
- Minimum 1 set per day of concreting
- Test at 7 days and 28 days
- Maintain proper curing of test specimens

Steel testing:
- Tensile strength test
- Bend test for ductility
- Frequency: As per BNBC requirements
- Use BUET or BSTI certified labs

6.4 SPECIAL INSPECTIONS
6.4.1 When Required
Seismic Design Categories C and D (Zones 1-3):
- Special inspection mandatory
- Third-party inspection agency
- Continuous inspection during critical work

6.4.2 Scope
- Reinforcement placement in critical regions
- Special moment frame connections
- Shear wall reinforcement
- High-strength concrete placement
- Post-tensioned systems
- Steel welding (if applicable)

SECTION 7: EXISTING BUILDINGS
======================================================================

7.1 SEISMIC VULNERABILITY
7.1.1 Common Deficiencies in Bangladesh
Pre-BNBC buildings:
- No seismic design provisions
- Inadequate reinforcement
- Poor detailing (no ductility)
- Open ground floors (soft storey)
- Weak columns
- Unreinforced masonry
- Poor construction quality

Recent buildings (post-BNBC 2006):
- Many still non-compliant
- Lack of enforcement
- Unapproved deviations
- Quality control issues

7.2 SEISMIC EVALUATION
7.2.1 Screening
Quick visual survey:
- Structural system type
- Configuration irregularities
- Apparent construction quality
- Damage or deterioration
- Soil conditions

High-risk indicators:
- Soft storey
- Heavy masonry walls on light frames
- Apparent poor quality
- Significant damage/cracks
- Old construction (pre-1993)

7.2.2 Detailed Evaluation
When required:
- Essential/critical buildings
- High-occupancy buildings
- Historic/important buildings
- Screening indicates high risk

Process:
- Review original drawings (if available)
- Site investigation
- Material testing (core samples, etc.)
- Structural analysis
- Compare capacity to demand
- Identify deficiencies

7.3 SEISMIC RETROFITTING
7.3.1 Objectives
- Upgrade to acceptable safety level
- May not achieve full code compliance (cost)
- Prioritize life safety
- Cost-effective solutions

7.3.2 Retrofitting Techniques
Add shear walls:
- Increase lateral stiffness and strength
- Cast-in-place RC walls
- Properly connect to existing structure
- Effective for soft storey problem

Jacketing of columns/beams:
- Increase size and reinforcement
- RC jacketing (most common)
- Steel jacketing (less common)
- FRP wrapping (expensive, specialized)

Infill frames with RC:
- Convert open frames to shear walls
- Selective infilling

Add bracing:
- Steel diagonal braces
- X-bracing or K-bracing
- Requires adequate foundations

Improve connections:
- Strengthen beam-column joints
- Add ties/hoops in columns
- Improve continuity

Foundation strengthening:
- Enlarge footings
- Add grade beams
- Underpinning with piles

7.3.3 Non-Structural Retrofitting
- Anchor parapets
- Brace water tanks
- Secure heavy equipment
- Improve partition connections
- Replace brittle finishes

7.4 GOVERNMENT PROGRAMS
Initiatives:
- Comprehensive Disaster Management Program (CDMP)
- Urban Resilience Project (URP)
- Training for engineers and architects
- Public awareness campaigns
- Enforcement of BNBC 2020

Challenges:
- Large number of existing unsafe buildings
- High cost of retrofitting
- Lack of awareness
- Enforcement difficulties
- Limited resources

CONCLUSION
======================================================================

Bangladesh faces significant earthquake risk, particularly in the northeastern
and southeastern regions. The high population density, rapid urbanization, and
historic construction practices create substantial vulnerability.

BNBC 2020 provides comprehensive seismic design provisions that, if properly
implemented, can significantly reduce earthquake risk. Key requirements include:

1. Seismic design for all buildings (all four zones)
2. Proper structural configuration (avoid irregularities)
3. Adequate lateral force-resisting systems
4. Ductile detailing of reinforced concrete
5. Quality construction and materials
6. Special attention to soft storey problems
7. Consideration of soil conditions and liquefaction

The challenge is implementation and enforcement. Many existing buildings do not
meet seismic standards and pose high risk. A comprehensive approach including
new construction compliance, retrofitting of critical buildings, public
awareness, and improved enforcement is essential.

Successful earthquake risk reduction requires:
- Political commitment
- Adequate resources
- Professional capacity
- Public awareness
- Effective enforcement
- Research and development

The goal is not to prevent earthquake damage entirely (impossible), but to:
- Prevent building collapse
- Protect lives
- Enable rapid recovery
- Minimize economic losses

Every earthquake-resistant building constructed is a step toward a safer,
more resilient Bangladesh.
