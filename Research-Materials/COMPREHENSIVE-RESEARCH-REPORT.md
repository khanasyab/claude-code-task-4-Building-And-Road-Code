## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This comprehensive research report documents building codes, construction standards, and public works documents from **Bangladesh** and **Sri Lanka**, with comparative analysis to the **Pakistan Building & Roads Code** (1989/1994 versions). The research reveals significant findings about the evolution of construction standards in South Asia and provides insights into institutional frameworks inherited from British colonial administration.

**Research Date:** November 10, 2025
**Geographic Scope:** South Asia (Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Pakistan)
**Historical Context:** Post-colonial administrative evolution (1947-present)
**Primary Finding:** Despite shared heritage, three distinct evolutionary paths emerged

## PART 2: SRI LANKA FINDINGS

### 2.1 Overview

**Country:** Democratic Socialist Republic of Sri Lanka
**Historical Name:** Ceylon (until 1972)
**Population:** ~22 million
**Historical Context:** British colonial administration, PWD abolished 1969

### 2.2 Primary Codes and Standards

#### **IMPORTANT:** No Single Unified National Building Code

Unlike Bangladesh (BNBC) or Pakistan (B&R Code), Sri Lanka has a **distributed regulatory framework** across multiple authorities:

1. Urban Development Authority (UDA) - Planning and building regulations
2. Sri Lanka Standards Institution (SLSI) - National standards
3. Construction Industry Development Authority (CIDA) - Public works specifications
4. Department of Buildings - Government building standards
5. Local Authorities - Municipal regulations

**Key Quote:** "In the Sri Lankan context sustainable building codes do exist but are not always legally enforceable"

#### Urban Development Authority (UDA) Regulations

**Legal Basis:**
- Gazette Extraordinary No. 392/9 dated 10th March 1986
- Major revision: 2021 (first in 35 years)

**Scope:**
- Planning regulations for urban development areas
- Building regulations in declared UDA areas
- Development control and permits

**Key Provisions:**
- Written approval required for development
- Building categories: A, B, C (based on use and floors)
- Qualified professionals must prepare plans
- Development permit process
- Annual licensing (2021 requirement)

**Authority:**
- Originally delegated to local authorities
- Partial reclamation by UDA (February 1, 2017)
- UDA supervision and control maintained

#### Sri Lanka Standards Institution (SLSI)

**Establishment:** 1964 (Bureau of Ceylon Standards Act No. 38)
**Status:** National Standards Body
**Standards Count:** ~2,000 SLS standards
**Mandatory Standards:** Only 32 standards (building materials, electrical, food, consumer products)

**SLS-EN SERIES (EUROCODES) - Major Achievement:**

Sri Lanka has adopted Eurocodes with National Annexes:

- **SLS-EN 1990:** Basis of Structural Design
- **SLS-EN 1991:** Actions on Structures
- **SLS-EN 1992:** Design of Concrete Structures (+ National Annex)
- **SLS-EN 1993:** Design of Steel Structures
- **SLS-EN 1994:** Design of Composite Steel and Concrete Structures
- **SLS-EN 1995:** Design of Timber Structures
- **SLS-EN 1996:** Design of Masonry Structures
- **SLS-EN 1997:** Geotechnical Design
- **SLS-EN 1998:** Design of Structures for Earthquake Resistance
- **SLS-EN 1999:** Design of Aluminum Structures

**Material Standards:**

Cement:
- SLS 107: Ordinary Portland Cement
- SLS 515: Masonry Cement
- SLS 1247: Blended Hydraulic Cement

Steel:
- SLS 26: Plain Steel Bars for Reinforcement
- SLS 375: Ribbed Steel Bars for Reinforcement
- SLS 949 Pt 1: Hot Rolled Steel Round Bars

Masonry:
- SLS 855: Cement Blocks
- Various brick and block specifications

#### Construction Industry Development Authority (CIDA)

**Formerly:** ICTAD (Institute for Construction Training and Development)
**Role:** Public works specifications, contractor registration

**KEY SPECIFICATIONS (SCA Series):**

**Application:**

## PART 3: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Three Models of Evolution

From the same British colonial PWD heritage, three distinct models emerged:

#### Model 1: PAKISTAN - Traditional Unified Code
**Pakistan Building & Roads Code 1989/1994**

**Characteristics:**
- ✅ Single unified code document
- ✅ Traditional PWD organizational structure
- ✅ Comprehensive coverage in one framework
- ⚠️ Code aging (1989/1994 versions)
- ⚠️ Modernization needs

**Strengths:**
- Clear, unified reference
- Comprehensive scope
- Established framework
- Integrated approach

**Challenges:**
- Code updating process
- Age of current version
- Modern standards alignment
- International code harmonization

#### Model 2: BANGLADESH - Modernized National Code
**Bangladesh National Building Code (BNBC) 2020**

**Characteristics:**
- ✅ Modern national building code (2020)
- ✅ International standards alignment (ASCE 7-05)
- ✅ Comprehensive and up-to-date
- ✅ PWD structure maintained alongside specialized agencies
- ✅ Regular updates (1993 → 2006 → 2012 → 2020)

**Strengths:**
- Recent modernization
- International best practices
- Comprehensive coverage
- Clear mandatory status
- Active development and updating

**Challenges:**
- Implementation and enforcement
- Capacity building needs
- Transition from older codes
- Awareness and training

#### Model 3: SRI LANKA - Distributed Specialized System
**Multiple Authorities, Eurocode Adoption**

**Characteristics:**
- ✅ PWD abolished early (1969)
- ✅ Specialized agencies created
- ✅ Eurocode adoption (SLS-EN series)
- ✅ Modern international standards
- ⚠️ No single unified building code
- ⚠️ Distributed regulatory framework

**Strengths:**
- Modern Eurocode adoption
- Specialized expertise
- Agency focus
- International alignment

**Challenges:**
- Coordination between authorities
- No unified code reference
- Variable enforcement
- Complexity for users

## PART 5: KEY FINDINGS

### 5.1 Major Discoveries

#### Finding 1: Three Distinct Evolutionary Paths

Despite identical starting points (British colonial PWD), three countries evolved three different systems:

1. **Pakistan:** Maintained traditional unified code, needs modernization
2. **Bangladesh:** Modernized with BNBC 2020, international alignment
3. **Sri Lanka:** Distributed system, early Eurocode adoption, no unified code

**Significance:** No single "best" model - each has strengths and challenges suited to national context.

#### Finding 2: Bangladesh Code Modernization Success

**BNBC 2020 represents a major achievement:**
- Modern international alignment (ASCE 7-05)
- Comprehensive coverage
- Recent publication (2022)
- Regular update cycle demonstrated
- Active research support (HBRI)

**Lesson:** Regular code updating is achievable with dedicated agency and resources.

#### Finding 3: Sri Lanka Eurocode Adoption

**SLS-EN series adoption is significant:**
- Modern international structural codes
- National annexes for local conditions
- Progressive technical approach
- University teaching alignment
- Future-oriented standards

**Lesson:** Eurocode adoption viable for South Asian context with appropriate adaptation.

#### Finding 4: Common Heritage, Divergent Solutions

**All three countries:**
- Inherited same PWD system
- Face similar challenges
- Serve similar development needs
- Have comparable resources

**Yet developed:**
- Different institutional structures
- Different code frameworks
- Different modernization paths
- Different enforcement mechanisms

**Lesson:** Colonial legacy does not determine development path; national choices matter.
