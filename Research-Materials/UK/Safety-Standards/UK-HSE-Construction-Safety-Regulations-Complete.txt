OVERVIEW
====================================================================================================

The Health and Safety Executive (HSE) is the national independent regulator for work-related
health, safety and welfare in Great Britain. Construction is one of the most heavily regulated
sectors due to its inherent hazards.

Legal Framework:
- Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 (Primary legislation)
- Construction-specific regulations (Statutory Instruments)
- Approved Codes of Practice (ACOPs)
- HSE Guidance Notes

Scope:
- All construction work in Great Britain (England, Scotland, Wales)
- Commercial, industrial, and domestic construction
- New build, refurbishment, maintenance, and demolition

THE CONSTRUCTION (DESIGN AND MANAGEMENT) REGULATIONS 2015 (CDM 2015)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active - Came into force 6 April 2015
Replaced: CDM 2007

Purpose:
Manage health, safety and welfare when carrying out construction projects. CDM 2015 sets out
what people involved in construction work need to do to protect themselves and others from harm.

Application:
- Applies to ALL construction work
- Additional duties for projects with more than one contractor
- Specific requirements for notifiable projects

Key Dutyholders and Their Duties:

1. CLIENTS
   Duties:
   - Make suitable arrangements for managing a project
   - Ensure other dutyholders are appointed
   - Ensure sufficient time and resources
   - Provide pre-construction information
   - Ensure principal designer and principal contractor fulfill duties
   - Ensure welfare facilities are provided

   For Notifiable Projects:
   - Notify HSE before work starts
   - Appoint principal designer and principal contractor in writing

2. DESIGNERS (including architects, engineers, quantity surveyors)
   Duties:
   - Consider health and safety in design decisions
   - Eliminate, reduce or control foreseeable risks
   - Provide information about remaining risks
   - Coordinate work with others

3. PRINCIPAL DESIGNER (for projects with more than one contractor)
   Duties:
   - Plan, manage, monitor and coordinate health and safety in pre-construction phase
   - Identify, eliminate or control foreseeable risks
   - Ensure designers carry out their duties
   - Prepare and provide health and safety information
   - Liaise with principal contractor
   - Prepare health and safety file

4. PRINCIPAL CONTRACTOR (for projects with more than one contractor)
   Duties:
   - Plan, manage, monitor and coordinate health and safety during construction
   - Liaise with principal designer
   - Prepare construction phase plan
   - Organize cooperation between contractors
   - Ensure suitable site inductions provided
   - Prevent unauthorized access
   - Ensure welfare facilities provided and maintained
   - Consult and engage with workers
   - Provide principal designer with information for health and safety file

5. CONTRACTORS (all contractors)
   Duties:
   - Plan, manage and monitor own work
   - Ensure workers are consulted and engaged
   - Ensure workers have appropriate skills, knowledge and training
   - Provide supervision, instructions and information
   - Do not start work unless satisfied construction phase plan is sufficient
   - Comply with directions from principal designer or principal contractor
   - Cooperate with others

6. WORKERS
   Duties:
   - Check own competence
   - Cooperate with others
   - Report anything likely to endanger health or safety
   - Follow training and instructions

Notifiable Projects:
Definition: Projects lasting more than 30 working days with more than 20 workers at any point,
           OR exceeding 500 person days

Notification Requirement:
- Client must notify HSE before work begins
- Use form F10 (online notification)
- Display notification on site

Construction Phase Plan:
Required for all projects with more than one contractor
Must be prepared BEFORE construction starts
Content: Site rules, arrangements for managing health and safety, emergency procedures,
         site induction, welfare facilities, etc.

Health and Safety File:
Required for all projects
Contains information about the building needed for future construction work
Passed to client on completion
Updated throughout building's life

Key Guidance:
- HSE L153: Managing health and safety in construction
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/construction/cdm/2015/

CONSTRUCTION (HEAD PROTECTION) REGULATIONS 1989
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active
Purpose: Require head protection on construction sites
Key Requirements:
- Employers must provide and maintain suitable head protection
- Workers must wear head protection where there is risk of head injury
- Head protection must comply with relevant standards

Exceptions: Limited exceptions where neck injury risk greater than head injury risk

WORK AT HEIGHT REGULATIONS 2005 (amended 2007)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active
Purpose: Prevent death and injury from falls from height
Application: All work at height where there is a risk of a fall liable to cause personal injury

Key Principle: Avoid work at height where reasonably practicable

Hierarchy of Control:
1. Avoid work at height if possible
2. Use work equipment or other measures to prevent falls
3. Use work equipment or other measures to minimize distance and consequences of a fall

Duty Holders: Employers, self-employed, and those who control work at height

Key Requirements:

1. WORK PLANNING
   - Properly planned by competent person
   - Appropriate supervision
   - Risk assessment

2. WORK EQUIPMENT
   - Must be appropriate for the task
   - Inspected before first use, after installation, and periodically
   - Inspection records kept

3. COLLECTIVE PROTECTION
   Preferred over personal protection:
   - Guard rails
   - Working platforms
   - Scaffold
   - Podium steps
   - Mobile elevated work platforms (MEWPs)

4. PERSONAL PROTECTION (when collective measures not practicable)
   - Safety harnesses and lanyards
   - Fall arrest systems
   - Work restraint systems
   - Rope access techniques

5. FRAGILE SURFACES
   - Identified and protected
   - Warning notices
   - Platforms or coverings provided

6. FALLING OBJECTS
   - Prevention measures
   - Exclusion zones
   - Barriers and covered walkways

7. INSPECTION
   Regular inspection of:
   - Scaffolding
   - Working platforms
   - Personal fall protection equipment
   - Excavation supports

   Inspection Frequency:
   - Before first use
   - After installation or assembly
   - At regular intervals (at least every 7 days for scaffolding)
   - After events likely to affect stability (e.g., adverse weather)

   Records:
   - Inspection records must be kept
   - Available on site

Key Guidance:
- HSE Guidance (INDG401): Working at height
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/work-at-height/

LIFTING OPERATIONS AND LIFTING EQUIPMENT REGULATIONS 1998 (LOLER)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active
Purpose: Reduce risks from lifting equipment
Application: All lifting equipment and lifting operations

Key Requirements:

1. EQUIPMENT STRENGTH AND STABILITY
   - Equipment must be of adequate strength and stability
   - Properly installed and positioned
   - Suitable for the task

2. SAFE WORKING LOAD (SWL)
   - Equipment clearly marked with SWL
   - Not exceeded

3. LIFTING OPERATIONS
   - Properly planned by competent person
   - Appropriately supervised
   - Carried out in a safe manner

4. LIFTING PEOPLE
   - Equipment must be designed for lifting people
   - Specific requirements for safety

5. THOROUGH EXAMINATION AND INSPECTION
   Before First Use:
   - New equipment: thorough examination
   - Existing equipment (new to you): thorough examination or inspection

   Regular Examination:
   - Lifting equipment used for lifting people: every 6 months
   - Other lifting equipment: every 12 months
   - Lifting accessories: every 6 months or according to examination scheme

   After Installation:
   - Thorough examination after installation at new site (if safety depends on installation)

   After Exceptional Circumstances:
   - After events likely to affect safety (e.g., accidents, modifications, prolonged out of use)

   Records:
   - Reports of thorough examinations must be kept
   - Available for inspection

6. DEFECTS
   - Equipment with defects must be taken out of service
   - Repaired by competent person
   - Re-examined before return to service

Equipment Covered:
- Cranes (all types)
- Hoists
- Lifts
- Elevating work platforms
- Vehicle inspection platform hoists
- Excavator arms with lifting attachments
- Lifting accessories (slings, shackles, chains, etc.)

Key Guidance:
- HSE L113: Safe use of lifting equipment
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/work-equipment-machinery/loler.htm

PROVISION AND USE OF WORK EQUIPMENT REGULATIONS 1998 (PUWER)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active
Purpose: Ensure work equipment is safe
Application: All work equipment in all industries

Key Requirements:

1. SUITABILITY
   - Equipment suitable for intended use
   - Suitable for conditions
   - Account taken of working environment

2. MAINTENANCE
   - Maintained in efficient state, working order, and good repair
   - Maintenance log kept

3. INSPECTION
   - Where safety depends on installation conditions: inspected after installation and before use
   - Where operating conditions could cause deterioration: inspected at suitable intervals
   - Records kept

4. RISKS
   - Specific risks from use identified and addressed
   - Measures taken to minimize risks

5. INFORMATION AND INSTRUCTIONS
   - Adequate information, instruction and training provided
   - Written instructions where appropriate

6. TRAINING
   - Workers trained in use of equipment
   - Specific training for complex or high-risk equipment

7. DANGEROUS PARTS
   - Protection from dangerous parts of machinery
   - Guards and protection devices
   - Hierarchy: fixed guards, interlocked guards, automatic guards, other protection

8. CONTROLS
   - Control systems safe
   - Start and stop controls clearly identified
   - Emergency stop controls where needed
   - Controls prevent inadvertent operation

9. ISOLATION
   - Equipment can be isolated from energy sources
   - Clear identification of isolation points

10. STABILITY
    - Equipment stabilized to prevent overturning or falling

11. LIGHTING
    - Adequate lighting for safe use

12. MAINTENANCE OPERATIONS
    - Safe to maintain, adjust, and clean

13. WARNINGS
    - Appropriate warnings and markings

14. TEMPERATURES
    - Protection from extreme temperatures

15. MOBILE WORK EQUIPMENT
    Additional requirements for:
    - Vehicles and mobile machinery
    - Ride-on equipment
    - Remote-controlled equipment
    - Roll-over protective structures (ROPS)
    - Falling object protective structures (FOPS)

Construction Equipment Covered:
- Power tools
- Hand tools
- Excavators
- Dumpers
- Concrete mixers
- Compressors
- Generators
- Cutting equipment
- All machinery

Key Guidance:
- HSE L22: Safe use of work equipment
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/work-equipment-machinery/puwer.htm

CONTROL OF SUBSTANCES HAZARDOUS TO HEALTH REGULATIONS 2002 (COSHH)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active (amended multiple times, most recently 2004)
Purpose: Protect workers from hazardous substances
Application: All workplaces where hazardous substances used

Construction Relevance:
- Cement and concrete (skin sensitization, burns)
- Silica dust (lung disease)
- Asbestos (mesothelioma, asbestosis, lung cancer)
- Solvents and paints
- Wood dust
- Diesel engine exhaust emissions
- Welding fume
- Adhesives and resins

Key Requirements:

1. ASSESSMENT
   - Risk assessment for work with hazardous substances
   - Consider health hazards and exposure routes
   - Review regularly

2. PREVENTION OR CONTROL
   Hierarchy:
   - Eliminate use of hazardous substance
   - Substitute with less hazardous substance
   - Use engineering controls (ventilation, enclosure)
   - Reduce number exposed and exposure time
   - Personal protective equipment (last resort)

3. CONTROL MEASURES
   - Local exhaust ventilation (LEV)
   - General ventilation
   - Wet cutting/damping down dust
   - Segregation
   - PPE (respiratory protection, gloves, etc.)

4. MAINTENANCE
   - Control measures maintained
   - LEV examined and tested every 14 months (records kept)

5. MONITORING
   - Exposure monitoring where appropriate
   - Health surveillance where identified in assessment

6. HEALTH SURVEILLANCE
   Required for certain substances (e.g., isocyanates, lead)
   Records kept for 40 years

7. INFORMATION, INSTRUCTION AND TRAINING
   - Information about hazards and risks
   - Training in safe working methods
   - Training in use of controls and PPE

8. EMERGENCY PROCEDURES
   - Procedures for accidents, incidents and emergencies
   - First aid provisions

Specific Construction Concerns:

RESPIRABLE CRYSTALLINE SILICA (RCS):
- Workplace Exposure Limit: 0.1 mg/m³ (8-hour TWA)
- Control measures:
  - On-tool extraction
  - Water suppression
  - RPE (FFP3 minimum)
  - Separation/enclosure
  - Health surveillance

ASBESTOS:
- Separate regulations (Control of Asbestos Regulations 2012)
- See asbestos section below

CEMENT:
- Skin sensitization risk
- Prevent contact with wet cement
- Gloves and skin protection
- Washing facilities

Key Guidance:
- HSE L5: Control of Substances Hazardous to Health
- HSE CIS36: COSHH essentials for construction
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/coshh/

CONTROL OF ASBESTOS REGULATIONS 2012
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active (amended 2022)
Purpose: Protect workers and others from asbestos exposure
Application: All non-domestic premises and common parts of domestic premises

Background:
- Asbestos banned in UK since 1999
- Still present in buildings constructed before 2000
- Major cause of work-related deaths (around 5,000 per year in UK)

Key Requirements:

1. DUTY TO MANAGE ASBESTOS (Regulation 4)
   Applies to: Duty holders (owners, occupiers, managing agents of non-domestic premises)
   Requirements:
   - Identify location and condition of asbestos or presumed asbestos
   - Assess risk from asbestos
   - Prepare plan to manage the risk
   - Implement the plan
   - Keep information up to date
   - Provide information to those likely to work on asbestos

2. ASBESTOS SURVEY
   Types:
   - Management survey: locate ACMs that could be disturbed during normal occupation
   - Refurbishment and demolition survey: locate all ACMs before work begins

   Survey Requirements:
   - Conducted by competent person
   - Documented in asbestos register
   - Reviewed regularly

3. RISK ASSESSMENT
   Before any work with asbestos:
   - Written risk assessment (plan of work)
   - Identify type of asbestos
   - Assess exposure level
   - Determine control measures needed

4. NOTIFICATION
   Notifiable Non-Licensed Work (NNLW):
   - Most asbestos work requires notification to HSE
   - 14 days before work starts

   Licensed Work:
   - Asbestos removal requiring licence from HSE
   - Higher risk work

5. TRAINING
   Mandatory training levels:
   - Asbestos Awareness: All construction workers
   - Category A training: Non-licensed asbestos work
   - Category B training: Licensed asbestos work

   Refresher: Annually or when work practices change

6. CONTROL MEASURES
   - Prevent or reduce exposure to as low as reasonably practicable
   - Hierarchy: prevent exposure > control exposure > mitigate exposure
   - Engineering controls and work methods
   - Respiratory protective equipment (RPE)
   - Protective clothing
   - Decontamination procedures
   - Waste disposal

7. AIR MONITORING
   Required for:
   - Assessment of exposure
   - Verification of controls
   - Background monitoring
   - Clearance testing

8. HEALTH SURVEILLANCE
   Required for:
   - Licensed asbestos work
   - Work likely to expose workers
   - Medical examination before work starts, then every 2 years
   - Records kept for 40 years after last entry

9. EXPOSURE LIMITS
   - Control Limit: 0.1 fibres/cm³ (4-hour time-weighted average)
   - Must not be exceeded
   - If exceeded, work must stop until controls improved

10. LICENSING
    HSE License Required For:
    - Work on asbestos insulation, coating, or insulating board
    - Specific exemptions for sporadic low intensity work

    Licensed Contractor Must:
    - Hold current HSE licence
    - Have suitable insurance
    - Notify HSE 14 days before work
    - Medical surveillance program
    - Keep records

11. WASTE
    - Asbestos waste is hazardous waste
    - Must be double-bagged or wrapped
    - Labelled appropriately
    - Consignment note required
    - Disposed at licensed facility

12. RECORDS
    - Exposure records: 40 years
    - Health surveillance records: 40 years
    - Air monitoring records: 5 years
    - Training records: Available for inspection

Types of Asbestos Work:

Licensed Work:
- Removal of asbestos insulation
- Removal of asbestos coating
- Removal of asbestos insulating board
- Requires HSE licence

Non-Licensed Work:
- Small-scale work with asbestos containing materials
- Short duration, low exposure
- Notification required (NNLW)

Exempt Work:
- Minimal exposure, sporadic and low intensity
- Examples: drilling holes, handling intact ACMs briefly

Key Guidance:
- HSE L143: Managing and working with asbestos
- HSE HSG248: Asbestos essentials task manual
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/asbestos/

CONTROL OF NOISE AT WORK REGULATIONS 2005
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active
Purpose: Protect workers' hearing
Application: All workplaces where noise exposure occurs

Exposure Action Values and Limit Values:
- Lower Exposure Action Value: 80 dB(A) daily or 135 dB(C) peak
- Upper Exposure Action Value: 85 dB(A) daily or 137 dB(C) peak
- Exposure Limit Value: 87 dB(A) daily or 140 dB(C) peak (must not be exceeded)

Key Requirements:

1. RISK ASSESSMENT
   - Assess noise exposure levels
   - Identify who is at risk
   - Decide on control measures

2. ELIMINATION OR CONTROL
   Hierarchy:
   - Eliminate noise at source
   - Reduce noise at source (quieter equipment, methods)
   - Engineering controls (enclosures, barriers, damping)
   - Distance/time controls
   - Hearing protection (last resort)

3. HEARING PROTECTION
   At 80 dB(A):
   - Make hearing protection available
   - Training and information

   At 85 dB(A):
   - Provide hearing protection
   - Establish hearing protection zones
   - Mandatory use of hearing protection

4. HEALTH SURVEILLANCE
   At 85 dB(A):
   - Health surveillance (hearing checks) required
   - Records kept

5. INFORMATION, INSTRUCTION AND TRAINING
   - Nature of risks from noise
   - Control measures and how to use them
   - How to use hearing protection
   - Health surveillance arrangements
   - Exposure action values and limit values

6. SIGNAGE
   - Hearing protection zones marked
   - Mandatory signs displayed

Construction Noise Sources:
- Pneumatic tools (90-115 dB)
- Angle grinders (95-105 dB)
- Concrete breakers (100-115 dB)
- Circular saws (95-105 dB)
- Impact wrenches (100-110 dB)
- Generators (85-100 dB)

Control Methods:
- Select quiet tools (low-noise models)
- Maintain equipment (reduces noise)
- Enclosures and barriers
- Limit exposure time (job rotation)
- Use alternative methods (hydraulic vs. pneumatic)

Key Guidance:
- HSE L108: Controlling noise at work
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/noise/

CONTROL OF VIBRATION AT WORK REGULATIONS 2005
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active
Purpose: Protect workers from hand-arm vibration syndrome (HAVS) and whole-body vibration
Application: All workplaces where vibration exposure occurs

Two Types of Vibration:

1. HAND-ARM VIBRATION (HAV)
   Causes: Hand-held power tools, hand-guided equipment
   Health Effects: HAVS (white finger), carpal tunnel syndrome, vibration-induced arthritis

   Exposure Action Value (EAV): 2.5 m/s² A(8)
   Exposure Limit Value (ELV): 5 m/s² A(8) (must not be exceeded)

2. WHOLE-BODY VIBRATION (WBV)
   Causes: Driving vehicles, mobile machinery
   Health Effects: Back pain, spinal damage

   Exposure Action Value (EAV): 0.5 m/s² A(8)
   Exposure Limit Value (ELV): 1.15 m/s² A(8) (must not be exceeded)

Key Requirements:

1. RISK ASSESSMENT
   - Assess vibration exposure
   - Identify who is at risk
   - Decide on control measures
   - Consider manufacturer's vibration data, exposure duration, working conditions

2. ELIMINATION OR CONTROL
   Hierarchy:
   - Eliminate need for vibration exposure
   - Select low-vibration equipment
   - Reduce exposure time
   - Work methods to minimize vibration
   - Maintenance of equipment
   - Information and training

3. CONTROL MEASURES (HAV)
   - Use low-vibration tools
   - Keep tools well-maintained
   - Limit trigger time
   - Job rotation
   - Keep hands warm
   - Avoid gripping tool tighter than necessary
   - Anti-vibration gloves (limited effectiveness, not primary control)

4. CONTROL MEASURES (WBV)
   - Use vehicles with good suspension and seats
   - Maintain vehicles and surfaces
   - Adjust seats properly
   - Limit driving time
   - Plan routes to minimize rough ground

5. HEALTH SURVEILLANCE
   At EAV (2.5 m/s² for HAV, 0.5 m/s² for WBV):
   - Health surveillance required
   - Look for symptoms of HAVS or back disorders
   - Records kept

6. INFORMATION, INSTRUCTION AND TRAINING
   - Health risks from vibration
   - Control measures
   - Exposure action and limit values
   - How to recognize symptoms
   - Reporting procedures

Construction Equipment Concerns:
HIGH VIBRATION (manage exposure carefully):
- Percussion tools (breakers, hammer drills, scabblers)
- Sanders and grinders
- Concrete pokers
- Chainsaws
- Jigsaws

MODERATE VIBRATION:
- Drills
- Planers
- Sanders

LOW VIBRATION:
- Power saws
- Angle grinders with speed control

Key Guidance:
- HSE L140: Hand-arm vibration
- HSE L141: Whole-body vibration
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/vibration/

PERSONAL PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT AT WORK REGULATIONS 1992 (PPE)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active (amended 2002, 2022)
Purpose: Ensure appropriate PPE provided and used
Application: All workplaces

Key Principle: PPE is the LAST RESORT after all other control measures

Key Requirements:

1. SUITABILITY
   - Appropriate for risks and conditions
   - Account taken of ergonomic requirements
   - Fits the wearer correctly
   - Compatible with other PPE if multiple items worn

2. ASSESSMENT
   - Assess PPE to ensure it is suitable
   - Consider risks, characteristics of workplace, worker requirements

3. PROVISION
   - Employer must provide PPE free of charge
   - PPE is personal issue (not shared where hygiene or fit important)

4. MAINTENANCE
   - Kept in good repair
   - Replaced when necessary
   - Cleaned and disinfected
   - Storage provided

5. ACCOMMODATION
   - Suitable storage for PPE
   - Protection from contamination, loss, or damage

6. INFORMATION, INSTRUCTION AND TRAINING
   - How to use PPE correctly
   - Why PPE needed
   - Limitations of PPE

7. USE
   - Workers must use PPE properly
   - Report defects or losses

Construction PPE:

HEAD PROTECTION:
- Hard hats (safety helmets) - EN 397
- Bump caps for low-risk areas - EN 812
- Mandatory on most construction sites

EYE AND FACE PROTECTION:
- Safety spectacles - EN 166
- Goggles
- Face shields
- Welding shields

HEARING PROTECTION:
- Ear plugs - EN 352-2
- Ear muffs - EN 352-1
- Required in designated zones

RESPIRATORY PROTECTION:
- Disposable masks (FFP1, FFP2, FFP3) - EN 149
- Half masks with replaceable filters - EN 140
- Full face masks - EN 136
- Powered respirators - EN 12941
- Selection depends on hazard and exposure level

HAND PROTECTION:
- General purpose gloves
- Cut-resistant gloves - EN 388
- Chemical-resistant gloves - EN 374
- Anti-vibration gloves - EN ISO 10819 (limited effectiveness)
- Latex/nitrile gloves for cement work

FOOT PROTECTION:
- Safety boots/shoes with toe protection - EN ISO 20345
- Steel or composite toe caps
- Penetration-resistant soles
- Slip-resistant soles
- Ankle support where appropriate

BODY PROTECTION:
- High-visibility clothing - EN ISO 20471 (mandatory near traffic/plant)
- Protective clothing for weather
- Chemical-resistant suits
- Welding aprons and gauntlets

FALL PROTECTION:
- Safety harnesses - EN 361
- Lanyards - EN 354
- Energy absorbers - EN 355
- Fall arrest systems - EN 363
- Work positioning systems - EN 358

CE/UKCA MARKING:
- All PPE must be CE marked (or UKCA from 2023)
- Category I: Simple design, minimal risks
- Category II: Neither I nor III
- Category III: Irreversible damage or fatal risks (requires type examination by notified body)

Key Guidance:
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/ppe/
- Various HSE guidance for specific PPE types

REPORTING OF INJURIES, DISEASES AND DANGEROUS OCCURRENCES REGULATIONS 2013 (RIDDOR)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Status: Active (replaced RIDDOR 1995)
Purpose: Ensure HSE/Local Authority informed of serious incidents
Application: All workplaces

Reportable Events:

1. DEATHS
   - Any work-related death
   - Report immediately (telephone then online)

2. SPECIFIED INJURIES
   Must report if worker suffers:
   - Fractures (except fingers, thumbs, toes)
   - Amputations
   - Loss of sight (permanent or temporary)
   - Crush injuries leading to internal organ damage
   - Serious burns (covering >10% body or causing damage to eyes, respiratory system, vital organs)
   - Scalping requiring hospital treatment
   - Unconsciousness caused by head injury or asphyxia
   - Injuries from electric shock or electrical burn leading to unconsciousness or requiring resuscitation or admittance to hospital for more than 24 hours

3. OVER-SEVEN-DAY INJURIES
   - Worker incapacitated for normal work for more than 7 consecutive days (not counting day of injury)
   - Report within 15 days of incident

4. INJURIES TO NON-WORKERS
   - Members of public injured and taken to hospital
   - Report if incident related to work activity

5. OCCUPATIONAL DISEASES
   Must report diagnosis of:
   - Carpal tunnel syndrome
   - Severe cramp of hand or forearm
   - Occupational dermatitis
   - Hand-arm vibration syndrome (HAVS)
   - Occupational asthma
   - Tendonitis or tenosynovitis (hand or forearm)
   - Any occupational cancer
   - Disease attributed to biological agent
   - Certain poisonings

6. DANGEROUS OCCURRENCES
   Specific incidents with potential for serious harm, including:
   - Collapse of scaffolding >5m high
   - Collapse of building or structure under construction
   - Collapse of excavation >5m deep
   - Explosion or fire causing suspension of normal work >24 hours
   - Collapse of lift or lifting equipment
   - Failure of load-bearing part of lifting equipment
   - Contact with overhead power line
   - Unintentional collapse of any building or structure under construction >5m high

Reporting Method:
- Online: www.hse.gov.uk/riddor/
- Telephone (deaths and specified injuries only): 0345 300 9923
- Records kept for 3 years minimum

Records Must Include:
- Date and time of incident
- Personal details of those involved
- Brief description of circumstances
- Nature of injury or condition

Key Guidance:
- HSE: Reporting accidents and incidents at work (INDG453)
- HSE website: www.hse.gov.uk/riddor/

OTHER RELEVANT HEALTH AND SAFETY REGULATIONS
====================================================================================================

THE HEALTH AND SAFETY AT WORK ETC. ACT 1974
Primary legislation establishing general duties:
- Section 2: Employer duties to employees
- Section 3: Employer duties to others
- Section 7: Employee duties
- Section 8: Duty not to interfere with or misuse safety provisions

MANAGEMENT OF HEALTH AND SAFETY AT WORK REGULATIONS 1999
General management requirements:
- Risk assessment (5 or more employees: must be written)
- Health and safety arrangements
- Health surveillance
- Competent assistance
- Procedures for serious and imminent danger
- Information for employees
- Cooperation and coordination (shared workplaces)
- Temporary workers information

WORKPLACE (HEALTH, SAFETY AND WELFARE) REGULATIONS 1992
General workplace requirements:
- Maintenance of workplace and equipment
- Ventilation, temperature, lighting
- Cleanliness, room dimensions
- Workstations and seating
- Floors, traffic routes
- Falls or falling objects
- Windows, doors, gates, walls
- Escalators and moving walkways
- Sanitary conveniences
- Washing facilities
- Drinking water
- Accommodation for clothing
- Facilities for rest and eating meals

MANUAL HANDLING OPERATIONS REGULATIONS 1992 (amended 2002)
Requirements for manual handling:
- Avoid hazardous manual handling where reasonably practicable
- Assess risks from manual handling operations
- Reduce risk of injury
- Provide information about loads
- Training in safe handling techniques

HEALTH AND SAFETY (FIRST-AID) REGULATIONS 1981
First aid provisions:
- Adequate and appropriate equipment and facilities
- Sufficient number of first-aiders or appointed persons
- Information provided to employees
- Construction sites typically require trained first-aider and comprehensive first aid kit

ELECTRICITY AT WORK REGULATIONS 1989
Electrical safety:
- Systems constructed to prevent danger
- Maintained to prevent danger
- Work on or near electrical equipment by competent persons
- Precautions for work on live equipment
- Protection against excess current
- Earthing and bonding
- Construction sites: 110V equipment, RCD protection

PRESSURE SYSTEMS SAFETY REGULATIONS 2000
Safety of pressure equipment:
- Competent person assessment
- Suitable protective devices
- Examination schemes
- Written scheme of examination
- Records kept

THE REGULATORY REFORM (FIRE SAFETY) ORDER 2005
Fire safety in workplaces and construction sites:
- Fire risk assessment
- Fire prevention measures
- Fire detection and warning
- Emergency routes and exits
- Fire-fighting equipment
- Training and information
- Not applicable to construction sites covered by CDM 2015 (construction phase plan covers fire safety)

====================================================================================================
ENFORCEMENT
====================================================================================================

Enforcement Authorities:
- Health and Safety Executive (HSE) - Construction sites
- Local Authorities - Some commercial premises
- Office for Nuclear Regulation (ONR) - Nuclear sites

Powers:
- Inspection and investigation
- Issue improvement notices (time to comply)
- Issue prohibition notices (immediate stop work)
- Prosecute for breaches
- Charge fees for intervention (FFI)

Penalties:
- Magistrates' Court: Fines up to £20,000, imprisonment up to 6 months
- Crown Court: Unlimited fines, imprisonment up to 2 years
- Corporate Manslaughter and Corporate Homicide Act 2007: Unlimited fines for organizations

Fee for Intervention (FFI):
- HSE charges for time spent investigating material breaches
- £163 per hour (2024/25)
- Applies where inspector identifies material breach

====================================================================================================
GAPS AND LIMITATIONS
====================================================================================================

1. COMPLEXITY
   - Multiple overlapping regulations
   - Requires professional interpretation for complex situations
   - Small businesses may struggle with compliance

2. ENFORCEMENT RESOURCES
   - HSE inspection capacity limited
   - Reliance on industry self-regulation
   - Not all sites inspected regularly

3. EMERGING RISKS
   - New technologies (e.g., drones, robotics) not fully addressed
   - Mental health and wellbeing emerging area
   - Climate change impacts (heat stress, extreme weather)

4. OCCUPATIONAL HEALTH
   - Less visible than safety issues
   - Long latency of diseases
   - Under-reporting of occupational ill health

5. SMALL SITES AND DOMESTIC WORK
   - Harder to regulate
   - Compliance variable
   - Householder projects largely exempt from CDM 2015
