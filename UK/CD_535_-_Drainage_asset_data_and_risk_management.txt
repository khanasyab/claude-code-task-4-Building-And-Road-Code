Introduction
Introduction
Background
This document defines the requirements for recording of inventory and condition of drainage assets,
and the management of flooding, pollution and cross-asset risks related to drainage.
Prior to this document, HD 43 set out the requirements for recording of drainage asset inventory
information. This was updated and supplemented by IAN 147, which set out advice for recording of
inventory and condition data for all types of drainage assets, and provided guidance for capturing this
data through surveys and digitisation of records.
This document formalises these requirements, and incorporates other drainage asset management
instructions and guidance that have been published prior to publication of this version.
National Application Annexes to this document provide further details of the scope and format of data,
and drainage asset management procedures specific to the Overseeing Organisation.
This document provides requirements and advice used to develop the design outputs required to
deliver the works in accordance with CC 500 [Ref 4.N].
Assumptions made in the preparation of the document
The assumptions made in GG 101 [Ref 6.N] apply to this document.

Terms and definitions
Terms and definitions
In addition to the terms defined in the table below, drainage asset types are defined and described in
greater detail in Appendix A. Catchments and sub-catchments are described in greater detail in
Appendix B. Activities, activity sets, asset sets and components are described in greater detail in
Appendix C.
Term
Definition
Acute pollution
Incidence of pollution that occurs suddenly.
NOTE: A spill as a result of an accidental discharge from a
vehicle is a potential cause of an acute pollution incident.
Activity
Represents an action such as a survey that was carried out
on that asset on a specific date.
Activity set
Multiple activities, possibly on different dates, on multiple
assets.
Asset condition
A measure of the current health of an asset, that indicates
its ability to perform as designed and its likelihood of failure.
NOTE: Condition is considered in terms of serviceability
(service condition) and structural integrity (structural
condition).
Asset condition survey
Field surveys that assess the condition of all drainage
assets in an area at the asset level, without detailed
recording of defects.
NOTE: Where the assets have not been previously
recorded the survey also captures the asset inventory,
connectivity and flow direction between assets.
Asset inventory information
Data and information concerning the physical, permanent
characteristics of one or more assets.
NOTE 1: Asset inventory includes details of each asset
type, their parts, information about their permanent
characteristics such as materials and dimensions, and how
the assets connect together to form a drainage system.
NOTE 2: To satisfy connectivity requirements, some
records in the asset inventory can comprise network
modelling items.
Asset inventory survey
Quick field surveys that either identify the location of priority
drainage assets or confirm the validity of existing inventory
records.
NOTE: The surveys are selective and are unlikely to survey
100% of the assets in an area.
Asset set
Continuous assets that work together to provide the
functionality of a combined asset.
Basin
A depression in the ground that temporarily detains storm
water flows.
NOTE: The water can be directed to a surface water outfall,
or it can percolate through to groundwater.

Terms and definitions
(continued)
Chronic pollution
Incidence of pollution that occurs over a long period of time,
not from an individual incident.
NOTE: Long-term run-off from the carriageway can cause
chronic pollution.
Component
Components model the change in dimension, shape,
material or lining along a continuous asset.
Continuous asset
Asset such as a pipe, ditch or channel that is represented
as a linear feature in GIS.
NOTE: The GIS geometry represents the asset's
centre-line, and does not reflect its width.
Detailed defect survey
Detailed field surveys to record all condition defects in the
surveyed drainage assets.
NOTE 1: For pipework and chambers, this involves internal
CCTV survey. For non-pipework assets, it involves detailed
survey, possibly with selective excavation of some buried
assets.
NOTE 2: Where the asset inventory has not previously
been recorded, or has not been recorded in detail, then the
detailed inventory is also captured.
NOTE 3: The survey can focus on particular assets in an
area of known performance problems, or can survey all the
drainage assets in the area, in which case the survey also
records how the assets connect together and flow
directions.
Diffuse pollution
Incidence of pollution involving pollutants from several
sources, over a long period of time, rather than from an
individual incident.
NOTE: Individually, the pollutants involved can have no
significant impact on water quality, but at a catchment scale
and/or in combination can become significant.
Drainage asset
An individual item within a drainage system, such as a gully,
pipe, ditch or pond.
Drainage data management system
(DDMS)
Overseeing Organisation's master database for managing
information concerning drainage assets and flooding.
Drainage catchment
A group of all the drainage systems and the adjacent land
they drain, between two geographical high points of the
highway network.
NOTE 1: Both carriageways of a dual carriageway are
considered part of the same catchment, but risks can be
assessed separately for each carriageway (see drainage
sub-catchment).
NOTE 2: A drainage catchment can include one or more
drainage systems.
Drainage sub-catchment
The separate carriageways of a drainage catchment, used
primarily for flood risk assessment.
NOTE: Sub-catchments can contain multiple systems.

Terms and definitions
(continued)
Drainage surveys
Detailed field investigations that are drainage specific, are
mainly carried out on foot, generally by drainage survey
specialists in accordance with CS 551 [Ref 5.N].
Drainage system
A group of drainage assets that are directly or indirectly
connected to each other, within a single drainage
catchment.
NOTE 1: Each individual drainage asset is part of only one
drainage system.
NOTE 2:Systems can cross sub-catchments within the
same catchment.
Flooding
The accumulation or passage of water at the ground surface
where it is not normally experienced.
Flood
A single incidence of flooding on or within 200 m of a
carriageway or within 500 m of a drainage asset, at a
particular location and time.
Flooding hotspot
An arbitrary extent of carriageway at risk of repeating
flooding
Mitigation
An action or procedure that is designed and implemented to
reduce a risk to an acceptable level.
NOTE 1: A mitigation does not necessarily eliminate a risk
entirely.
NOTE 2: Acceptability criteria for the level of residual risk
can be defined on a case-by-case basis.
Network modelling item
An asset inventory record that takes the place of one or
more physical point asset or continuous asset records to
satisfy drainage network connectivity requirements.
NOTE: Example usages are where there is either no
physical asset, where the detailed arrangement of the
physical assets are not yet known, or where flow direction is
generalised in a region asset. Further details and examples
are given in Appendix A.
Point asset
An asset such as a chamber that is represented as a point
in GIS.
NOTE 1: The dimensions or physical shape of the asset are
not represented in the GIS geometry, but only as data
attributes.
NOTE 2: Some assets of a linear physical shape such as
weirs are represented as point assets because the overall
flow direction is across their width, rather than along their
length.

Terms and definitions
(continued)
Pollution
The presence or introduction of a substance that can have
an adverse impact on the environment, over a short or long
period, regardless of cause and whether this is accidental or
deliberate.
NOTE: Pollution can affect environmental factors such as
air quality, noise, water quality, visual impacts, etc; only
water quality impacts are within the scope of this document.
Pollution incident
An incidence of pollution at a specific location, either on an
acute or chronic basis.
Pond
A depression in the ground with a permanent water body.
Priority asset register
Flooding or pollution risk assessment information for all
priority assets, recorded in the DDMS in addition to the
asset's inventory and condition information.
Priority asset
Drainage assets that present a risk of pollution or flooding
i.e. all outfalls, soakaways and culverts.
Region asset
Asset such as a pond or basin that is represented as a
closed polygon in GIS.
NOTE: The GIS geometry reflects its physical boundary.
Repeated flooding
The occurrence of at least two floods within a rolling
five-year period, on the same sub-catchment.
Service condition
The performance of an asset and severity of the defects that
affect its serviceability, independent of its structural
condition.
NOTE: Service defects are addressed by maintenance of
the asset such as cleansing or vegetation clearance.
Spill
A single spillage on, or within 200 m of, the carriageway, at
a particular location and time.
NOTE: A spill does not necessarily result in the occurrence
of a pollution incident.
Structural condition
The condition of the fabric of the asset and the severity of
defects that affect its integrity.
NOTE: Structural defects are addressed by repairing or
replacing the asset.
Sub-catchment flood risk
The assessed flood risk for each and every sub-catchment.
Watershed
The high point between two drainage catchments. The
drainage systems on either side flow in opposite directions.

2. General requirements
2.
General requirements
When to do drainage surveys
2.1
Drainage surveys shall be undertaken to collect asset inventory and condition data to address
knowledge deficiencies.
2.1.1
Asset data to fill knowledge deficiencies may be required for the following:
1)
risk management;
2)
scheme development;
3)
condition inspections; or,
4)
reactive or proactive asset maintenance.
Risk management
2.2
Drainage surveys shall be undertaken to collect asset inventory and condition data to fill data gaps or
verify inventory information when managing flooding, pollution and cross asset risks.
2.2.1
Data gap and verification surveys to assist with risk management may include undertaking the following
in accordance with CS 551 [Ref 5.N]:
1)
validation surveys to verify inventory information held on the Drainage Data Management System
(DDMS);
2)
priority asset surveys to locate the outfall or soakaway of each drainage catchment and any
culverts; and,
3)
all assets condition and connectivity surveys to determine the inventory, asset level condition and
connectivity of all drainage assets to better understand the reasons for flooding or cross-asset
impacts and determine locations to focus resources where more detailed surveys are needed.
Scheme development
2.3
Drainage surveys shall be undertaken to collect asset inventory and condition data to systematically
develop drainage schemes, and wider schemes that have a drainage element, in accordance with the
Overseeing Organisations standard business processes.
2.3.1
The Overseeing Organisation's standard business processes for scheme development may include the
following stages during which a drainage survey is undertaken:
1)
feasibility study;
2)
preliminary design;
3)
detailed design; and
4)
pre-construction.
2.4
A post-construction survey shall be undertaken following completion of a scheme to:
1)
capture as-built inventory and condition data;
2)
update the Overseeing Organisations DDMS; and,
3)
confirm that design and construction contractual requirements have been met.
2.5
A post-construction survey shall be undertaken following completion of the drainage works and
following completion of works to other assets that could damage the newly installed or renewed
drainage.
NOTE 1
This is to ensure that the as-built inventory and condition data collected is a true reflection at handover
into operation and maintenance.
NOTE 2
If construction work continues following the completion of the drainage works, damage can occur to
new or renewed drainage assets due to overrun by vehicles and plant.

2. General requirements
Condition inspections
2.6
Knowledge of the condition of all drainage assets shall be updated on a cyclical basis in accordance
with GS 801 [Ref 1.N].
2.6.1
Condition inspections of above ground drainage assets may be satisfied by undertaking either:
1)
a drainage inspection in accordance with GS 801 [Ref 1.N]; or,
2)
a drainage survey in accordance with CS 551 [Ref 5.N].
2.6.2
Condition inspections of below-ground drainage assets and specialist drainage equipment (such as
pumps and valves) need specialist survey equipment and drainage asset knowledge, and should be
carried out as a drainage survey in accordance with CS 551 [Ref 5.N].
Reactive or proactive maintenance
2.7
As part of reactive or proactive asset maintenance in accordance with GM 701 [Ref 2.N], drainage
surveys shall be undertaken to collect asset inventory and condition data .
2.7.1
Reactive and proactive asset maintenance may include:
1)
post-incident investigations, of:
a)
flooding;
b)
pollution (spills); or,
c)
cross-asset impacts.
2)
intelligence lead maintenance, including such items as:
a)
works planning informed by instrumentation monitoring;
b)
silt level monitoring to plan optimum timing and location of gully cleaning; or,
c)
pre-works contamination sampling and testing to assess spoil disposal requirements.
3)
a find and fix approach that combines identification and immediate remediation of minor defects.

3. Asset inventory data
3.
Asset inventory data
3.1
All drainage assets shall be recorded in the Overseeing Organisation's drainage data management

5. Managing flood risk
5.
Managing flood risk
General requirements
5.1
The drainage asset shall be managed to minimise the current and future predicted impacts of flooding
on the network's assets, users, workers and third parties.
5.2
In addition to the drainage asset inventory and condition data, registers of culverts, floods and one of
either flooding hotspots or sub-catchment flood risk shall be maintained.
NOTE
Assessment and mitigation procedures, risk assessment criteria, and information recording
requirements for culverts, floods, flooding hotspots and sub-catchment flood risk are given in the
National Application Annexes.
Recording flood data
5.3
Information and data about flooding shall be recorded in the Overseeing Organisation's DDMS for
flooding data.
NOTE
Overseeing Organisation-specific information on DDMS including data dictionary, format and validation
requirements is provided in the National Application Annexes.
5.4
Where flooding is potentially related to a culvert, the flooding risk due to the culvert shall be assessed
and recorded in the DDMS priority asset register for culverts.
Floods
5.5
As soon as flooding is notified, actions shall be commenced to mitigate any impacts.
5.5.1
The flooding and arising mitigation actions should be logged within the operational management
system within 24 hours.
5.6
The flooding shall be assessed to determine whether it is a reportable flood.
NOTE
Overseeing Organisation-specific definitions of a reportable flood are provided in the National
Application Annexes.
5.7
All floods reported to the Overseeing Organisation or its agents, that are either on the road network or
within 200 metres of it, or within 500 m of a drainage asset, shall be entered into the DDMS, within 7
days of the report.
NOTE 1
Flooding can be reported by members of the public, emergency services, Overseeing Organisation
personnel or their agents.
NOTE 2
Where a reported flood is further than 200 metres from the network and 500 m from a drainage asset, it
is not necessary to enter this into the DDMS.
5.8
Within the initial 7-day period of the flood being reported, the following information shall be recorded in
the DDMS:
1)
location of the approximate centre of the flood using the national grid and transformation
according to the requirements of the Overseeing Organisation;
2)
date and time the flood was initially reported; and,
3)
whether the flood is affecting the carriageway and/or adjacent third party property.
5.8.1
Where additional information about the flood is confirmed within the initial 7-day period, this should be
entered into the DDMS.
NOTE 1
Overseeing Organisation-specific requirements on flood location are provided in the National
Application Annexes.
NOTE 2
Not all information regarding a flood is likely to be known within 7 days, such as causes and
identification of further required actions.

6. Managing risks to water quality
6.
Managing risks to water quality
General requirements
6.1
The drainage asset must be managed in accordance with the European Community Directive
2000/60/EC [Ref 3.N] to:
1)
prevent the deterioration of the status of all types of surface water and groundwater bodies; and,
2)
support the protection, enhancement and restoration of all bodies of surface water and
groundwater.
NOTE 1
The legislation which translates this directive into law is given in the National Application Annexes of LA
113 [Ref 7.N].
NOTE 2
Pollution of water bodies can occur either due to:
1)
short-term incidents at a specific location that cause acute pollution, such as accidental spills
from vehicles; or,
2)
carriageway run-off over a longer period of time or wider geographical area, causing chronic or
diffuse pollution.
Outfalls and soakaways
6.2
In addition to the drainage asset inventory and condition data, registers of outfalls and soakaways shall
be maintained.
NOTE
Assessment and mitigation procedures, risk assessment criteria, and information recording
requirements for soakaways and outfalls are given in the National Application Annexes.
Recording spillage data
6.3
All spills on the highway network shall be recorded on the DDMS with the following information, within
24 hours of the spill's occurrence:
1)
location of the approximate centre of the spill using the national grid and transformation according
to the requirements of the Overseeing Organisation;
2)
date and time the spill was initially reported; and,
3)
whether the spill is affecting the carriageway and/or adjacent third party property.
NOTE
Overseeing Organisation-specific requirements on spill location are provided in the National Application

7. Managing cross-asset risks
7.
Managing cross-asset risks
7.1
A cross-asset approach shall be taken to managing the risks caused by, and to, the drainage asset.
7.2
When determining the overall level of risk that a drainage asset causes, the drainage-related condition
of associated non-drainage assets shall be assessed, in addition to the condition of the drainage asset
itself.
7.2.1
Best practice advice in CIRIA C714 [Ref 1.I] should be followed when implementing processes for
managing the cross-asset risks related to drainage.
NOTE 1
As an example of assessment using non-drainage assets, the presence of hydrophilic vegetation on an
earthwork can indicate underperforming or insufficient drainage, even if the condition of the drainage
asset itself is good.
NOTE 2
Inadequate carriageway drainage resulting in slow removal of water from the pavement can lead to
premature failure of the pavement surface or formation layers, as well as the risks associated with
flooding.
7.3
Where improvement or remedial works are required on non-drainage assets, and these are partly or
wholly as a result of poor condition or insufficient drainage, these works shall include measures to
address the future cross-asset risk due to the drainage assets.
7.4
Works carried out to non-drainage assets shall be designed and implemented such that they do not
cause an adverse impact to the drainage asset.
NOTE
Potential adverse impacts to drainage assets due to work on other assets can include, for example:
1)
physical damage to drainage assets, for example, as a result of excavations or installation of
other assets such as VRS;
2)
changes to run-off characteristics such that existing drainage is unsuitable or of insufficient
capacity; or,
3)
release of foreign materials into the drainage system, above normal background amounts, which
might result in blockages, reduced capacity or risks to water quality.
7.5
Works carried out to drainage assets shall be designed and implemented such that they do not cause
an adverse impact on other assets.

Asset example usages
A2.1
Point assets - inlets and outlets
A2.1.1
Outfalls, inlets and outlets
Figure A.1 shows an example usage of an inlet, outlet and outfall. A ditch flowing into a culvert, to pass
underneath a road, would have an inlet at the upstream end of the culvert. As the culvert emerges from
beneath the road into another ditch, it would have an outlet. The ditch discharges into a third party river
and in this case does not have a physical structure. The ownership boundary (and the limit of the
Overseeing Organisation's responsibility) coincides with the river bank and this defines the outfall
location.
Figure A.1 Example use of inlets, outlets and outfalls
Points to note:
1)
If the ownership boundary had been midway between the carriageway and the river, then the
outfall would be at that location, not where the ditch physically discharges into the river.
2)
The outfall has the potential to pollute third party waters and like all outfalls is included within the
outfall register.
3)
If the connection between the culvert and the river had been piped, instead of a ditch, then the
outfall would also have been an outlet. Outlets are only priority assets where they are also
outfalls, in which case they would be included within the outfall register.
4)
Indicative highway ownership boundaries, and hence potential outfall locations, may be found in
mapping data sets available at www.gov.uk. Definitive ownership boundary information is
available from HM Land Registry www.gov.uk/government/organisations/land-registry
5)
Where the ownership boundary is along a watercourse, under riparian law, the boundary would
be the centreline of the watercourse channel, although the discharge point into the third party
waters would be on the river bank.

or gravity drain. The downstream end of the pipe connects part way along the ditch, and therefore
has a connector node that references the ditch. (See example use of connector nodes, below.)

A2.2
Drainage network modelling items
A2.2.1
Ghost nodes
A ghost node is a network modelling point item used to complete a drainage network by representing a
node that has no physical manifestation. The ghost node allows connectivity between assets to be
defined, by satisfying the requirement that all continuous assets have an upstream and downstream
point.
Example uses of ghost nodes include:
1)
the ends of ditches or other continuous assets where there is no physical point asset;
2)
at the connection between two surface assets such as a ditch and a pond, where there is no
physical asset such as a flow control device; and,
3)
at the connections between sections of a herringbone drain, where there is no physical asset.
A2.2.2
Connector nodes
A connector node is a network modelling point item representing an incoming intermediate connection
to a continuous asset. A connector node is located at the downstream end of the incoming asset.
Because it is at an intermediate location along the outgoing continuous asset, it is neither the upstream
nor downstream point asset of this continuous asset. The connector node has a connectivity reference
to associate it to the continuous asset that is downstream of it.
The connector node would normally be geographically located on its downstream continuous asset, but
need not be in all cases. Similarly, point assets where the path of connection is unknown (e.g. an
unknown gully connector) or informal (e.g. a grip inlet with no formal downstream drainage) can refer to
a downstream continuous asset, into which they would connect at an intermediate point.
Figure A.3 shows an example use of connector nodes. The example given above for grip inlets, also
shows example uses.

two known point assets in which the route of the continuous connection between them is unknown, but

control device point asset. Region connectors join the region node to each point where a continuous
asset enters or leaves the region asset.

E/1. General requirements
E/1.
General requirements
When to do drainage surveys
E/1.1
Drainage surveys shall be undertaken to collect asset inventory and condition data to systematically
develop drainage schemes, and wider schemes that have a drainage element, in accordance with the
following business processes:
1)
Operations: Develop, Design and Deliver scheme process (3D);
2)
Major Projects: Project Control Framework (PCF); and,
3)
Designated Funds: Environment and Wellbeing Designated Fund (EWDF).
E/1.2
Drainage surveys shall be undertaken to update the asset inventory and condition in the Geotechnical
and Drainage Management Service (GDMS) after completion of a scheme at the following stages of
each business process:
1)
3D process in Stage 5 Construction;
2)
PCF process in Stage 6 Handover; and,
3)
EWDF process in Stage 4 Implementation.
E/1.2.1
Drainage surveys may additionally be undertaken at the following stages of each business process:
1)
3D process:
a)
Stage 1 Options Assessment - to gather additional data to support option development;
b)
Stage 2 Preliminary Design - to gather additional data to progress a preliminary design of
the preferred option; and,
c)
Stage 3 Detailed Design - to fill any outstanding data gaps required to complete the
detailed design.
2)
PCF process:
a)
Stage 3 Preliminary design - to develop the preliminary design and assess the condition of
existing assets; and,
b)
Stage 5 Construction preparation - dilapidation survey to record asset condition
immediately prior to the start of construction.
3)
EWDF process:
a)
Stage 2 Feasibility - to inform options and develop preliminary designs; and,
b)
Stage 3 Detailed design - to progress the preferred option into a detailed design.
Roles and responsibilities
E/1.3
Each operational area shall nominate a suitably qualified and experienced person or persons to carry
out each of the following functions:
1)
drainage liaison engineer responsible for all matters related to the management of the drainage
asset; and,
2)
flood champion responsible for all matters related to the management of flooding risk.
NOTE
"Suitably qualified and experienced" means a minimum of EngTech or equivalent, and a minimum of
five years' experience of managing highway drainage assets.
E/1.3.1
The functions of the drainage liaison engineer and flood champion may be carried out by persons with
alternative role titles.
E/1.3.2
The functions of the drainage liaison engineer and flood champion may be carried out by the same
person.
E/1.4
Those carrying out the functions of drainage liaison engineer and/or flood champion shall be subject to
acceptance by National Highways, if not employed by National Highways.

E/1. General requirements
Performance measures and metrics
E/1.5
Information shall be recorded on the GDMS to enable National Highways to accurately report against
the performance metrics defined in the Operational Metrics Manual. OMM [Ref 7.N]
E/1.6
Flood and flood risk data shall be recorded on the GDMS to facilitate the monthly calculation of the
drainage performance indicator, the drainage resilience metric defined in the Operations Metrics
Manual. OMM [Ref 7.N]
E/1.7
All floods recorded on the GDMS shall be assessed against the rainfall that occurred at the time and
location of the flood, to ascertain if the flood occurred during an extreme weather event that exceeded
the drainage design criteria for that location.
E/1.8
Where a flood is found to have occurred during an extreme rainfall event, it shall be removed from the
drainage resilience metric calculation.
NOTE 1
The metrics defined in the Operational Metrics Manual OMM [Ref 7.N] are reviewed periodically and
subject to change.
NOTE 2
National Highways defines additional drainage and flooding performance measures and metrics for
internal reporting and monitoring purposes.
E/1.9
Where further information is required to satisfy the metric assurance process, a response to a request
for this information shall be provided within seven days.
NOTE

work that has taken place, or of the active management processes implemented, in order for the
mitigation to be assured and hence counted in the metric.

NOTE 1
Factors such as whether the live carriageway is affected, the duration of impact, the safety impact or
whether any other National Highways assets are being damaged do not come into the definition of a
flood. Such matters are considered in the assessment of the impact when reporting a flood on the
GDMS. The flood is a "near miss" if the live carriageway is not impacted, but it is still recorded as a
flood on the GDMS.
NOTE 2
Where the sub-catchment risk status is C or D the sub-catchment is not a priority for mitigation works,
however, opportunities for mitigation are to be considered in association with other works being carried
out in the sub-catchment.
E/3.10
The following design requirements shall be taken as defining a flood:
1)
any standing or flowing water that extends beyond the CD 526 [Ref 10.N] requirements of:
a)
1.5m from the kerb for the hard shoulder; or,
b)
1.0m from the kerb for the hard strip;
2)
any standing water at pedestrian, cycle or equestrian crossings contrary to the requirements of
CD 526 [Ref 10.N];
3)

E/3.10.1
The following may be used to distinguish between a flood and a puddle:
1)
water depth estimated to be more than 30 mm; or
2)
one or more wheel tracks affected irrespective of water depth; or
3)
third party land or property affected; and,
4)
flood area estimated to be 10 m2 or more.

Photographic examples of what is and what is not defined as a flood are given in Appendix E/A.
E/3.11
Flooding that is located on or within 200 m of the carriageway or within 500 m of a drainage asset shall
be recorded on the GDMS as a flood.
E/3.12
The location of all floods shall be recorded to OSGB36 datum, using the OSTN15 transformation model.
NOTE
Where the location of the flood is recorded as a relative location, such as a marker post chainage, this
is in addition to the location as OS coordinates.
E/3.13
The severity of floods shall be quantified in accordance with the flood severity index (FSI) definition,
which can be downloaded from the GDMS.
NOTE 1
The GDMS automatically calculates the flood severity index from the information entered for each flood.
NOTE 2
A flood receives an FSI score even if the carriageway is not affected.
NOTE 3
A flood receives an FSI score even if the duration of impact is less than 15 minutes.
NOTE 4
The only way that a flood can receive an FSI of 0 is if it cannot be snapped to the Linear Network Model
on the GDMS as it is more than 200 m from the carriageway and more than 500 m from a drainage
asset, and is therefore not classed as a flood.
E/3.14
The nature of any impacts of a flood on third parties or other assets shall be assessed and recorded on
the GDMS.
E/3.15
The flood and arising mitigation actions shall be logged within the operational management system
within 24 hours as detailed in the ADMM [Ref 1.N].
E/3.16
All floods shall be entered into the GDMS within 7 days as detailed in the ADMM [Ref 1.N].
E/3.17
Flood records shall be completed, reviewed and set to closed status on the GDMS within 28 days of the
flood's occurrence as detailed in the ADMM [Ref 1.N].
