Introduction
Introduction
Background
This document contains design requirements for traffic signalling systems installations.
Assumptions made in the preparation of the document
The assumptions made in GG 101 [Ref 14.N] apply to this document.
It is assumed that the parties reading TD 101 are aware of additional documents to those within the
DMRB. The Department for Transport (DfT) prepares general advice, some of which is applicable to
motorway and all purpose trunk roads. Of these documents, the most relevant are Local Transport
Notes (LTN), and Traffic Advisory Leaflets (TAL), which mostly cover topics that apply to local rather
than strategic roads, but some are of general relevance.
It is assumed that a traffic signalling engineer will be part of the review process throughout all stages of
traffic signalling systems design.
Mutual Recognition
Where there is a requirement in this document for compliance with any part of a "British Standard" or
other technical specification, that requirement may be met by compliance with the Mutual Recognition
clause in GG 101 [Ref 14.N].

Terms and definitions
Terms and definitions
Term
Definition
Adjacent authorities
Any organisation which has responsibility for the control
and management of the highway.
NOTE: Adjacent authorities can be either borough,
county or city governance. It can also include major
transport infrastructure locations that have a direct link
to the highway such as airports, ports, rail and freight.
Asset identification label
The label affixed to traffic signalling systems equipment
and infrastructure to support the identification of different
assets, for example through the use of a unique
equipment reference.
Bar code label
The label affixed to traffic signalling systems equipment
for the purposes of identifying, recording and managing
assets using an asset management system.
Control strategy
A system used to control the operation of traffic
signalling system.
NOTE 1: Control strategy includes isolated adaptive
control, co-ordinated control (such as urban traffic
control), cableless linking or fixed-time.
NOTE 2: A traffic signalling system installation can be
capable of operating more than one type, depending on
factors such as time of day or traffic conditions.
Countdown timers
Light signals prescribed by diagram 4002.1A in The
Traffic Signs Regulations and General Directions UKSI
2016/362 [Ref 29.N], and defined as a "unit indicating to
pedestrians the time remaining to cross the road".
Design file
A file of information and certification for a scheme,
produced during the design process to provide a record
of the development of the scheme, the decisions made
and the safety considerations.
Farside light signals
Light signals viewed from across the road, prescribed by
diagrams 4002.1, 4003.2 and 4003.5 in The Traffic
Signs Regulations and General Directions UKSI
2016/362 [Ref 29.N].
Full pedestrian stage
All pedestrian phases of a controlled crossing or junction
run at the same time while traffic is held on a red signal.
Hardstanding maintenance working area
A hard-surfaced area of sufficient size and construction
to provide a safe working area for maintenance
purposes.

Terms and definitions

Term
Definition
Ingress protection (IP)
Defined in BS EN 60529 [Ref 4.N] as "degree of
protection: the extent of protection provided by an
enclosure against access to hazardous parts, against
ingress of solid foreign objects and/or against ingress of
water and verified by standardised test methods".
Inspection and test plan (ITP)
Document detailing the inspection activities and tests to
be undertaken on an item of equipment or system to
demonstrate that it functions in accordance with the
relevant specifications and requirements. Prepared in
accordance with BS ISO 10005 [Ref 3.I].
In-station equipment
The computer hardware located within a control room.
Low level cycle signals
Light signals prescribed by diagram 3000.2A in The
Traffic Signs Regulations and General Directions UKSI
2016/362 [Ref 29.N].
Maintenance access point
A suitable and safe location where authorised workers
can park a vehicle. This includes a maintenance
hardstanding or a parking facility off-network.
Maintenance hardstanding
An area where authorised workers can stop adjacent to
the carriageway (as defined in DMRB CD 169 [Ref
27.N]).
Microprocessor optimised vehicle
actuated (MOVA)
A control strategy based on minimising stops and delays
which maximises capacity at a single controlled junction.
Nearside light signals and wait indicator
equipment
Items of traffic signalling systems equipment for use by
pedestrians, equestrian users and cyclists.
Partnering organisations
Organisations and stakeholders with responsibilities or
interests in traffic signalling systems.
NOTE: Examples include local authorities, emergency
services, public transport and freight groups.
Practical reserve capacity (PRC)
The available unused capacity of a traffic signal junction.
NOTE 1: The practical reserve capacity that is positive
indicates that a junction has spare capacity and may be
able to accept more traffic.
NOTE 2: A PRC value is calculated by the commonly
used traffic signal modelling software.
Roadside equipment
All on-street hardware to direct traffic and provide
sequencing to the road users.

Term
Definition
Secret regulatory signs
Signs defined in TOPAS 2543 [Ref 2.I] as "regulatory
signs prescribed in TSRGD or specially authorised by
the Secretary of State that may be placed in a signal
head housing located according to the diagrams of
Schedule 14 part 3 of TSRGD and which are internally
illuminated and which signs permitted to be used in a
part time operational mode (so called secret signs)".
Site conditions
Physical conditions of the proposed area of the schemes
to be considered as part of the design stage.
System software
Software that controls a computer's hardware and
provides a platform for other software.
NOTE: An example is an operating system.
Telecommunications service provider
(TSP)
The TSP provides a 'service capability' for conveying
data signals that link traffic signalling systems
installations to in-station equipment.
Traffic signal controller
Traffic control equipment and the associated cabinet.
Traffic signal head
An item of traffic signalling systems equipment as
defined in BS EN 12368 [Ref 32.N].
Traffic signalling engineer
A person with specialist skills, knowledge and
experience for the design and commissioning of traffic
signalling equipment.
Traffic signalling systems equipment
All items of hardware installations that operate together
and indicate to users to proceed with safety at road
traffic junctions against relevant diagrams of The Traffic
Signs Regulations and General Directions 2016 UKSI
2016/362 [Ref 29.N], and associated amendments, for
England, Wales and Scotland, and the Traffic Signs
Regulations (Northern Ireland) NISR 1997/386 (TSRNI)
[Ref 43.N]for Northern Ireland.

Terms and definitions
(continued)
Term
Definition
Traffic signalling systems installations
Permanent traffic signalling equipment at prescribed
locations as defined in The Traffic Signs Regulations
and General Directions UKSI 2016/362 [Ref 29.N], and
associated amendments, for England, Wales and
Scotland on all-purpose trunk roads, and the Traffic
Signs Regulations (Northern Ireland), NISR 1997/386
(TSRNI) [Ref 43.N] for Northern Ireland. For standalone
crossings in Northern Ireland, such as 'Pelicans' and
'Puffins', the relevant legislation is The Zebra, Pelican
and Puffin Pedestrian Crossings Regulations (Northern
Ireland) 2006 NISR 2006/164 2006 [Ref 30.N].
NOTE: Examples of locations include junctions,
emergency vehicle stations, level crossings, airports,
tunnels, swing/movable bridges and signalised
crossings.
Traffic signalling systems interfacing
equipment
Equipment used in connection with the signals (including
the content of all instructions stored in, or executable by,
that equipment).
NOTE: Examples include bus priority systems, variable
message signs (VMS) and urban traffic control (UTC)
interfaces.
Traffic signalling systems
The combination of traffic signalling equipment and/or
traffic control equipment, associated software, hardware
and data.
TSP interface
The interface between the TSP and the traffic signalling
system.
NOTE: This can include the TSP's service delivery point
or the ducted network interface; for example a
designated interface chamber.
Wig-wag signals
Light signals prescribed by diagram 3014 in The Traffic
Signs Regulations and General Directions UKSI
2016/362 [Ref 29.N], and defined as "light signals for the
control of road traffic at level crossings, swing or lifting
bridges, airfields or in the vicinity of premises used
regularly by fire and rescue authority, Scottish Fire and
Rescue Service, police or ambulance vehicles".

4.4

1)
mounting height;
2)
horizontal angle from kerb face, edge of carriageway or edge of tactile paving; and
3)
high user group densities.
NOTE 2

verified on site during the SAT.
NOTE 3
High level repeater nearside light signals can be used to mitigate the impact of reduced visibility due to
high user group densities.
NOTE 4
Narrow field of view on nearside light signals can be used to reduce the risk relating to road users
confusing green nearside light signals at a different waiting area.
4.5
Nearside light signals and wait indicator equipment shall be provided at all islands incorporating waiting
