Introduction
Introduction
Background
This document provides the requirements for the specialist activity of surveying highway drainage
systems by a number of different methods. The selection of the most appropriate survey method is
detailed in CD 535 [Ref 8.N].
SD 15/03, and accompanying parts, set out a specification, notes for guidance and a method of
measurement, for surveying drainage pipework using closed-circuit television (CCTV). Since its
publication in 2003 the Overseeing Organisations have developed national drainage data management
systems. In order to expedite the population of these systems with drainage asset inventory and
condition information, new survey methods have been researched and developed that are both quicker
and lower cost than CCTV survey, and cover more drainage asset types than just pipework. Some of
these drainage survey methods were introduced in IAN 147/12 with supporting guidance notes
providing further detail on their application. HD 217/17 introduced the use of ground penetrating radar
(GPR) for surveying the condition of combined surface and sub-surface filter drains. The requirements
for these various drainage survey methods are now brought together and updated in this one
document, superseding the previous publications.
National Application Annexes to this document provide further details on the requirements for drainage
surveys specific to the Overseeing Organisation.
This document provides requirements and advice used to develop the design outputs required to
deliver the works in accordance with CC 500 [Ref 7.N].
Assumptions made in the preparation of this document
The general requirements in GG 101 [Ref 12.N] apply to this document.
Mutual recognition
Where there is a requirement in this document for compliance with any part of a "British Standard" or
other technical specification, that requirement may be met by compliance with the Mutual Recognition
clause in GG 101 [Ref 12.N].

Terms and definitions
Term
Definition
All assets condition and connectivity survey
A survey carried out to determine the inventory,
asset level condition and connectivity of all
drainage assets in a drainage system.
NOTE: Also known as connectivity survey.
All assets defect survey
A survey carried out to determine the inventory,
condition and detailed defects of pipework and the
associated chambers and gullies and the
inventory, asset level condition and connectivity of
all other drainage assets in a drainage system.
Axis of drain
A longitudinal line projected centrally along the
inside of the drain (pipework or culvert).
Back-office data operative
The person responsible for analysing and assuring
the quality of the survey data in the back-office.
Blockage
An obstruction within the drainage system.
Chamber
Access point that can be a manhole, catchpit or
other structure as defined in CD 535 [Ref 8.N].
Chambers defect and geometric survey by
laser scanner
A survey carried out to determine the inventory,
condition, detailed defects and internal geometry
of chambers, including the use of a laser scanner
with inbuilt panoramic still camera(s) and a
closed-circuit television (CCTV) camera.
Confined space
Any place, including a chamber, trench, pipework,
culvert or similar space where, by reason of its
nature, there is a foreseeable risk of asphyxiation,
loss of consciousness due to increased body heat,
drowning, or serious injury from fire or explosion.
Continuous defect
A longitudinal pipework defect.
Data round-tripping
The process carried out in an area of existing data
that ensures one-version-of-the-truth of the
drainage inventory and condition data is
maintained on the DDMS, by downloading the
available data before a survey commences, and
then checking, updating or adding to it in the field,
before uploading it back to the DDMS, including
any unchanged data, replacing the previous
version of the data on the DDMS.
Ditch profile survey
A survey carried out to determine the longitudinal
and cross-sectional profile of ditches in association
with an all assets condition and connectivity
survey and including the determination of potential
soil contamination.

Term
Definition
Drainage asset
An individual item within a drainage system, such
as a gully, pipe, ditch or pond.
Drainage data management system
Overseeing Organisation's master database for
managing information concerning drainage assets
and flooding.
Drainage highway catchment
A group of all the drainage systems and the
adjacent land they drain, between two
geographical high points of the highway network.
NOTE 1: Both carriageways of a dual carriageway
are considered part of the same catchment, but
risks can be assessed separately for each
carriageway.
NOTE 2: A drainage highway catchment can
include one or more drainage systems.
Drainage system
A group of drainage assets that are directly or
indirectly connected to each other, within a single
drainage highway catchment.
NOTE: Each individual drainage asset is part of
only one drainage system.
Filter drain condition survey by ground
penetrating radar
A survey carried out of the service condition due to
sediment contamination of the filter medium of
combined surface and sub-surface drains, and
other forms of filter drain, including the use of
ground penetrating radar (GPR).
Forced ventilation
The procedure of venting the drainage system by
removing a number of chamber covers and using
ducting, air jets and fans to force air into the
system to purge potentially dangerous gases that
have accumulated within the system.
Ghost node
Connects adjacent continuous assets where there
is no physical asset.
Jetting operative
The person responsible for carrying out jetting of
drainage assets on the site.
Laboratory operative
The person responsible for analysing the soil
samples and interpreting the soil sample results in
the testing laboratory.
Node
Manhole, catchpit, inspection chamber, outlet,
gully, rodding eye or any other point from which
the drainage survey commences or terminates.
Pan-and-tilt camera
A self-propelled camera with a directionally
adjustable lens.
Phantom node
Upstream or downstream end point of a known
drainage system indicating the limit of knowledge.

Pre-cleansing
The removal of silt and debris prior to the

Priority asset
An outfall or soakaway that presents a risk of

Priority asset survey
A survey carried out in areas where the highway
drainage system is unknown or uncertain, in order
to locate the outfall or soakaway point of each
drainage catchment, and any culverts passing
under the carriageway, to facilitate management of
pollution and flooding risk.
Remote survey
The survey of drainage systems by means that
preclude man-entry into the drainage assets.
Rodding camera
A CCTV camera that is moved along the pipework
using flexible rods.
NOTE: Such cameras are generally used to
access 150 mm or smaller diameter pipes,
particularly those with bends.
Service condition
The performance of an asset and severity of the
defects that affect its serviceability, independent of
its structural condition.
NOTE: Service defects are addressed by
maintenance of the asset such as cleansing or
vegetation clearance.
Set-up

space to carry out work for any purpose unless it is not reasonably practicable to achieve that purpose
without such entry.

3.

Survey requirements
Scope of survey
3.1
A validation survey shall be carried out to verify whether the inventory information held on the drainage
data management system (DDMS), previously derived from drawings, is a true record of the visible
surface point assets or not.
3.2
A validation survey shall include the following:
1)
a walkover survey to verify surface visible point assets only;
2)
determination of whether the recorded location and asset type of the surface visible point assets
is correct or not; and,
3)
submission of digital data in the format defined by the Overseeing Organisations in the NAAs to
CD 535 [Ref 8.N]
NOTE 1
A validation survey is:
1)
a quick walkover survey;
2)
used to assess drainage data that has been derived from drawings, not previous field surveys;
3)
carried out in the daytime; and
4)
only carried out where it is safe to access the verge, hard shoulder or footpath without lane
closures, unless sharing other traffic management arrangements.
NOTE 2
A validation survey does not:
1)
primarily record previously unrecorded inventory, although any such point assets found during the
course of the survey are to be captured;
2)
record asset condition; nor
3)
require any prior vegetation clearance.

2)

3)
retaining as part of the data, without change, any inventory and condition information on any
non-surveyed assets; and,
4)
submission for uploading of this information to the DDMS.

4.
Priority asset survey
Survey requirements
Scope of survey
4.1
A priority asset survey shall be carried out in areas where the highway drainage system is unknown or
uncertain, in order to locate the outfall or soakaway of each drainage catchment, and any culverts
passing under the carriageway, to facilitate management of pollution and flooding risk.
4.2
A priority asset survey shall include the following:
1)
a desk study to identify potential priority asset locations for field study;
2)
a walkover survey of the targeted locations;
3)
recording of the location, inventory and asset level condition of each identified priority asset; and,
4)
submission of digital data in the format defined by the Overseeing Organisations in the NAAs to
CD 535 [Ref 8.N].
NOTE 1
A priority asset survey is:
1)
carried out where there is little or no drainage inventory on the DDMS;
2)
a "quick win" to expedite the acquisition of knowledge of the key drainage assets that can present
a risk of either pollution (outfalls and soakaways) or flooding (culverts);
3)
a combination of a desk study and a walkover survey;
4)
carried out in the daytime; and,
5)
carried out only where it is safe to access the verge, footpath, adjacent earthworks or adjacent
land without lane closures, unless sharing other traffic management arrangements.
NOTE 2
A priority asset survey does not:
1)
record the drainage assets that the priority assets connect to; nor,
2)
require any prior vegetation clearance.
Data round-tripping
4.3
Survey data shall be round-tripped from the DDMS where available.
4.4
Where there is some drainage inventory information in the location to be surveyed on the DDMS, the
survey shall round-trip this data in accordance with the specific procedures and formats detailed by the
Overseeing Organisations in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N] by:
1)
downloading the available information;
2)
confirming or updating the inventory and condition of the priority assets in the field;
3)
retaining as part of the data, without change, any inventory and condition information on any
non-surveyed assets; and,
4)
submission for uploading of this information to the DDMS.
4.5
Where there is no existing drainage inventory information in the location to be surveyed on the DDMS,
the survey shall consist of the following:
1)
a first time survey of the inventory and condition of the priority drainage assets only; and,
2)
the submission for upload of this information to the DDMS in accordance with specific procedures
and formats detailed by the Overseeing Organisations in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N].

5.

Survey requirements
Scope of survey
5.1
A filter drain condition survey shall be carried out to determine the service condition due to sediment
contamination of the filter medium of combined surface and sub-surface drains, and other forms of filter
drain, including the use of ground penetrating radar (GPR).
5.2
A filter drain condition survey by GPR shall include the following:
1)
details of the location, date, equipment and personnel of the survey;
2)
GPR survey of the required filter drains;
3)
survey of the location and type of all associated nodes at each end of each filter drain to the
accuracy stated in CD 535 [Ref 8.N] for a condition survey, and to the national survey grid stated
by the Overseeing Organisations in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N];
4)
interpretation and recording of the variation in void ratio of the filter medium along the length of
the filter drain as an indication of the degree to which the filter medium has become contaminated
with sediment;
5)
recording of the overall asset level service and structural condition grades of the filter medium;
6)
preparation of a report on recommended remedial works or further investigations; and,
7)
submission of a digital report and digital data sets in the formats defined by the Overseeing
Organisations in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N].
NOTE
A filter drain condition survey by GPR is;
1)
a field survey along the surface centre line of a filter drain;
2)
carried out using a vehicle-mounted GPR; and,
3)
carried out either remote from the carriageway or at the edge of the carriageway and could
require traffic management.
Data round-tripping
5.3
Survey data shall be round-tripped from the DDMS where available.
5.4
Where there is some drainage inventory information in the location to be surveyed on the DDMS, the
survey shall round-trip this data in accordance with specific procedures and formats detailed by the
Overseeing Organisations in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N] by:
1)
downloading the available information;
2)
confirming or updating the inventory, condition and defects of the filter drain medium and the
inventory of the associated nodes in the field;
3)
retaining as part of the data, without change, any inventory and condition information on any
non-surveyed assets; and,
4)
submission for uploading of this information to the DDMS.
5.5
Where there is no drainage inventory information in the location to be surveyed on the DDMS the
survey shall consist of:
1)
a first time survey of the inventory, condition and defects of the filter drain medium and the
inventory of the associated nodes; and,
2)
the submission for upload of this information to the DDMS in accordance with specific procedures
and formats detailed by the Overseeing Organisations in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N].
NOTE

provided in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N].

5.9

5.11

1)
a combination of a desk study and a quick walkover survey;

Scope of survey
7.1
A pipework and chambers defect survey shall be carried out to determine the inventory, condition and

7.2
A pipework and chambers defect survey by CCTV shall include the following:
1)
details of the location, date, equipment and personnel of the survey;
2)
details of any pre-cleansing, removal of blockages, root cutting or other associated works carried
out;
3)
survey of the required pipework and/or culverts;
4)
survey of all chambers and gullies connected to the required pipework and/or culverts and
associated interconnections;
5)
survey of the location of all chambers and gullies and the levels of chamber covers, gully
gratings, chamber inverts, gully inverts and the inverts of pipework connections;
6)
identification of the asset type of all surveyed assets in accordance with the classification system
detailed in CD 535 [Ref 8.N];
7)
recording of all the mandatory and optional data fields for asset inventory, structural condition
defects, service condition defects, and survey parameters using the codes detailed by the
Overseeing Organisations in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N] for all pipework and/or culverts,
chambers and gullies surveyed;
8)
recording of an overall asset level structural and service condition grade;
9)
recording of an in-pipe video showing the nature of the pipework and the defects recorded;
10)
taking of still photographs from the in-pipe video showing the nature of typical pipework and
specific defects recorded;
11)
taking of still photographs showing the typical nature and specific defects of the chambers and
gullies surveyed; and,
12)
submission of a digital report and digital data sets in the formats defined by the Overseeing
Organisations in the NAAs to CD 535 [Ref 8.N] and as detailed in the reporting requirements
below.

1)
a field survey carried out where full and detailed knowledge of the condition and defects of the
below ground pipework is needed;
2)

pipework; or,
c)

repair, replacement or redesign of the drainage system is being investigated; or

1)

2)

angular measurement range of the inclinometer;
3)
corrugated steel, twin-wall or other forms of pipe where the invert is not smooth; nor,
4)
pipework with sediment in the invert.

1)

2)

planning of future cleaning or re-profiling works;
3)

3)

4)
at all significant changes in vertical gradient;
5)
at all significant changes in plan alignment; and,
6)
at intermediate points such that the spacing between profiles is not more than 50 m.

Appendix A. Drainage condition grade quick assessment
Appendix A. Drainage condition grade quick assessment
A1
Introduction
This procedure provides a quick method of assessing the overall condition grade of highway drainage
assets without undertaking a detailed defect-level survey. It can be used for most drainage asset types,
both above and below ground, and is intended for use with the following:
1)
all assets condition and connectivity survey;
2)
priority asset survey;
3)
filter drain condition assessment by GPR (structural condition only); and,
4)
any other form of quick asset survey or inspection where a measure of current condition is
required.
A2
Structural and service condition
Each drainage asset is visually assessed for both structural and service condition and a whole number
grade from 1 to 5 is assigned to each. In certain cases a grade of 9 or 0 may be assigned. The grades
are defined in CD 535 [Ref 8.N].
Where there are several defects of different types or severity in an asset, the most severe defect
determines the structural or service grade.
Where the inspection of an asset has been attempted, but it was not possible to assess the condition
(for example due to the asset being buried or otherwise obscured), a grade of 9 is assigned. Where it
has only been possible to inspect part of an asset, the grades reflect those parts that have been seen.
Where the unseen part could not be inspected because, for example, the pipe was blocked or the ditch
was severely overgrown with vegetation, then these defects are taken into account in assessing the
overall asset grades.
A grade of 0 is equivalent to no grade, indicating that assessment of the asset's condition has not been
attempted, for example because recording of that asset's condition is irrelevant (such as for ghost
nodes), or because the previously recorded asset could not be found in the field.
A3
Asset grouping
For the purposes of the quick assessment procedure, the various drainage asset types are grouped
together into assets of similar construction and function, as shown in Table A.1 below. The grouping
generally follows that of CD 535 [Ref 8.N], but with the following additional subdivisions:
1)
chambers are split into chambers and gullies;
2)
ditches and channels are split into four depending on whether their construction is hard or soft
and open or enclosed;
3)
inlets and outlets are assessed using different criteria, depending on whether there is a formal
structure, such as a headwall, or not; and,
4)
the asset type formerly known as combined surface and sub-surface filter drain is now an asset
set consisting of a filter pipe and filter drain for which the condition is assessed and reported
separately.
The ancillaries group is a mixture of various asset types, some of which may occur either as a
standalone asset, or as an attribute of another asset, for example a flow control device within a
chamber or inlet/outlet. Where it occurs as part of another structure, then the condition grades relate to
the total structure, including the ancillary item. Pumping stations are a special case and require
specialist mechanical and electrical inspections that are beyond the scope of the quick assessment
method, which is limited to a high level assessment of whether there is deterioration of the structure
and whether the pumping station is performing (pumping water).

Appendix B. Cleaning of drainage assets
B1
Introduction
This Appendix covers the cleaning of drainage assets.
Cleaning of drainage assets arises from the following:
1)
pre-cleansing to improve the collection of asset inventory and condition data during a drainage
survey;
2)
asset maintenance; and,
3)
refurbishment of existing drainage as part of scheme construction.
Where cleaning is required, the works are to be detailed in:
1)
the task order for the drainage survey;
2)
the task order for the maintenance activity; or,
3)
the specification for the scheme required by CC 500 [Ref 7.N].
Drainage asset types are defined in CD 535 [Ref 8.N]
Cleaning of drainage assets is to be in accordance GM 701 [Ref 2.N]and this Appendix.
Any damage or defect found during cleaning of drainage assets that presents a safety hazard is to be
immediately reported to the Overseeing Organisation.
The cleaning of drainage assets is not to result in the contamination of adjacent watercourses or
groundwater, see Sections B5 and B7.
All cleaning activities are to be recorded in the Overseeing Organisation's maintenance management
record system.
B2
Equipment
A gully sucker is to have a minimum of:
1)
a 125 mm diameter gulley arm with boom jets;
2)
an exhauster with a minimum output of 5.95 m³/min;
3)
a minimum 5455 litres capacity; and,
4)
a suction facility capable of displacing 55 m³/min of air at 95% vacuum.
A road sweeper is to have:
1)
a dual sweeper function; and,
2)
under mounted water jets for dust suppression.
A road sweeper may be a combination unit with a gully sucker.
A combination suction and jetting tanker is to be capable of:
1)
operating at a maximum rate of 850 l/min at 150/2250 bar/psi;
2)
have a minimum of 20 m of 100 mm diameter suction hose for cleaning pipes up to 300 mm
diameter and 150 mm diameter suction hose for cleaning pipes of diameter greater than 300 mm;
3)
have a minimum of 150 m of 25 mm or 38 mm diameter jetting hose and a capability of jetting up
to 200 m; and,
4)
be capable of removing silt and fibrous roots;
5)
be able to carry silt and debris over 10 m horizontally and 10 m vertically; and,
6)
automatic and continuous water recycling.

Cleaning is to take place from downstream of the blockage in an upstream direction.
The cleaning is to take place from a chamber, although it may be necessary to make a temporary
excavation into the pipeline in some circumstances.
If it is not possible to jet in an upstream direction, then the pipe is to be plugged below the blockage and
the jetter then used to "back jet" to a suction hose.

assessed and appropriate mitigation put in place.

10% for pipe diameters less than 600 mm, and less than 5% for pipe diameters 600 mm and above.
Up to three passes per drainage run are to be completed to clean the pipework. Following this, if the
pipework is not considered clean, the location is to be marked on site using a semi-permanent means
and reported to the Overseeing Organisation for consideration of remedial works to remove the

B4.4
Health and safety
The use of pressurised water for jetting may result in serious internal injuries that may not be apparent
at the skin surface. High protection Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) is to be used by all jetting
operatives in accordance with the Water Jetting Association Code of Practice WJA 2015 [Ref 4.N].
The Contractor is to ensure that all hoses are free from damage and that the equipment is in full
working order.
Where overhead electric cables are present, there is a potential danger from accidental strike by a jet
of water. Mitigation in the form of a jetting tent may be required in these instances.
All jetting activities are to be undertaken by a two-operative team and a safe working zone set up
around the jetting equipment.
The Contractor is to develop site specific risk assessments and method statements to be approved by
the Overseeing Organisation prior to the works.
B5
Silt and debris removal
When cleaning drainage assets measures should be taken to ensure that the adjacent water courses or
groundwater via soakaways, will not be contaminated. Contamination includes mud or soil being
washed or flushed into streams as well as other more obvious contaminants including diesel fuel, oil
and chemicals.
This will be achieved by:
1)
plugging the outlet below the jetting point or in the downstream drainage system using stanks or
stoppers during cleaning; and,
2)
use of a gully sucker; or,
3)
use of a combination suction and jetting tanker.
Equipment is to be able to carry silt and debris over the horizontal and vertical distances specified in
section B2. Distances in excess of these may be required on a site by site basis, this is to be detailed in
the relevant task order or specification listed in B1 and agreed with the Overseeing Organisation in
advance of the work being undertaken on site.
All surplus water, debris, silt and arisings removed by the cleaning operation is to be disposed of in
accordance with section B.7.
Silt, sand and rubble is to be de-watered prior to discharge at a licensed tip.

B6
Vegetation clearance
Suitable equipment is to be available to clear inlets and outlets, locate and clear covers, and remove
light vegetation, to enable access to drainage assets and minimise survey abandonments.
Clearance is to be undertaken by a dedicated two-operative team with valid tickets for the equipment to
be used.
The extent of clearance is to be agreed with the Overseeing Organisation in advance of the works.
More significant clearance may require a specialist operation.
B7
Spoil disposal
All spoil arising from the cleaning, maintenance or refurbishment of the drainage asset is to be
characterised prior to the works to determine whether the spoil be treated as hazardous waste or
non-hazardous spoil for the purposes of disposal. In some limited circumstances where the material is
demonstrated to be non-hazardous spoil it is possible to deposit it on site; in all other cases the material
is hazardous waste to be disposed of off-site.
B7.1
Drainage spoil waste types
Wastes that are always hazardous are known as 'absolute hazardous' (AH). Wastes that are always
non-hazardous are known as 'absolute non-hazardous' (AN). Wastes that may be hazardous or
non-hazardous are known as mirror hazardous (MH) or mirror non-hazardous (MN).
Depending on its origin, drainage spoil can be initially categorised as shown in Table B.3. The waste
codes, descriptions and types in Table B.3 are taken from the Technical Guidance WM3: Waste
Classification - Guidance on the classification and assessment of waste TG WM3 [Ref 18.N].

B7.2
Testing and characterisation of drainage spoil
For spoil which is absolute hazardous (AH) in Table B.3, it is not necessary to determine what
chemicals are in the spoil in order to classify it as hazardous or not. However, the spoil is to be
assessed to determine which hazardous properties it displays in order to complete a hazardous waste
consignment note and determine a suitable means of off-site disposal (see hazardous drainage spoil
disposal section below).
For spoil which may or may not be hazardous, pre-works testing is to be carried out in accordance with
the soil characterisation, sampling and testing procedures of this standard.
There is to be no presumption that any drainage maintenance spoil is non-hazardous without pre-works
testing and characterisation.
The soil characterisation, sampling and testing procedure is likely to assign one of the 'mirror' waste
codes listed in Table B.3, i.e. MH or MN.
B7.3
Hazardous drainage spoil disposal
All spoil that has been identified as hazardous (either AH or MH) is to be disposed of off-site in
accordance with current legislation and regulations. A consignment note is to accompany the waste.
The note identifies and lists all relevant hazardous substances in the waste, their concentration and
hazardous properties. Further detail on these requirements is given in the TG WM3 [Ref 18.N] and in
the appropriate National Environment Agency guidance on hazardous waste disposal referenced in the
National Application Annex.
Where disposal off-site is necessary, Waste Acceptance Criteria (WAC) testing (see EPG [Ref 10.N]) is
to be carried out to determine the method of off-site disposal. WAC testing is not (and cannot be) used
to determine whether the waste is hazardous or not. WAC testing determines whether waste is
acceptable for different types of landfill.
Evidence of the Contractor's waste carrier license is to be provided to the Overseeing Organisation,
plus copies of any waste consents required for the specific task.
B7.4
Non-hazardous drainage spoil disposal
Where pre-testing of drainage spoil has determined that the spoil is non-hazardous (MN) and
characterised as either waste code 17 05 06 (non-hazardous dredging spoil) or waste code 17 05 04
(non-hazardous soil and stones), then an application may be made to the appropriate National
Environment Agency for an exemption for on-site disposal. Further information on the exemption
process (where relevant) is given in the National Application Annex.

Appendix E/A. Cleaning of drainage assets, spoil disposal
Appendix E/A. Cleaning of drainage assets, spoil disposal
E/A1
Hazardous drainage spoil disposal (additional to CS 551, Appendix B, clause
B7.3)
Further details on completing a consignment note to accompany hazardous waste is given in
Hazardous waste: consignment note guidance HW(CNG) [Ref 5.N].
It can be possible to use a 'summary consignment note' for diffuse network infrastructure such as public
roads. The conditions for this are set out in 'Hazardous waste: consignment note guidance' HW(CNG)
[Ref 5.N]under diffuse network infrastructure.
E/A2
Non-hazardous drainage spoil disposal (additional to CS 551, Appendix B,
clause B7.4)
Where pre-testing of drainage spoil has determined that the spoil is non-hazardous (MN) and
characterised as waste code 17 05 06 (non-hazardous dredging spoil), but may include plant matter,
then an Environment Agency (EA) D1 exemption (see D1 Waste Exemption [Ref 2.N]) may be
registered on the EA website. A D1 exemption is valid for three years and may be registered against a
linear section of the highway network.
The drainage works are to be carried out as follows to satisfy the terms of a D1 exemption:
1)
the spoil is to be deposited at the closest possible point to the location of the dredging either:
a)
on the banks of the ditch from which it was dredged; or,
b)
on a width of land adjoining (immediately next to) the ditch that allows the removal and
deposition of the spoil by mechanical means in one operation;
2)
the spoil is not to be treated, other than by screening or by removing water;
3)
any excess water is allowed to drain away;
4)
the spoil does not include any large items, litter or fly-tipped waste, which is to be screened out if
present and sent to an exempt or permitted waste facility; and,
5)
the volume of spoil is not to exceed 50 cubic metres for each metre length of land it is deposited
on, over any 12 month period.
Where pre-testing of drainage spoil has determined that it will be non-hazardous (MN) and
characterised as waste code 17 05 04 (non-hazardous soil and stones) then an EA U1 exemption (see
U1 Waste Exemption [Ref 8.N]) may be registered on the EA website. A U1 exemption is for the use of
waste in construction so may only be of use in limited circumstances. If a U1 exemption cannot be
applied then, at the least, it should be possible to dispose of the spoil off-site as non-hazardous waste.
