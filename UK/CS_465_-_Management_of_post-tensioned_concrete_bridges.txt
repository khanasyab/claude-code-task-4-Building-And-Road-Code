Introduction
Introduction
Background
Generally post-tensioned concrete bridges have given good service and few have had significant
problems. However, tendons can be vulnerable to corrosion and severe deterioration where internal
grouting of tendon ducts is incomplete and moist air, water and contaminants can enter the ducting
system.
Experience has shown that construction practices and a lack of maintenance are of the greatest
significance in affecting deterioration of post-tensioned concrete bridges. For most forms of concrete
bridge construction, defects tend to be relatively easy to interpret and result in ductile modes of failure.
Safety critical defects in post-tensioned concrete are typically hidden, very difficult to detect and may
result in a brittle mode of failure. The primary problem with post-tensioned bridges which sets them
apart from other types, is the difficulty in establishing the internal condition of the tendons because
external signs of distress are not generally expected to occur.
For this reason the most vulnerable bridges need monitoring and reinspection after an appropriate
interval, since the level of uncertainty about the condition of the post-tensioning system increases with
time. Visual inspections alone may not give warning of imminent collapse; on the other hand, intrusive
investigations can be expensive and potentially damaging for the structure and should only be carried
out if there is a clear need.
This document details a process of risk review, risk assessment and risk management for
post-tensioned concrete bridges to provide assurance on the safety of the stock of those bridges.
Assumptions made in the preparation of this document
The assumptions made in GG 101 [Ref 5.N] apply to this document.

Terms and definitions
Terms and definitions
Terms
Term
Definition
Corrosion test area
A panel typically 2m x 1m to which corrosion testing is undertaken on
a grid basis.
Critical point
Any location where a post-tensioning system intersects a critical
section.
Critical section
A section at high risk from ingress of moist air, water and
contaminants, initiating corrosion of the post-tensioning system.
NOTE: Critical sections can include regions where voids could form
preferentially in ducts or where yield points could form a collapse
mechanism, or both.
Duct and tendon exposure
The physical process of removing concrete to expose part of a
post-tensioning duct and subsequently removing grout to expose the
tendon.
End anchorage exposure
The physical process of removing concrete, capping material or end
cap to expose part of a post-tensioning end anchorage.
General inspection
A visual inspection of all parts of the structure which can be inspected
without the need for special access equipment or extensive traffic
management arrangements.
Hazard score
A score applied to each hazard identified in the risk assessment
based on the individual hazard risk level.
Load assessment
Determination of a structure's load carrying capacity.
Mean hazard risk
level score
A score for prioritising structures based on all of the hazard risk levels
identified in the risk assessment.
Overseeing Organisation
Highways England and the highways or roads authorities of the
Devolved Administrations of Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland
and their successors.
Principal inspection
A close examination, within touching distance, of all inspectable parts
of a structure.
PTSI site inspection
A visual inspection of a post-tensioned concrete bridge in accordance
with this document.
PTSI site investigation
A detailed investigation of a post-tensioned concrete bridge involving:
1) external examination;
2) materials testing;
3) non destructive testing; and
4) internal examination in accordance with this document.
Risk assessment
The identification of hazards, risk events, likelihood, consequence
and risk level in accordance with this document.

1. Scope
1.
Scope
Aspects covered
1.1
This document shall be used for the management of all post-tensioned concrete bridges and includes
the processes of:
1)
risk review;
2)
risk assessment; and,
3)
risk management.
NOTE
The scope includes bridges, any parts of which have been constructed using post-tensioning
techniques in which tendons (bars, wires, single strands or multi-strands) are tensioned so as to apply
a compressive force to precast concrete or hardened cast in-situ concrete elements. The tendons can
be in ungrouted, grouted or greased internal or external ducts, or otherwise protected (e.g. concrete or
mortar surround), or unprotected. The tendons can be in longitudinal, transverse, secondary, vertical,
inclined, tied-down or tied members.
Implementation
1.2
This document shall be implemented forthwith on all schemes involving the management of
post-tensioned concrete bridges on the Overseeing Organisations' motorway and all-purpose trunk
roads according to the implementation requirements of GG 101 [Ref 5.N].
Use of GG 101
1.3
The requirements contained in GG 101 [Ref 5.N] shall be followed in respect of activities covered by
this document.

2. Management processes
2.3
Additional risk reviews shall be carried out following any changes to the structural form or loading, or
when defects related to the post-tensioned elements have been identified.
2.3.1
A risk review may be carried out before the next scheduled principal inspection with the agreement of
the Overseeing Organisation.
2.4
A risk assessment shall be undertaken using the information identified in the risk review.
2.5
Updates to the risk assessment shall be carried out when recommended by the risk review and agreed
with the Overseeing Organisation.
2.6
Each post-tensioned concrete bridge shall have an individual risk management plan, resulting in a set
of bridge specific risk management measures or the recommendation to continue with the routine
inspection and maintenance regime.
2.7
Records produced from the management process shall be uploaded to the relevant asset management
system in accordance with the requirements in CG 302 [Ref 1.N].
Personnel
2.8
The person responsible for overseeing the risk review, risk assessment and risk management process
and the post-tensioned special inspection (PTSI) shall have specialist experience of post-tensioned
bridge design, construction and management.
2.9
The person responsible for overseeing the risk review, risk assessment and risk management process
and the post-tensioned special inspection (PTSI) shall be agreed with the Overseeing Organisation's
Technical Approval Authority.

3. Risk review
3.
Risk review
General
3.1
The risk review process is a desk study and shall include a review of historical records, identification of
critical sections and calculation of critical points.
3.2
The outcome of the risk review shall be to recommend one of the following:
1)
updating the most recent risk assessment and risk management plan;
2)
completing a new risk assessment and risk management plan;
3)
stating that no updates are required to the most recent risk assessment and risk management
plan.
3.3
The risk review shall determine the need for a PTSI site inspection.
3.4
A risk review report shall be prepared for each post-tensioned concrete bridge.
3.5
The risk review report shall be submitted to the Overseeing Organisation.
Document review
3.6
When a risk review report is available, it shall be examined together with:
1)
the most recent risk assessment and risk management report; and,
2)
all documents listed under clause 3.7 which have been issued subsequent to the most recent risk
review.
3.6.1
In examining the most recent risk assessment and risk management report, the following should be
assessed:
1)
time elapsed since it was prepared;
2)
whether the hazards previously identified are still relevant;
3)
whether any known hazards are missing from the risk assessment; and,
4)
whether recommended risk management measures have been actioned.
3.7
The following documents shall be examined when a previous risk review report is not available:
1)
as-built drawings and construction records;
2)
PTSI reports;
3)
special inspection (SI) reports relevant to post-tensioned elements;
4)
the most recent principal inspection (PI) and general inspection (GI) reports;
5)
monitoring reports relevant to post-tensioned elements;
6)
load assessment reports;
7)
reports and records in accordance with CS 470 [Ref 7.N];
8)
maintenance records.
3.7.1
In examining the available as-built details and construction and maintenance records, the following
should be included in the risk review:
1)
year of construction;
2)
structural form;
3)
type and location of post-tensioning system(s);
4)
degree of redundancy;
5)
structural dimensions;
6)
mode of articulation;

elements at the time of stressing. These details can sometimes provide information on the expected
permeability, durability and relative performance of the various elements of a bridge.

5. PTSI site inspection
5.
PTSI site inspection
General
5.1
The objectives of a PTSI site inspection shall be to:
1)
verify the type of bridge construction and any variations, form of articulation, geometry of the
sections and the type and locations of all construction joints;
2)
identify any post-tensioned elements showing signs of distress and/or deterioration, particularly at
and adjacent to critical sections as identified in the risk review; and,
3)
identify any access constraints at critical sections.
5.1.1
The inspection should be undertaken from within touching distance of the post-tensioned elements.
NOTE 1
It is important that the inspection objectives can be achieved without unnecessary duplication of the
close range inspection that can follow in the PTSI site investigation, particularly where the cost of the
necessary access and traffic management is high.
NOTE 2
For efficiency, the inspection can be undertaken with the principal inspection, to make use of planned
access arrangements.
5.2
A PTSI site inspection report shall be prepared following the inspection.
Site inspection
5.3
The inspection shall identify defects relevant to the post-tensioning system(s) including:
1)
cracking in post-tensioned concrete elements;
2)
delamination and spalling of concrete;
3)
visual evidence of reinforcement corrosion;
4)
deterioration of construction joints;
5)
deterioration of expansion and structural joints;
6)
deterioration of the water management systems;
7)
deterioration of the deck surfacing;
8)
excessive deflection;
9)
deterioration of bearings.
5.4
When defects are observed they shall be classified according to the Inspection Manual for Highway
Structures IMHS V1 [Ref 5.I].
5.5
The location, severity and extent of all defects shall be recorded.
5.5.1
When cracking to post-tensioned elements or water leakage through the deck have been identified,
their possible cause(s) should be determined and taken into account in the risk assessment or PTSI
site investigation.
NOTE 1
For advice on undertaking inspections and identifying defects refer to CS 450 [Ref 4.N] and the
Inspection Manual for Highway Structures IMHS V1 [Ref 5.I].
NOTE 2
Advice on diagnosing cracking can be found in CS TR22 [Ref 6.I], CS TG02 [Ref 3.I] and the Inspection
Manual for Highway Structures IMHS V1 [Ref 5.I].
NOTE 3
Guidance on the detection and management of hidden defects, with case studies for post-tensioned
concrete bridges, can be found in CIRIA C764 [Ref 4.I].
5.6
Where areas of deterioration are discovered that require immediate action to maintain public safety,
then the procedures in CS 470 [Ref 7.N] shall be followed.

7. Monitoring
7.
Monitoring
General
7.1
This section shall be read in conjunction with the requirements and guidance on monitoring interim
measures in CS 470 [Ref 7.N].
NOTE 1
Monitoring can form an important part of risk management strategies for keeping deteriorating
post-tensioned concrete bridges in service by detecting any ongoing changes in structural behaviour
and deterioration.
NOTE 2
Monitoring systems can only detect deterioration from the time of installation.
NOTE 3
For high risk structures there can be a case for installing monitoring systems as part of a long term
management strategy, even if there is currently no evidence of deterioration.
7.1.1
Monitoring of post-tensioned concrete bridges may include various methods such as:
1)
acoustic emission;
2)
natural frequency;
3)
strain monitoring; and,
4)
deflection.
NOTE 1
Guidance on monitoring of post-tensioned bridges, including acoustic emission, can be found in CS
464 [Ref 8.N] and the Inspection Manual for Highway Structures IMHS V1 [Ref 5.I].
NOTE 2
Natural frequency depends on stiffness and mass distribution. Since the structural mass does not
normally change significantly, monitoring natural frequency can provide an effective method for
indirectly monitoring stiffness. Measurement of natural frequency can also be a very effective way of
determining the force in free lengths of external tendons and can detect very small changes in this.
NOTE 3
Recommendations on methods and devices which can be used to determine strain in concrete are
given in BS 1881-206 [Ref 13.N].
NOTE 4
Analysis of segmental structures typically shows that joints open at approximately 50% of the ultimate
load. Strain monitoring can therefore offer the possibility of detecting loss of prestress in segmental
structures long before it becomes critical. In a segmental structure with unbonded tendons, the first
joints to open can be identified by simple structural analysis and instrumentation can be confined to
these locations. However, in segmental structures with bonded tendons (or even external tendons
encased in mortar), broken tendons can re-anchor over short distances which could result in tendon
failure being missed if it is concentrated in a joint other than the one being instrumented.
NOTE 5
Deflection due to loss of stiffness can be very small right up to the point of failure since loss of stiffness
due to deterioration can be very localised.
NOTE 6
Deflection is more sensitive to loss of prestress in structures with unbonded post-tensioning, since loss
of prestress cannot be confined to a small length. If deflection monitoring is to be used in this situation
it is important to use a conservative high estimate of the degree of bond achieved in the actual
structure, rather than the conservative no bond assumption used in assessment.
7.1.2
Deflection measurement for monitoring grouted or partially grouted post-tensioned structures should
not be used.
7.2
In order to fully understand the possible failure modes of the structure, a structural analysis shall be
undertaken prior to designing the monitoring system.
7.3
Monitoring systems shall be tested and calibrated on installation, with monitoring trigger levels set.
7.3.1
Monitoring systems should have built in redundancy and be designed for maintenance to cater for
future damage and deterioration of their components.
7.4
A programme for monitoring and interpretation of the results shall be prepared in order to provide
advance warning of significant changes in structural behaviour.

8.
Repair and strengthening
General
8.1

Repair
8.2
Concrete repairs shall be undertaken in accordance with CS 462 [Ref 11.N] and BS EN 1504 [All parts]
[Ref 9.N].
NOTE 1
Guidance on the repair of concrete structures with reference to CS 462 [Ref 11.N] and the BS EN 1504
[All parts] [Ref 9.N] can be found in CS TR69 [Ref 7.I].
NOTE 2
The 'preflex' method can be used to repair damage resulting from impact or blast. In most cases,
accidental damage is caused by over-height vehicles striking deck soffits. This type of damage is likely
to be localised and amenable to repairs, providing no damage has occurred to the stressing wires. The
'preflex' method works by preloading the damaged member prior to repairing, placing the area of repair
under increased tension. Once the repair is completed and has achieved sufficient strength, the preload
is removed, inducing a compressive stress in the material. This can help the repair material to work
effectively with the existing concrete and improve future performance of the structure under live loading.
NOTE 3
Void grouting can be used to protect exposed tendons from corrosion and to assist re-anchoring if
fracture were to occur. Small, dry, isolated voids pose less of a durability problem than large voids
where tendons are exposed to air, moisture and contaminants, leading to corrosion. Ungrouted tendons
are effectively unbonded which can result in a lower assessed capacity for that element, if it was not
designed as such. Guidance on void grouting can be found in CS TR72 [Ref 1.I] and CS 462 [Ref 11.N].
8.3
Materials used for concrete repairs shall be in accordance with with BS EN 1504-3 [Ref 10.N].

Appendix B. Prioritisation
B1
General
Groups of structures can be prioritised for the purposes of implementing their risk management plans.
Various methods of prioritisation can be used with agreement of the Overseeing Organisation. It is
important that the primary focus of the risk assessment is on the risk management plan. Prioritisation
scores should not be used in isolation or solely relied upon for identifying high risk structures.
B2
Risk rating
The risk rating is one method which can be used for the prioritisation of post-tensioned concrete
bridges. The risk rating can be determined by combining the year of construction, bridge form,
vulnerable details and materials, condition and consequence factors (refer to Section 4 - risk
assessment and risk management). The risk rating is expressed as a percentage using equation B.1.

Appendix C. Example risk assessment hazards and prioritisation
C1
Introduction
The example hazards provided in this appendix are given primarily to illustrate application of the risk
assessment and prioritisation processes. The format is not intended to be prescriptive and assessors
may supplement the content to suit particular cases.
The year of construction and structural form should be reviewed and discussed in the risk assessment
report, prior to the assessment of hazards. These factors are important since they will affect the risk
level of each identified hazard.
C2
Example hazards
The example hazards provided below do not relate to a particular structure.
C2.1
Vulnerable details and material hazards
C2.1.1
Tendons located close to the upper surface of the deck
Risk event - Water and salts penetrate the deck waterproofing and concrete resulting in corrosion of the
reinforcement and post-tensioning system, and deterioration of concrete.
Likelihood - High. The original deck waterproofing system is still in use (44 years old). During the latest
principal inspection cracking to the carriageway surfacing above the intermediate supports was
observed, which could be indicative of similar defects in the underlying waterproofing system. At their
highest point above the intermediate supports, there is only 60mm of concrete cover to the
post-tensioning ducts. The PTSI 'Phase 3' site investigations determined the presence of voids in
several locations, particularly over supports. The tendons exposed were found to have only minor
surface corrosion.
Consequence - High. If water, chlorides and sulfates were to penetrate the deck waterproofing,
concrete and ducts, deterioration of the post-tensioning system would likely occur, which could result in
loss of capacity and eventually structural failure.
Hazard risk level - High.
Risk management measures:
1)
undertake further PTSI site investigations at tendon high points to determine the current condition
of the post-tensioning system;
2)
investigate the need to replace the waterproofing and resurface the deck to prevent any further
possible deterioration.
C2.1.2
Anchorages concealed at joints
Risk event - Water and salts penetrate the longitudinal end anchorages located below the expansion
joints resulting in corrosion of the post-tensioning system and reinforcement, and deterioration of
concrete.
Likelihood - Medium. The most recent general and principal inspections reported water staining and
dampness at the deck ends, suggesting that water has been penetrating the expansion joints. The
expansion joints over the end supports are 44 years old, exceeding their expected service life. No end
anchorage exposures were undertaken during the 1997 PTSI 'Phase 3' site investigation. 2 No. ducts
and tendons towards the deck ends were investigated in 1997 and confirmed to be in good condition
with no voids present and no significant corrosion at these locations. Concrete dust samples taken
from the deck adjacent to the deck ends indicated that 2 of the 12 concrete samples had high chloride
contents exceeding the accepted critical value of 0.3% by mass of cement.
Consequence - High. Deterioration of the post-tensioning end anchorages with possible loss of
capacity and eventually structural failure.

C2.2.1
Cracking in post-tensioned concrete elements
Risk event - As well as general structural deterioration, water and salts penetrate the cracks resulting in

Likelihood - Medium. The 1998 PTSI 'Phase 3' site investigation report concluded that the tendon ducts

eventually structural failure.

1)
undertake further PTSI site investigations to establish the condition of the tendons, grout and

2)
