Introduction
Introduction
Background
Good landscape design integrates new and existing roads into the landscape, minimises adverse
impact on setting, maximises design quality and strengthens sense of place for those on and off the
road.
Development of this document has been influenced by:
1)
the UK Government's commitment in ratification of the European Landscape Convention ELC
2000 [Ref 7.N] (hereafter referred to as the Convention), to recognising landscape matters in law,
and promoting landscape planning, protection, and management policies;
2)
the Convention's ELC 2000 [Ref 7.N] widely adopted definition of landscape which recognises:
a)
landscape as a resource inclusive of townscape;
b)
the relationship between people and place; and
c)
all landscapes as important irrespective of their location (i.e. seascapes, rural, urban, and
peri-urban areas) or condition (i.e. natural, outstanding or ordinary).
This document aligns with Directive 2011/92/EU as amended by 2014/52/EU EIA Directive 2014/52/EU
[Ref 1.N], as transposed into UK law.
The environmental function codes, landscape element codes, and environmental element codes for
masterplans are included within this document.
Assumptions made in the preparation of this document
The assumptions made in GG 101 [Ref 6.N] apply to this document.

Terms and definitions
Terms and definitions
Term
Definition
Character area
A geographical area that can be recognised as distinct from its adjacent
areas due to variations in components and activities, and the way in
which these are typically perceived.
NOTE 1: The alignment of a character area's boundary is determined
independently of administrative and property boundaries and may
overlap these where the landscape's character, characteristics, or
individual features, causes it to do so.
NOTE 2: Refer to GLVIA Third Edition [Ref 2.I] for more guidance on
understanding landscape character.
Design excellence
Encourages better team collaboration involving innovative and creative
solutions of design and landscape quality and value, focusing on the
needs of people and their communities, the goals of sustainability and
building pride of place while acknowledging the importance of both cost
and whole-life cost.
Design quality
Safe, functional and effective design, responding positively and
sensitively to landscape character, cultural heritage and communities,
while also conforming to the principles of sustainable development.
Design strategy
Establishes and implements specific actions, measures or requirements
to manage and influence the project's design life-cycle and deliver value
for money.
Environmental element
Features relevant to achieving non-landscape environmental objectives
in respect of auditory amenity, biodiversity, and water quality.
Environmental function
Identifies and states the purpose of different features and their physical
nature to design and manage a road network, i.e. why they are there
and the intention for achieving them.
NOTE: Used to attach environmental objectives to engineering, and
other built elements, to influence their design and/or operational
maintenance.
Good road design
Good road design is inclusive, resilient and sustainable that aims to put
people at its heart; appreciated for its usefulness, and elegance in its
design reflecting the beauty of the natural, built and historic environment
through which it passes, enhancing it where reasonably possible.
NOTE: Source of reference GG 103 [Ref 5.N].

Terms and definitions

Term
Definition
Landscape
'An area, as perceived by people, whose character is the result of the
action and interaction of natural and/or human factors.' ELC 2000 [Ref
7.N]
NOTE 1: About the relationship between people and place.
NOTE 2: Inclusive, covering natural, rural, urban, and peri-urban areas
and applies not only to special or designated landscapes or countryside
but to everyday or degraded landscapes.
NOTE 3: A resource that 'results from the way that different components
of our environment - natural and cultural - interact together and are
perceived. Source of definition GLVIA 3 GLVIA Third Edition [Ref 2.I].
Landscape design
An integral part of road design that entails collaboration with other
design disciplines to integrate a wide range of design components that
can include:
1) landform including geology and soils;
2) built structures;
3) roads, paths, steps, ramps, railings, etc (including accessibility
considerations);
4) vegetation and planting;
5) ecology - habitats and species;
6) drainage, such as sustainable urban drainage systems (SUDS);
7) water features, art and other installations (such as educational
installations);
8) furniture including roadside furniture and signage; and
9) lighting.
Landscape elements
Broad classification types of component parts of the landscape with
specific requirements or management needs to achieve their
longer-term objectives.
NOTE: These can be subdivided according to their detailed design or
management needs.
Landscape objectives
The context-specific criteria developed by the designer, alongside the
landscape strategy, against which the landscape design is tested to
determine its success in achieving design quality and good road design.
Landscape quality
The degree to which a place is cohesive and intact in composition and
design, free of clutter, in good physical condition, and delights the
senses (through scenic beauty, for example).

Term
Definition
Landscape strategy
A holistic design approach that applies the principles of good road
design, alongside other appropriate guidance and best practice, to the
specific context of the design task, incorporating the measures required
to meet the needs and maximise the benefits of the design, and
arranging those measures into a cohesive, site-specific set of landscape
design solutions.
NOTE: Also referred to as the 'design vision'.
Masterplan
A plan illustrating an overall development concept - vision - of all
aspects for the whole site or area establishing functional,
interrelationship proposals between all parts of the site/area which then
guides the detailed design.
Mitigation strategy
A strategy to prepare for and lessen the effects of an impact to reduce
adverse, and potentially long-term, effects.
Sense of place
The degree to which a character area (or a specific location within a
character area) provides a distinct, recognisable and memorable
experience.
NOTE: Refer to GLVIA Third Edition [Ref 2.I] for more guidance on
understanding sense of place.
Special landscapes
(special landscape areas)
A non-statutory local landscape designation used by Local Planning
Authorities to define areas of high landscape importance.
NOTE: Also referred to as 'local landscape areas'.
Tree girth
Measurement taken around the trunk usually at 1.5 m from the ground.

1. Scope
1.
Scope
Aspects covered
1.1
This document shall be used to specify the requirements for landscape design, for the construction,
improvement, operation, and maintenance of all UK motorway and all-purpose trunk roads.
1.2
Construction, improvement, operation and maintenance of roads shall be in line with the design's
landscape strategy and/or defined set of landscape objectives, incorporating excellence and quality in
good design, value for money and whole-life cost throughout the design lifecycle, as identified within
GG 103 [Ref 5.N] 'Introduction and general requirements for sustainable development and design'.
NOTE
Good road design is a matter of design and landscape quality, maximising aesthetic and environmental
benefits, and respecting the special character and sense of place of each individual location.
1.3
This document shall be used to identify the appropriate codes for masterplans to illustrate
environmental mitigation and enhancement measures.
NOTE
The requirements of the Overseeing Organisation apply. Refer to the National Application Annex where
applicable.
Implementation
1.4
This document shall be implemented forthwith on all schemes involving design on the Overseeing
Organisation's motorway and all-purpose trunk roads according to the implementation requirements of
GG 101 [Ref 6.N].
1.4.1
The requirements set out in this document should be applied in the preparation of all schemes for the
construction and improvement of UK motorways and all-purpose trunk road projects.
Use of GG 101
1.5
The requirements contained in GG 101 [Ref 6.N] shall be followed in respect of activities covered by
this document.

2. Principles and purpose
2.
Principles and purpose
Approach to design
2.1
The principles of good road design established within GG 103 [Ref 5.N] shall be applied and
complement the general principles of design excellence, such that the overall design:
1)
maximises benefits above and beyond a compliance with minimum requirements;
2)
is context-specific in its response to landscape;
3)
incorporates landscape design and whole-life cost; and
4)
encompasses landscape design components, including biodiversity, heritage, acoustics, water
management, and public access requirements, .
2.2
Maintaining and, where possible, enhancing landscape quality shall form part of all good road design
and be measured by how well the design serves those who use it, those who live, work and enjoy the
land alongside it, and the landscape through which it passes.
NOTE
Landscape quality is fundamental to the design process throughout the design life-cycle and plays an
important role in the overall cost and whole-life cost of a project.
2.3
Good road design shall create opportunities to conserve and enhance the landscape and is measured
by the design's response(s) to:
1)
reflect and respect people's needs;
2)
provide or incorporate a sense of place (place-making);
3)
be robust, buildable, functional, operational, and maintainable;
4)
manage and minimise landscape disturbances and the damage from both temporary and
permanent works;
5)
climate change and its challenges;
6)
be flexible, sustainable (including and minimising waste) - whole-life cost; and
7)
deliver environmental gains.
2.4
A landscape strategy and a set of landscape objectives shall be developed to inform any landscape
design.
2.4.1
The landscape strategy and landscape objectives should:
1)
inform and be informed by the wider design strategy;
2)
be delivered by the design solutions presented in the environmental / landscape masterplan; and
3)
encourage design excellence and design quality that is sensitive to and integrates the road into
the local context.
NOTE 1
The requirements of the Overseeing Organisation apply. Refer to the National Application Annex where
applicable.
NOTE 2
The development of a landscape strategy involves the collation of opportunities, constraints, and risks
related to the landscape design and the design solutions necessary to meet need and maximise
benefits, and explains, spatially, how these fit together.
NOTE 3
Landscape objectives enforce the landscape strategy (and are derived from it) by stating what needs to
be achieved, and where.
2.5
The landscape strategy and landscape objectives shall lead to designs that:
1)
maintain consistency with the adjacent and surrounding landscape as necessary to avoid a
reduction in landscape quality;
2)
raise landscape quality and strengthen sense of place where proportionate opportunities exist to
do so; and

3. Design objectives
3.
Design objectives
Landscape strategy
3.1
A landscape strategy is a holistic design approach that shall outline the principles of landscape design.
3.2
The landscape design shall align with GG 103 [Ref 5.N] and maintain or, where the proportionate
opportunity exists, enhance landscape quality by demonstrating:
1)
protection and enhancement of the local environment;
2)
sensitivity to the local context and its numerous, and sometimes complex combinations of
landscape elements such as fields, woodlands, water bodies, hedges (and other boundaries),
pathways, streets, buildings and settlements;
3)
the creation of new views and the protection of existing views along the road to sustain visual
interest and maximise sense of place;
4)
the integration of footpaths, bridleways and side roads into the landscape to minimise severance;
5)
the integration of roadside barriers, fences and walls with their surroundings;
6)
high quality structure designs that respect the local landscape character;
7)
measures to retain and enhance tranquillity and/or wildness;
8)
sensitivity to 'dark skies' areas, minimising adverse environmental impacts and the intrusion
caused by lighting;
9)
the reflection and integration of the surrounding pattern and species grouping in any new planting;
10)
the reflection and integration of enhancement opportunities to biodiversity;
11)
the safeguarding of individual trees/woodland, and other ecological interests; and
12)
place-making and the protection and enhancement of the surrounding historic and/or urban
environment.
NOTE
For the design of highways structures reference is made to CD 351 [Ref 3.I] The design and
appearance of highway structures.
3.3
Where the design is required to make choices regarding the road alignment, good road design shall be
applied to enhance integration with the surrounding landscape and environment and to avoid or
minimise:
1)
intrusion into undisturbed, high-quality landscapes;
2)
intrusion into views from nearby property and public places;
3)
intrusive embankments crossing valleys and low-lying land;
4)
cuttings that create notches on the skyline or scars on hillsides and sidelong ground;
5)
junctions that are, by siting or design, unsympathetic to their landscape setting;
6)
land take required for large landscape earthworks affecting heritage and nature conservation
sites;
7)
changes to drainage regimes; and
8)
unsympathetic and intrusive side road crossings.
3.4
Good road design shall integrate a road into the surrounding landscape, through its response to the
landscape character context, to avoid harm to its setting.
3.4.1
Designs should be aligned with the existing landform to minimise earthwork requirements and help
achieve a cut-and-fill balance.
3.4.2
Designs should provide a balance between concealing the road and allowing for distinctive views out,
highlighting important features (such as landmark structures or striking natural features).
3.4.3
The interrelationship of landscape design with road-corridor elements forms an important part of
landscape quality in good road design and should create opportunities for providing or improving upon
the sense of place, for those on the road and in the wider landscape.

3. Design objectives
3.4.4
The coordination of road-corridor elements in good road design and their alignment with the
surrounding landscape characteristics should integrate a road with its surroundings and minimise its
adverse impact on setting.
3.4.5
The landscape strategy should be used to reduce the linearity of the road, to reduce severance of the
receiving landscape pattern and mitigate the adverse impacts upon landscape character that occur as
a result.

Appendix A. Approach to landscape design - new roads
A1
Scope
This appendix provides guidance on the approach to landscape design for new roads incorporating
three key aspects - connecting people, connecting places and connecting processes. It also provides
guidance on the design approach to such aspects as landform, alignment, planting, the road corridor,
rural landscapes, and aesthetic quality. Supplementary guidance can be available for specific
landscape areas or aspects of landscape treatment but will require the prior agreement of the
Overseeing Organisation for use.
A2
Options development
The design of new roads involves the development of options/alternatives and an assessment of the
effects each would have on the broader landscape, its character and setting as the design evolves.
Assessing and understanding these effects is fundamental to the design life-cycle in influencing the
decision making and arriving at the final design solution, but does involve time. For this reason, the
early involvement of a suitably qualified landscape architect during the options phase is important to
understand these effects, so that the best options are developed and the design choices are fully
informed.
Achieving a good design requires commitment from all parties (including the client, design team,
contractor, statutory environmental bodies, and local communities) during the design life-cycle along
with a clear understanding of aesthetic principles (i.e. line, shape, sense of place, texture, etc.) and
sensitivity of the environment (susceptibility and value), as well as the functional and technical
considerations. An appreciation of conflicting project requirements requires collaborative working in
achieving a consensus to an aesthetic and sensitive solution' designing in a way that aligns with the
broader aspirations of the local communities and stakeholders.
Many factors influence road design with the landscape and its setting playing an important part in how
the road is perceived and sits within the context of the local landscape character. A clear understanding
(through a landscape appraisal) of the aesthetic qualities, the landscape and its character, and the
surrounding environment/setting, together with an appreciation of the potentially conflicting functional
and technical requirements of the proposals, is required to develop a landscape strategy and/or defined
set of project specific landscape objectives. These play an important role in design development.
A landscape strategy and/or defined set of project-specific landscape objectives forms the basis of the
design solutions presented in an environmental/landscape masterplan, identifying the functions and
elements required to deliver the project's proposed environmental/landscape design. To ensure design
quality throughout the design life-cycle, the masterplan informs the development of the detailed design,
which in turn informs the construction and operational phases of the overall design and ultimately
delivers the landscape strategy and/or objectives.
A3
Alignment and landform
The consideration and development of alignment design(s) should include the locations of main roads,
side roads, rights of way, junctions, structures, access roads, sustainable drainage systems and any
associated major utilities works. Alignment plays an important part in minimising the effects of road
design on landform and, in turn, the landscape. As such, the application of good road design at the
alignment stage can reduce the extent of the works, its cost, and the difficulty in avoiding or mitigating
adverse environmental effects. An alignment that utilises the existing landform and reflects natural
slopes can minimise a road's impact on setting whilst providing the road with a strong sense of place.
A4
The road corridor
Redundant roads, overbridges, side road crossings, junctions, signs, footpaths, bridleways, side roads,
lighting, fences and retaining structures that form part of a road design need to respect and reflect the
local landscape character and its setting. Integration into the landscape is important, with nature
recovery and landscape enhancement possible through careful design, the right landform and planting,

It is important for the design to conserve and enhance the cultural and historic character and setting of

and attractive to use. While the geometry of a route is important to meet the needs of the user, it is not
the only quality required to demonstrate good design. A useful route is one that connects people to
places they want to go. This may be as a route to places of work, education, employment, and so on,
or it may be connecting communities to their surrounding landscapes and landscape features (such as
scenic viewpoints). It could certainly be both at the same time. WCH routes may also be part of, or
provide in whole, circular recreational routes - these are a valuable resource to communities and their
health and well-being. There are a wide range of qualities that make WCH routes attractive to use, and
these include the difficulty of the terrain and their perceived safety. Good road design provides WCH
routes that allow the user to interact with the landscape - to explore its various sensory features (a good
route allows a user to explore with all their senses, not just visual). Variation along a route is important
to allow the user to structure their journey, and to provide interest. The alignment of the route can be
designed to reflect the local landscape pattern, the topography, and to give the user a sense of
exploring space. Planting and habitat design can provide the user with opportunities to interact with
nature and reinforce sense of place, and the opening and closing of views can be used to help them
appreciate the varying scale of the landscape through which they pass.

Appendix B. Approach to landscape design - improving existing roads
B1
Scope
This appendix provides guidance on the approach to landscape design for improving roads within
existing highway land or within limited land take. Works of this nature are often limited in scope, which
can result in a number of constraints, but can also provide opportunities for enhancement to the
aesthetic quality of the existing road. The guidance provided relates to such aspects as landform,
alignment, planting, the road corridor, and aesthetic quality.
B2
Options development
As with new roads many factors can influence the design for improving existing roads with the
landscape and its setting playing an important part in how the improvement is perceived and sits within
the context of the local landscape character. While it can prove more difficult to promote improvements
to aesthetic qualities of existing roads, opportunities to enhance the views and setting should still be
sought, providing interest for the users of the road and well as the visual receptors in the wider
landscape. An understanding of the landscape, its character and the surrounding environment/setting,
together with an appreciation of conflicting functional and technical considerations, is required. This in
turn influences a landscape strategy and/or project specific landscape objectives that plays an
important role in design development and towards improving the aesthetic qualities.
The same process of developing options/alternatives is used but can be limited in scope due to the
project's location and existing layout. As with designing for new roads, an assessment of the effects
each would have on the broader landscape, its character and setting, as the design evolves, is
required. Understanding these effects is fundamental to the design life-cycle in influencing the final
design solution, but does involve time. For this reason, the early involvement of a suitably qualified
landscape architect during the options phase is important to understand these effects, so that the best
options are developed and the design choices are fully informed.
Achieving a good design requires commitment from all parties (including the client, design team,
contractor, statutory environmental bodies, and local communities) during the design life-cycle along
with a clear understanding of aesthetic principles (that is, line, shape, sense of place, texture, etc.) and
sensitivity of the environment (susceptibility and value), as well as the functional and technical
considerations. An appreciation of conflicting project requirements requires collaborative working in
achieving a consensus to an aesthetic and sensitive solution; designing in a way that aligns with the
broader aspirations of the local communities and stakeholders.
Design tasks that are notably limited in scope may not warrant an environmental/landscape masterplan,
but a landscape strategy and/or set of project specific landscape objectives can suffice to inform and
deliver the landscape design and reduce the impact of the road on people and the environment.
B3
Improvement techniques
An integrated approach should:
1)
minimise conflict by respecting and integrating the road into the local context;
2)
minimise adverse effects to the natural, built, and historic environment;
3)
provide enhancement opportunities for biodiversity, landscape and townscape; and
4)
provide interest for both the road user and those in the wider landscape, such as residents,
visitors to the landscape and its features, users of local roads, and walkers, cyclists, and
horse-riders (WCH).
Design solutions can, therefore, need to consider new techniques. These can include appropriate
planting designs, use of materials, contrasting forms, colour, texture and introducing variety and
opening up views to create visual interest and reduce the impact caused by limited land take. A
balance between the proportion of hard and soft landscape elements is important but this is often
determined by location and local context.

S/5. Seeding of verges, visibility splays and cent...
S/5.
Seeding of verges, visibility splays and central reserves
Seed mixes for verges, visibility splays and central reserves
S/5.1
Where verges, visibility splays and widened central reserves feature soft landscape, these shall be
seeded with an appropriate seed mix.
S/5.1.1
Seed mixes used on verges, visibility splays and central reserves should be low-growing,
low-maintenance, native, and species-rich, to promote biodiversity, reduce maintenance and create a
visually attractive roadside environment.
Soil requirements for species-rich grasslands
S/5.2
The substrate for seeding of species-rich grassland shall be subsoil, bare-substrate, or shallow
low-fertility topsoil as defined in BS 3882 [Ref 5.N].
S/5.3
Topsoil, subsoil and bare substrate shall be tested for nutrient levels and suitability for supporting the
proposed seeding mix and if necessary modified to encourage wildflower growth and minimise
competition from undesirable weed species and coarse grasses.
S/5.3.1
Not using topsoil in the specification of new species-rich grassland has the potential to result in a topsoil
surplus, which should be addressed early in the design process, considering the different topsoil depths
required for landscape and environmental design elements and the area of land required for the project.
NOTE
Topsoil is a vital reservoir for biodiversity containing a highly diverse range of organisms including
bacteria, fungi, microflora and microfauna which interact and contribute to the carbon and nitrogen
cycles. There is a need to preserve and maintain topsoil in good condition to safeguard biodiversity and
store carbon to help combat climate change ( Dobbie et al 2011 [Ref 3.I]).
S/5.4
Topsoil shall not be buried or spread to excessive depths that lead to degradation of the soil biome or
result in development of anaerobic conditions.
NOTE
Topsoil does not normally perform well below a depth of 400 mm from the surface, where there is an
increase in self-compaction and where the biochemical oxygen demand often exceeds the rate of
aeration. This often results in the development of anaerobic conditions that are detrimental to plant root
functions ( Soils CoP [Ref 1.I]).
S/5.4.1
The removal of topsoil from site should only be considered as a last resort and be avoided unless it can
be demonstrated that it would have a beneficial environmental effect.

Appendix S/A. Approach to landscape design and environmental
mitigation measures [LD 117, Appendix A]
S/A1
Scope of Appendix
This appendix provides guidance on the approach to preparation of drawings at the various stages of
design development from EIA to completion of construction.
S/A2
Design stage drawings
Landscape design and environmental mitigation drawings are typically prepared for each of the
following design stages:
1) Landscape and Visual Impact Assessment
Landscape and environmental mitigation drawings to set out the proposals to mitigate landscape and
environmental effects and provide environmental enhancements. Generic planting and seeding mix
types including the habitat type they are intended to create are included as a minimum. The landscape
and environmental mitigation drawings prepared at this stage are in place of the 'masterplan' referred to
in Appendices A and B of LD 117 [Ref 4.N].
2) Contract
Indicative landscape and environmental design contract drawings to accommodate post EIA
developments to the road design, typically including greater detail than the landscape and
environmental mitigation drawings. Indicative planting and seeding species mixes are typically
included.
3) Tender
Drawings submitted by tenderers containing a similar level of information and detail to the indicative
landscape and environmental design contract drawings but modified to align with the conceptual
engineering design proposals including any innovations or value engineering changes proposed by the
tenderer.
4) Detail design
Drawings including full details required for construction.
5) Completion
As-built drawings to be submitted to the Overseeing Organisation in the relevant geographical
information system.
